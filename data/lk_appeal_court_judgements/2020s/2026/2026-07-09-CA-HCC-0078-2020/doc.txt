Judgment

1. In this appeal, the 2nd Accused-Appellant (hereinafter referred to as “the Appellant”)

challenges the conviction and sentence imposed by the Learned High Court Judge of

Negombo on 08.07.2020. In High Court Case No. HC-16/2005, the two accused were

indicted on the following counts:

a. That on or about 08.06.2012 in Weligampitiya, the 1st and 2nd accused were for

possession of 5.496 kg of Heroin without the authority or a license as permitted

by the Director in terms of Chapter 5 of the Poisons, Opium and Dangerous

Drugs Ordinance, an offence punishable under Section 54A (d) of the said

Ordinance.

b. At the same time, during the same transaction, the 1st and 2nd accused, by

trafficking 5.496 kg of Heroin, committed an offence punishable under Section

54A (b) of the Poisons, Opium and Dangerous Drugs Ordinance (as amended).

2. The 1st Accused remained absent throughout the trial, and accordingly, the trial against

him was conducted in absentia. At the conclusion of the trial, the Learned High Court

Judge found both accused guilty on the charges contained in the indictment and

convicted them accordingly. Consequently, both accused were sentenced to death on

each count.

3. It is against the said conviction and sentence that the 2nd Accused-Appellant has

preferred the present appeal.

4. The following are the grounds of appeal urged by the Appellant:

a. Adoption of previous proceedings is irregular;

b. Grave injustice has been caused to the Appellant due to improper evaluation of

evidence by the Learned Trial Judge;

c. There was no evidence of exclusive possession;

d. There was no evidence to prove trafficking; and

e. There was no evidence to treat the Appellant as an accomplice.

5. At the trial, three police officers namely PW1, PW2, PW8, and PW10 testified for the

prosecution.

6. I shall first consider whether the adoption of the proceedings by the Learned Trial Judge

was irregular or erroneous. The Appellant contends that, although the Learned Trial

Judge proceeded on the basis of adopting the proceedings recorded before his

predecessor in office, there is nothing on record to demonstrate that the parties had

consented to such adoption.

7. It is common ground that the question of adoption of proceedings is mainly governed

by Section 48 of the Judicature Act, No. 2 of 1978, as amended by the Judicature

(Amendment) Act, No. 27 of 1999. Section 48 reads:

“In the case of death, sickness, resignation, removal from office, absence from Sri

Lanka, or other disability of any Judge before whom any action, prosecution,

proceeding or matter, whether on any inquiry preliminary to committal for trial or

4 | P a g e

otherwise, has been instituted or is pending, such action, prosecution, proceeding

or matter may be continued before the successor of such Judge who shall have

power to act on the evidence already recorded by his predecessor, or partly

recorded by his predecessor and partly recorded by him or, if he thinks fit, to re-

summon the witness and commence the proceedings afresh:

Provided that where any criminal prosecution, proceeding or matter (except on an

inquiry preliminary to committal for trial) is continued before the successor of any

such judge, the accused may demand that the witnesses be resummoned and

reheard.”

8. It must be emphasized that Section 48 does not require a successor Judge to formally

record the adoption of the proceedings previously conducted by his predecessor.

Accordingly, the omission to make such a formal record does not render the

proceedings irregular, nor does it vitiate the judgment, unless it is shown that the

omission has occasioned prejudice to the substantial rights of a party or resulted in a

failure of justice.

9. It is to be noted that the trial in the High Court had commenced before the High Court

Judge N.V. Karunathilake on 06.05.2015, where the evidence-in-chief of PW1 was

partly concluded. The remainder of the trial was thereafter proceeded with by the High

Court Judge R.A. Ranaraja. On 29.01.2018, at the commencement of the proceedings,

the Learned Trial Judge stated that, having adopted the evidence led thus far, he would

continue with the trial.

“මේ දක්වා මෙමෙයවා ඇති සාක්ි ො ඉදිරිමේ පිළිමෙන නඩු විභාෙය

පවත්වා මෙන යාෙට ො තීරණය කරමි.” (vide page at 249 of the Appeal Brief)

10. It is true that there is nothing on record to demonstrate that the parties consented to such

adoption. Similarly, there is nothing to indicate that either party raised any objection

when the proceedings were so adopted. The Appellant was, throughout the trial, duly

represented by an Attorney-at-Law. In those circumstances, it is difficult to infer that

the Appellant had any objection to the adoption of the evidence led before the

predecessor Judge.

11. Further, had the Appellant been aggrieved by the procedure followed by the Trial Judge

in adopting evidence, he could have raised an objection at the appropriate stage or made

an application to recall any witness already examined. The absence of any such

objection or procedural step is a relevant factor in assessing the Appellant’s present

contention. More significantly, the Appellant has failed to establish that the Learned

Trial Judge's adoption of the evidence recorded before his predecessor, without

expressly obtaining the Appellant's consent, resulted in any prejudice to the Appellant's

substantial rights or occasioned a failure of justice. In the absence of such prejudice,

the alleged irregularity, even if established, cannot constitute a valid ground for setting

aside the proceedings or the judgment.

12. At this juncture, it is apposite to refer to Article 138 of the Constitution, which defines

the scope and extent of the jurisdiction vested in the Court of Appeal in the exercise of

its appellate powers.

13. Article 138(1) reads as follows:

“The Court of Appeal shall have and exercise subject to the provisions of the

Constitution or of any law, an appellate jurisdiction for the correction of all errors

in fact or in law which shall be committed by the High Court, in the exercise of its

appellate or original jurisdiction or by any Court of First Instance, tribunal or other

institution and sole and exclusive cognizance, by way of appeal, revision and

restitutio in integrum, of all causes, suits, actions, prosecutions, matters and things

of which such High Court, Court of First Instance, tribunal or other institution may

have taken cognizance: Provided that no judgement, decree or order of any court

shall be reversed or varied on account of any error, defect or irregularity, which

has not prejudiced the substantial rights of the parties or occasioned a failure of

justice.”

14. The language of the proviso to Article 138(1) of the Constitution is clear and

unambiguous. It mandates that the Court of Appeal shall not reverse or vary the

judgments, decrees, or orders of the original courts on account of any error, defect, or

irregularity, unless such error, defect, or irregularity has prejudiced the substantial rights

of the parties or occasioned a failure of justice.

15. In this regard, Sections 436 and 456A of the Code of Criminal Procedure Act would

also be of much relevance. Section 436 reads:

“Subject to the provisions hereinbefore contained any judgment passed by a court of

competent jurisdiction shall not be reversed or altered on appeal or revision on

account –

(a) of any error, omission, or irregularity in the complaint, summons, warrant,

charge, judgment, summing up, or other proceedings before or during trial or in any

inquiry or other proceedings under this Code; or

(b) of the want of any sanction required by section 135, unless such error, omission,

irregularity, or want has occasioned a failure of justice.”

16. Section 456A of the Code of Criminal Procedure Act reads as follows:

“The failure to comply with any provision of this Code shall not affect or be deemed

to have affected the validity of any complaint, committal or indictment or the

admissibility of any evidence unless such failure has occasioned a substantial

miscarriage of justice.”

17. The following authorities illustrate the consistent approach of our appellate courts in

dealing with cases originating in the trial courts where procedural errors or irregularities

are alleged or apparent.

18. In Kiri Mahaththaya and Another v. Attorney General [2020] 1 Sri LR 10, the

Supreme Court was called upon to decide whether non-compliance with Sction 196 of

the Code of Criminal Procedure Act, which mandates the High Court to read and

explain the indictment to the accused and ask whether he pleads guilty or not guilty of

the offence charged (arraignment of accused), vitiates the conviction. The Supreme

Court firmly held that if the conviction is to be vitiated, the Appellant must satisfy the

Court that such non-compliance has caused prejudice to the substantial rights of the

accused or has occasioned a failure of justice, as stipulated in the proviso to Article

138(1) of the Constitution.

CA/HCC/0078/2020

7 | P a g e

19. In Vilma Dissanayake v. Leslie Dharmaratne [2008] 2 Sri LR 184 at 185, Justice

Sarath N. Silva C.J. emphasized that the re-summoning of witnesses and the

recommencement of proceedings de novo are exceptional measures and not the ordinary

course to be adopted. The general rule is that proceedings may be continued by a

successor Judge unless circumstances exist which render such continuation prejudicial

to the rights of a party or likely to occasion a failure of justice.

“It is necessary for a succeeding Judge to continue proceedings since there are

changes of Judges holding office in a particular Court due to transfers, promotions

and the like. It is in these circumstances that Section 48 was amended giving a

discretion to a Judge to continue with the proceedings. Hence the exercise of such

discretion should not be disturbed unless there are serious issues with regard to the

demeanour of any witness recorded by the Judge who previously heard the case.”

20. In Krishnakumar v. Attorney General [2021] 2 Sri LR 454 at 458, Abayakoon J.

stated:

“I find that the plain reading of section 48 of the Judicature Act, as amended by Act

No. 27 of 1999 is very much clearer as to the intention of the legislature. The

intention has been to provide for the conclusion, as expeditiously as possible, a trial

commenced before another judge without causing prejudice to an accused person.

The legislature in its wisdom has provided for the trial judge to continue with the

trial by acting on the evidence previously recorded by his predecessor, but by the

proviso of the section has provided an opportunity for an accused in a criminal

prosecution to demand that the witness may be re-summoned and reheard ensuring

the right of an accused for a fair trial.”

21. In Sunil Jayarathna v. Attorney-General [2011] 2 Sri. L.R. 92, it was held that when

considering the proviso to Article 138(1) of the Constitution, it is evident that the

judgment of the High Court need not be reversed or interfered with on account of any

defect, error or irregularity which has not prejudiced the substantial rights of the parties

or occasioned a failure of justice.

22. Accordingly, the prevailing legal position emerging from the aforementioned case law

and statutory provisions is that a judgment cannot be set aside merely on account of a

procedural irregularity. The accused must further establish that the irregularity

complained of has resulted in prejudice to his substantial rights or has occasioned a

failure of justice. In the absence of such prejudice, appellate intervention is not

warranted.

23. For the foregoing reasons, I find no merit in the first ground of appeal advanced by the

Appellant.

24. I shall now turn to the remaining grounds, namely, whether there was sufficient

evidence to establish the Appellant's exclusive possession of the contraband and

whether the prosecution proved the offence of trafficking beyond reasonable doubt.

25. Evidently, the raid was conducted pursuant to information received by PW1 from one

of his confidential informants. According to PW1, the informant had informed him that

two persons were arriving in possession of narcotic drugs and requested him to proceed

to St. Anne’s Hotel on Weligampitiya–Negombo Road.

26. The evidence further reveals that, at the time of the arrest, the Appellant had nothing in

his possession. It was only the 1st accused who was carrying a bag, from which PW1

claimed to have recovered the heroin. Accordingly, the alleged recovery of the

contraband was effected exclusively from the bag carried by the 1st accused and not

from the possession of the Appellant.

27. In the indictment, both the 1st and 2nd accused were charged with the joint possession

and trafficking of heroin. Whether possession is joint in nature must necessarily be

determined in light of the particular facts and circumstances of each case.

28. In the present case, the only evidence relied upon by the prosecution to connect the

Appellant to the possession and trafficking of heroin is that he was in the company of

the 1st accused at the time of the arrest. However, the evidence further reveals that,

although the Appellant was subjected to a body search by the police officers, no heroin

or any other incriminating article was found in his possession. Thus, apart from his

mere presence with the 1st accused, the prosecution has failed to adduce any direct

evidence establishing that the Appellant exercised possession, control, or dominion

over the heroin allegedly recovered from the bag carried by the 1st accused.

29. In a case of this nature, the Court must be satisfied that the accused had conscious and

exclusive possession of the dangerous drug in question before a conviction can be

sustained. Mere possession, without proof of knowledge and control, is insufficient to

establish criminal liability, particularly in respect of a charge of trafficking.

30. As emerged during cross-examination, the Appellant's residence was situated in close

proximity to the place of arrest. It is noteworthy that, despite arresting the Appellant on

allegations of possession and trafficking of heroin, the police took no steps to search

his residence. Had the Appellant in fact been engaged in trafficking narcotic drugs, a

search of his residence may reasonably have been expected to yield material relevant

to the investigation. The failure to conduct such a search is a circumstance that assumes

significance when assessing the strength of the prosecution case against the Appellant.

31. In the instant case, the 1st and 2nd accused were charged with the joint possession and

trafficking of heroin and were convicted primarily on the basis that both were present

at the location at the time of the raid. However, the issue of paramount importance is

whether the Appellant possessed the requisite knowledge that the parcel concealed by

the 1st accused contained heroin.

32. Knowledge is an essential ingredient in establishing possession, whether actual,

constructive, or joint. Therefore, before the Appellant can be held criminally liable for

the possession or trafficking of heroin, the prosecution must establish beyond

reasonable doubt that he was aware of the nature of the substance contained in the parcel

and exercised some degree of control or dominion over it. Mere presence in the

company of the 1st accused, without proof of such knowledge, is insufficient to sustain

a conviction for joint possession or trafficking. It is a well-established principle that

possession, in the context of narcotic offences, must be conscious as well as exclusive.

33. The word 'conscious' means awareness of a particular fact. It is a state of mind that is

deliberate or intended. In the present case, no evidence was adduced to establish that

the Appellant was in actual possession of the contraband at the time of his arrest. Nor

was there any material placed before the Court to demonstrate the Appellant’s state of

mind so as to suggest any knowledge of, or nexus with, the 1st accused’s possession of

heroin.

34. Accordingly, in the absence of proof of conscious possession or any circumstance

indicative of the Appellant’s control, participation, or concurrence, the essential link

required to sustain the charge against the Appellant is not established.

35. Furthermore, in the absence of direct evidence demonstrating that the Appellant was in

exclusive possession of the narcotic substance, possession, if any, must necessarily be

established by circumstantial evidence.

36. Mere association with, or proximity to, a person found in possession of a narcotic

substance is insufficient, by itself, to establish joint possession. The prosecution must

prove, beyond a reasonable doubt, circumstances from which the Court can reasonably

infer that the Appellant knowingly participated in the possession of the narcotic drug.

37. In the present case, the only evidence against the Appellant is that he was seen in the

company of the 1st accused at the time of the arrest, and that the alleged heroin was

recovered from a bag carried by the 1st accused. No evidence was adduced to establish

that the Appellant exercised any control or dominion over the bag or its contents, or that

he had knowledge that the 1st accused was in possession of heroin. In the absence of

such evidence, the Appellant's mere presence in the company of the 1st accused is

insufficient to establish either joint possession of, or criminal liability in respect of, the

narcotic substance.

38. In Brock v. State, 162 Tex.Crim.R. 339, 285 S.W.2d 745 (1956), the Texas Court of

Criminal Appeals established a foundational standard for establishing constructive

possession of contraband. The court ruled that when contraband (such as illicit drugs)

is found in a location where the accused is not in exclusive possession, the evidence

must show additional "affirmative links" to connect the accused to the contraband. A

conviction based solely on circumstantial evidence cannot be sustained if the proof only

amounts to a strong suspicion or mere probability, and it must exclude every other

reasonable hypothesis except guilt.

39. In Glenn v. United States, 271 F.2d 880 (1959), the Sixth Circuit wrote:

"The mere presence of the accused at the scene of a crime does not, of itself, justify

drawing an inference that he participated therein."

40. Lord Wilberforce in Warner v. Metropolitan Police Commissioner (1968) 52 Criminal

Appeal Report 373, has considered this matter as follows;

“The question, to which an answer is required, and in the end, a jury must answer it,

is whether in the circumstances the Accused should be held to have possession of the

substance, rather than mere control. In order to decide between these two, the jury

should, in my opinion, be invited to consider all the circumstances- to use again the

words of Pollock and Wright, possession in the common law, P. 119- the ‘modes or

events’ by which the custody commences and the legal incident in which it is held.

By these I mean, relating them to typical situations, that they must consider the

manner and circumstances in which the substance, or something which contains it,

had been received, what knowledge or means of knowledge or guilty knowledge as

to the presence of the substance, or as to the nature of what has been received, the

Accused had at the time of receipt or thereafter up to the moment when he is found

with it; his legal relation to the substance or package (including his right of access

to it). On such matters as these (not exhaustively stated) they must make the decision

whether, in addition to physical control, he has, or ought to have imputed to him the

intention to possess, or knowledge that he does possess, what is, in fact, a prohibited

substance. If he has this intention or knowledge, it is not additionally necessary that

he should know the nature of the substances.”

41. This proposition was considered by His Lordship Ismail, J in his Judgment on K.

Sumanawathi Perera v. Attorney General 1998 2 SLR 20, held inter alia that;

“Whether in the circumstances the accused should be held to have possession of the

substance, rather than mere control Court should consider all the circumstances -

the modes or events by which the custody commences and the legal incident in which

it is held.”

42. Recently, His Lordship Aluwihare PC J in the case of Mohamed Iqbal Mohamed

Sadath v. Attorney General, SC Appeal 110/15, (SC Minutes 14.12.2020), held:

“Lord Wilberforce went on to state that, on such matters as above, though not

exhaustively stated, it must be decided whether, in addition to physical control, he

has or ought to have imputed to him, the intention to possess or knowledge that he

does possess, what is in fact a prohibited substance. The above reasoning in my view

is a rational guideline that should be adopted in deciding as to whether the Accused

had the knowledge (the requisite mens rea) that what he possessed is a prohibited

substance, even though he may not have known the precise nature of the substance.”

43. In R. v. Bland, Criminal LR 1988 41 at page 42, it was held that:

“…allowing the appeal and quashing the conviction, there was no evidence of

assistance, active or passive; the fact that the Appellant and Ratcliff lived together

in the same room was not sufficient evidence from which the jury could draw such

an inference. There was sufficient evidence from which the jury could have inferred

knowledge on the Appellant's part that Ratcliff was drug-dealing, but no more.

Assistance, though passive, required more than mere knowledge; for example, it

required evidence of encouragement at least, or of some element of control, which

was entirely lacking in the case. The case should have been withdrawn from the

jury.”

44. At the conclusion of the prosecution case, the Appellant chose to make a dock

statement. In his statement, the Appellant stated that, while he was on his way home,

the 1st accused had inquired from him about the whereabouts of a particular place, and

that it was at that moment that both of them were arrested by the police.

45. The prosecution also led evidence in rebuttal to establish that the Appellant was

acquainted with the 1st accused, on the basis that both had previously been prosecuted

for the possession of heroin. However, it emerged from the evidence that the Appellant

had been acquitted of that charge.

46. The Learned Trial Judge applied the Lucas principle against the Appellant and

concluded that the rebuttal evidence established that the Appellant had made a false

statement in his dock statement. On that basis, the Learned Trial Judge held that the

Appellant's falsehood constituted an additional circumstance strengthening the

prosecution case in accordance with the Lucas principle.

47. In my view, however, such an approach is open to serious doubt. The mere fact that the

Appellant was previously prosecuted together with the 1st accused does not necessarily

establish that they were acquainted at the time of the present incident, particularly when

the Appellant had been acquitted of the earlier charge. Moreover, before the Lucas

principle can be invoked, the Court must be satisfied that the impugned statement was

a deliberate falsehood told out of a consciousness of guilt. A finding of falsity, without

more, does not justify the application of the Lucas principle, nor can it be treated as

substantive evidence of the Appellant's guilt.

48. As Lord Lane CJ held in R. v. Lucas (1981) 2 All ER 1008 at 1011:

“Statements made out of court, for example statements to the police, which are proved

or admitted to be false may in certain circumstances amount to corroboration…To

be capable of amounting to corroboration the lie told out of court must first of all be

deliberate. Secondly it must relate to a material issue. Thirdly the motive for the lie

must be a realisation of guilt and a fear of the truth…Fourthly the statement must be

clearly shown to be a lie by evidence other than that of the accomplice who is to be

corroborated, that is to say by admission or by evidence from an independent

witness. As a matter of good sense, it is difficult to see why, subject to the same

safeguard, lies proved to have been told in court by a defendant should not equally

be capable of providing corroboration.”

49. Hence, in the factual context of the present case, the Learned Trial Judge's reliance on

the Lucas principle is wholly unjustified and cannot be sustained. The circumstances

relied upon by the prosecution did not establish that the Appellant had deliberately told

a lie out of a consciousness of guilt. Consequently, the application of the Lucas principle

in the present case is inconsistent with both its rationale and the well-established

principles governing its operation.

50. In light of the foregoing factual and legal analysis, the conclusion reached by the

Learned Trial Judge regarding the Appellant's guilt is unsupported by the evidence and

cannot be sustained.

51. More importantly, in the absence of any evidence establishing that the Appellant

exercised control or dominion over the contraband, or that he did any act indicative of

his knowledge of, or concurrence in, the possession of the heroin by the 1st accused, the

essential ingredients of joint possession have not been proved beyond reasonable doubt.

In those circumstances, it would be unsafe to allow the conviction to stand.

52. For the foregoing reasons, I set aside the conviction and sentence of the Appellant dated

08.07.2020.

53. Accordingly, the appeal is allowed.
