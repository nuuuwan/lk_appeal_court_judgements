Since I am not relying on the evidence of PW4, and noting that the sole eyewitness is PW2,

I am mindful of how our courts have considered such evidence, recognizing the evidence

of single eyewitness.

Sumanasena v. Attorney General, 1999 (3) SLR 137, Jayasuriya.J held

“In our law of evidence the salutary principle is enunciated that evidence must not

be counted, but weighed and the evidence of a single solitary witness if cogent and

impressive could be acted upon by a Court of law. Section 134 of the Evidence

Ordinance sets out that "no particular number of witnesses shall in any case be

required for the proof of any fact'. In an Indian case the conviction for murder was

affirmed on the mere circumstantial evidence given by a solitary witness and a

pointed reference was made to the principle which we have adumbrated above vide

Mulluwa v. The State of Maddhya Pradesh. Testimony must always be weighed

and not counted and these principles have been followed by Justice G. P. A. De

Silva in Walimunige John v. State; King v. N. A, Fernando(. Thus, the Court could

have acted on the evidence of the solitary witness Nandasena provided the trial

Judge was convinced that he was giving cogent, inspiring and truthful testimony

In Shivaji Sahebrao Bobade v. Maharashtra 1974 (1) SCR 489, Krishna Iyer,J held that;

“In our view there is only one eyewitness, P.W. 5, Vilas. Even if the case against

the accused hangs on the evidence of a single eye-witness it may be enough to

sustain the, conviction given sterling testimony of a competent, honest man,

although as a rule of prudence courts call for corroboration. It is a platitude to say

that witnesses have to be weighed and not counted since quality matters more

than quantity in human affairs. We are persuaded that the PW 5 is a witness for

truth but in view of the circumstances that he is interested, we would still want

“… (1) By and large a witness cannot be expected to possess a photographic

State of Uttar Pradesh v. M.K. Anthony, by D. A. Desai J, reported in Supreme Court

“While appreciating the evidence of a witness, the approach must be

find out whether it is against the general tenor of the evidence given by the witness

unworthy of belief. Minor discrepancies on trivial matters not touching the core of

the case, hyper-technical approach by taking sentences torn out of context here or

What is the evidentiary value of such dock statements?

This was considered in CA 186/2009 Kiribandara Muhandiramlage Rohana Tissa Kumara

v. AG Decided on 18.10.2011 W.L.Ranjith Silva, J page 133 (Criminal Law-Unreported

2011 Volume 2)

“A dock statement must be capable of creating a reasonable doubt and not a fanciful

doubt. (CA 89/2001). Even though the Trial Judge failed in his duty to consider the dock

statement adequately and impartially, if no credence can be given to the evidence of

the Appellant then there is no reason for the appellate court interfere with the

conviction. (vide. Dharmadasa v. Director General, Commission to Investigate Briery

and Corruption 2003 1 SLR 64). In Misnagollage Siriyawathie v. The State CA 159/95

(HC Avissawella 72/1992) it was held that even if there is a misdirection by the learned

trial Judge, in view of the overwhelming evidence placed against the accused by the

prosecution, and the correct findings and adjudications reached by the learned trial

judge on all the issues in the case, and in the Light of the cogent and overwhelming

material against the accused there is no reason for the Appellate Court to interfere with

the findings of the learned High Court Judge. It was held by the Court Simongs

Ekanayake v. The Attorney General. CA 129/2005 (HC Anuradhapura 142/2002) that

where the trial judge has the dock, nor miscarriage of justice would take place due to

the lapse of the Trial Judge if there is material to say that the dock statement is palpably

false, then the findings of the trial court should not be overturned.”

In the case of Queen Vs. Kularatne (1968) 71 NLR 529, it was held that while

jurors must be informed that such a statement must be looked upon as evidence

subjected however, to the infirmities that the accused’s statement is not made under

oath and not subjected to cross-examination.

Held further;

1. If the dock statement is believed, it must be acted upon to.

2. If it raised a reasonable doubt in their minds about the case of the prosecution,

the defence must succeed. and;

3. It must not be used against another accused.

‘ one must bear in mind that when a dock statement is considered anywhere in the

judgment, the judge who heard the evidence is aware of the prosecution case and would

The question before us is whether all four accused shared a common intention to murder

the deceased. As there are no eyewitnesses to identify who actually fired off the fatal shot,

I note that the case rests entirely upon circumstantial evidence. It is incumbent upon the

prosecution to establish, beyond a reasonable doubt, that the Appellants shared the

common murderous intention to commit the offence.

I note that all 4 Appellants were charged for murder under Section 32 of the Penal Code.

which I find myself in respectful agreement) if the Appellant’s actions are capable

of innocent explanation, even though such explanation is not given, the benefit of

such explanation should accrue to the Appellant. In contrast, in this case, the

Appellant has in fact offered the explanation for his subsequent conduct.”
