FACTUAL BACKGROUND

The Silvermill Coco Ventures (Pvt) Ltd (hereinafter will be referred to as the Appellant) is a

limited liability company domiciled in Sri Lanka and incorporated under the Companies Act No.

07 of 2007. The Appellant is involved in the business of producing steam power using biomass

for supplying energy.

The Appellant has submitted the return for the above taxable period claiming certain deduction

which was denied by the Assessor by the intimation letter dated 5th of January 2017. Accordingly,

the Assessor has issued assessment without granting those deductions by the Notice of

Assessment dated 3rd of March 2017. The Appellant being aggrieved by the decision of the

Assessor, made an appeal to the Commissioner General of Inland Revenue (hereinafter will be

referred to as the Respondent). The Respondent in his determination dated 11th of March 2018

determined the appeal by upholding the assessment issued by the Assessor. The Appellant being

dissatisfied with the said determination of the Respondent, made an appeal to the Tax Appeals

Commission (TAC). The TAC after considering the submissions made by both parties, dismissed

the appeal by the determination dated 6th of March 2024 and confirmed the determination made

by the Respondent.

Being dissatisfied with the said determination, the Appellant has requested the TAC to cause a

Before proceeding to analyze the questions of law before this Court, I find it is necessary to

briefly set out the relevant factual background of this case.

The Appellant is a sister company of Silvermill Holdings Limited (currently known as Giriulla

Mills Limited), both companies being managed by the same Board of Directors. The Appellant

acquired a total of 516,667 shares in Silvermill Holdings Limited on 21st of July 2011 and 23rd of

December 2011.

In 2015, the group has undertaken a corporate restructuring, whereby it has decided that the

Silvermill Holdings Limited would function as the holding company of the group, with the other

group entities being reorganized as its subsidiaries in accordance with the restructuring plan.

Consequently, in order to implement the restructuring, Silvermill Holdings Limited had

repurchased the shares held by the Appellant. (X11)

In computing its tax liability for the year of assessment 2014/2015, the Appellant has excluded

ANALYSIS

The questions of law II, III, IV, and V are in relation to the tax on gains from the disposal of the

unlisted shares by the Appellant. Therefore I shall address all these four questions together in the

following manner.

should be treated as the realization of a long-term investment and, accordingly, as a capital receipt

rather than taxable income.

The Respondent contends that the amount received from the sale of shares were derived in the

course of the Appellant's business with the intention of making a profit and, accordingly, are

liable to be taxed under sections 13(t) and 44 of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006.

The Appellant, on the other hand, contends that it does not engage in the regular trading of shares

and that the impugned transaction cannot therefore be characterized as part of a share trading

business. The Appellant further asserts that its Articles of Association do not identify share

trading as one of its objects or business activities.

It is the Appellant's position that the disposal of the shares occurred solely as part of a corporate

restructuring exercise and it constitutes the realization of a long-term investment rather than a

transaction undertaken in the ordinary course of business for the purpose of earning profits.

The section 13(t) of IRA 2006, which deals with the miscellaneous exemptions from income tax

and the section 44 of the IRA 2006, which deals with Rate of income tax on sale of any share or a

It should be noted that sections 13(t) and 44 of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006 serve

distinct purposes. Section 13(t) is a computational provision that sets out the manner in which

income need to be taxed, while section 44 prescribes the rate of income tax applicable to certain

profits arising from the sale of shares or share warrants.

Importantly, section 44 identifies a specific category of share disposals that are subject to tax,

namely disposals occurring within two years of acquisition. However, neither section 13(t) nor

section 44 establishes that every gain arising from the disposal of shares is taxable. These

provisions do not operate as charging provisions that bring all gains from share disposals within

the scope of income tax regardless of the nature of the transaction.

In applying section 44 of the IRA 2006, it should be noted that the provision applies only to

shares disposed within two years of their acquisition. However, the facts of the present case reveal

that the Appellant acquired the shares in Silvermill Holdings Limited on 21st of July 2011 and

23rd of December 2011, and subsequently disposed them to the same company on 7th of January

2015 (X26), which was well beyond the two-year period stipulated in section 44.

Accordingly, the gains arising from the disposal of those shares cannot be subjected to tax under

section 44 of the Act, as the disposal falls outside the period expressly prescribed by the

provision.

Therefore, I am of the view that the Respondent has misconstrued the scope and effect of sections

13(t) and 44 of the Act.

In light of the nature of the transaction and the circumstances of the present case, I shall now

proceed to consider whether the gain arising from the disposal of the shares is characterized as a

capital gain or as taxable income under the IRA 2006.

Capital Gains Tax is a tax on the gain realized from the transfer or disposal of an investment

asset. The taxable gain is generally calculated as the difference between the consideration

received on disposal and the cost of acquiring the investment asset. Capital gains tax was first

introduced in Sri Lanka by the Income Tax Act No. 13 of 1959, which expressly brought capital

gains within the scope of “profits and income” chargeable to income tax. Thereafter, gains

arising from the disposal of capital assets remained a taxable source of income under successive

Inland Revenue enactments. Under the Inland Revenue Act No. 38 of 1979, capital gains

constituted a distinct category of taxable income and included gains arising from, inter alia, the

transfer of property, the surrender or transfer of rights in property, the redemption of shares or

debentures, and certain corporate reorganizations.

Page 13 of 19

However, this position changed with the enactment of the Inland Revenue (Amendment) Act No.

10 of 2002, which abolished capital gains tax with effect from 31st of March 2003. Consequently,

gains of a capital nature ceased to be a separate source of income chargeable to tax. This has been

clearly accepted by both parties as well.

The issue in the present appeal is whether the gain derived by the Appellant from the transfer of

unquoted shares, which were allegedly held as a long-term investment, constitutes a capital

receipt arising from the realization of an investment or taxable income liable to income tax.

In addressing this issue, it is necessary to examine the charging provisions of the Inland Revenue

Act No. 10 of 2006. Section 2 of IRA 2006 imposes income tax on the “profits and income” of

every person for a year of assessment, while section 3 of IRA 2006 identifies the categories of

profits and income that are chargeable to tax.

Accordingly, the section 3 of IRA 2006, which deals with the Income chargeable with Tax,

mentions the following;

“3. For the purpose of this Act, “profits and income” or “profits” or “income” means;

(a) the profits from any trade, business, profession or vocation for however short a period

carried on or exercised;

(b) the profits from any employment;

(c) the net annual value of any land and improvements thereon occupied by or on behalf

of the owner, in so far as it is not so occupied for the purposes of a trade, business,

profession or vocation;

Page 14 of 19

(d) the net annual value of any land and improvements thereon used rent-free by the

occupier, if such net annual value is not taken into account in ascertaining profits and

income under paragraphs (a), (b) or (c) of this section, or where the rent paid for such

land and improvements is less than the net annual value, the excess of such net annual

value over the rent to be deemed in each case the income of the occupier;

(e) dividends, interest or discounts;

(f) charges or annuities;

(g) rents, royalties or premiums;

(h) winnings from a lottery, betting or gambling ;

(i) in the case of a non -governmental organization, any sum received by such

organization by way of grant, donation or contribution or any other manner; and

(j) income from any other source whatsoever, not including profits of a casual and non-

recurring nature.”

Significantly, the IRA 2006 does not include capital gains as a distinct source of taxable income.

Although the Respondent contends that the profits arising from the sale of shares fall within

section 3(j) of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006 and are therefore taxable on that basis, it

should be noted that section 3(j) is a residual provision intended to capture profits and income that

do not fall within the other specific categories enumerated in section 3. The application of section

3(j) must therefore be considered with due regard to its expressed limitations.

Specifically section 3(j) expressly excludes ‘profits of a casual and non-recurring nature.’ In the

present case, neither the Assessor nor the Respondent has properly considered or established

whether the gain arising from the disposal of the shares constitutes a profit of a casual and non-

Page 15 of 19

recurring nature. In particular, no adequate analysis appears to have been undertaken as to

whether the impugned transaction, falls within the scope of the aforesaid exclusion.

Accordingly, it is evident that the Assessor and the Respondent failed to give due consideration to

this material aspect of section 3(j) before concluding that the gain was liable to be taxed under

that provision.

However, it need to be noted that the question whether a gain, profit, or receipt is capital in nature

or constitutes income arising from a trade or business in the nature of its trade, depends on the

character and surrounding circumstances of the particular transaction, as well as the intention of

the taxpayer in acquiring and disposing of the asset.

Accordingly, the proceeds derived from the sale of an asset cannot become automatically taxable

merely on the basis that the transaction yields a profit. Therefore, the next question that needs to

be addressed is whether the said asset was held as a capital investment and subsequently realized,

or whether it was acquired and disposed of as part of a profit-making scheme.

Where the transaction amounts to the mere realization of a long-term investment, the resulting

gain is generally regarded as a capital receipt. Conversely, where the transaction forms part of a

commercial venture undertaken with a view to profit, the gain may be characterized as revenue

and therefore constitute taxable income.

In the English Case of Californian Copper Syndicate Ltd v Harris (1904) 5 TC 159 , Lord

Justice Clerk held the following view in determining whether a gain acquired through realization

of long term investment constitutes a Capital receipt or falls within the scope of taxable income.

“It is quite a well settled principle in dealing with questions of assessment of Income Tax,

that where the owner of an ordinary investment chooses to realise it, and obtains a

greater price for it than he originally acquired it at, the enhanced price is not profit in

Page 16 of 19

the sense of Schedule D of the Income Tax Act of 1842 assessable to Income Tax. But it

is equally well established that enhanced values obtained from realisation or conversion

of securities may be so assessable, where what is done is not merely a realisation or

change of investment, but an act done in what is truly the carrying on, or carrying out, of

a business. The simplest case is that of person or association of persons buying and

selling lands or securities speculatively, in order to make gain, dealing in such

investments as a business, and thereby seeking to make profits. There are many companies

which in their very inception are formed for such a purpose, and in these cases it is not

doubtful that, where they make a gain by a realisation, the gain they make is liable to be

assessed for Income Tax.

What is the line which separates the two classes of cases may be difficult to define, and

each case must be considered according to its facts, the question to be determined being-

Is the sum of gain that has been made a mere enhancement of value by realising a

security, or is it a gain made in an operation of business in carrying out a scheme for

profit-making?”

A similar approach has also been taken by His Lordship Dr. Ruwan Fernando, J in the case of

LOLC Finance PLC Vs. The Commissioner General of Inland Revenue CA /TAX/0018 /2019

decided on 27.01.2023.

“Any frequency or isolated transaction which is not carried on in the course of business is

not necessarily free from income tax but such frequency transaction not in the ordinary

course of the taxpayer’s business may provide a guide in determining whether a

transaction is one on capital or revenue nature (Silke on South African Income, p. 32).

The question whether an isolated transaction not in the course of business is in the

nature of a capital or revenue depends on the profit-making intention behind the

transaction (supra, p. 32).

Page 17 of 19

Accordingly, where a person buys a property with the intention of reselling it at a profit

and any such profit made would be taxable even though such person does not ordinarily

carry on any business of buying and selling property and even though the transaction may

be an isolated one (supra, p. 32). The test is that if the profit arises out of trading, it is

taxable notwithstanding the fact that the transaction is one of isolated one (Stephen v.

C.I.R. (1919) W.L.D. 1). This means that the fact that a transaction is isolated is not

decisive to decide whether the receipt is the capital or income and what is decisive is

whether the assessee is doing business of selling and buying property even though it

carries on only one single transaction (C.I.R. v. Stott, (1928) A.D. 252).

The resulting position is that the proceeds derived from a sale of property would be of an

income nature under section 3 (a) if the assets were acquired for the purpose of resale at a

profit-making while carrying on of a trade or business notwithstanding the fact that the

transaction may be of an isolated nature (Silke on South African Income, p. 40). On the

other hand, if the property is acquired for the purpose of using it for the purpose of

deriving income i.e., as an investment, and not for the carrying out of a scheme for

profit-making, the proceeds derived from a subsequent sale are of a capital nature

(supra)”

In light of the foregoing authorities, the crucial aspect that need to be addressed is the true nature

of the transaction in question and the intention of the party. The Appellant has established that the

gain arising from the disposal of the shares resulted from a one-time transaction undertaken

pursuant to a group restructuring exercise. The materials before this Court does not indicate that

the Appellant regularly engaged in the acquisition and disposal of shares or otherwise carried on a

business of investing for the purpose of deriving profits.

Moreover, the Respondent has failed to convince this court that the Appellant was engaged in the

business of investing or trading in shares. The mere fact that the disposal yielded a profit is

insufficient, in itself, to characterize the gain as taxable income.

Page 18 of 19

Accordingly, I am of the view that the Tax Appeals Commission erroneously concluded that the

Appellant was engaged in the business of investing and the gain arising from the disposal of its

unlisted shares in Silvermill Holdings Limited pursuant to a group restructuring process was

liable to income tax under the IRA 2006.

For the foregoing reasons, I answer Questions of Law II, III, IV, and V in the affirmative.
