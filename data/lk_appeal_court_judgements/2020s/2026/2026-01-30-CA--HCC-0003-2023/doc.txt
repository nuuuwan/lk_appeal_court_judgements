Pradeep Hettiarachchi, J

Judgment

1. The appellant was indicted before the High Court of Tangalle on three counts of

committing grave sexual abuse on a girl under sixteen years of age, offences

punishable under Section 365B(2)(b) of the Penal Code, as amended by Act No. 22 of

1995 and Act No. 29 of 1998.

2. The trial commenced before the learned High Court Judge of Tangalle, and at the

conclusion of the trial, the appellant was found guilty of all three charges and

accordingly convicted. The learned trial Judge sentenced the appellant to ten years’

rigorous imprisonment on each count. Further, the learned trial Judge ordered the

appellant to pay a sum of Rs. 500,000.00 as compensation to the victim, with a default

sentence of one year’s simple imprisonment.

3. Being aggrieved by the said conviction and sentence, the appellant has preferred the

present appeal. As directed by Court, both parties have filed their respective written

submissions.

4. In his written submissions, the appellant advanced several grounds of appeal;

however, at the hearing, counsel confined the argument solely to the alleged failure to

prove the age of the victim. The learned Additional Solicitor General likewise

conceded that the age of the victim had not been proved. Regrettably, neither learned

counsel for the appellant nor the learned Additional Solicitor General exercised due

diligence in the present appeal, as the birth certificate of the victim had in fact been

marked in evidence during the trial before the High Court.

5. As is evident from the proceedings dated 12.09.2017, the age of the victim was in

dispute, and the learned High Court Judge directed the prosecution to tender a copy of

the victim’s birth certificate on the next date of trial. Accordingly, on 02.08.2018, the

original birth certificate of the victim, together with a copy thereof, was tendered to

court. The copy was filed of record, and the original was returned to the victim.

6. According to the birth certificate, the date of birth of the victim is 28.11.1994. The

period of the alleged offences, as stated in the charges, was between 01.06.2008 and

07.12.2008. It is therefore evident that the victim was under sixteen years of age at the

time of the alleged offences. Accordingly, the ground advanced by the appellant

regarding the alleged failure to prove the age of the victim is devoid of merit.

7. Nevertheless, I shall proceed to consider the other grounds of appeal as well,

notwithstanding the fact that the appellant did not pursue them during oral argument,

although they were raised in the written submissions. The appellant contended that the

evidence of the prosecutrix was not corroborated and, therefore, that it would be

unsafe to act on such uncorroborated evidence in a case of this nature.

8. The appellant place much reliance on the case of Premasiri vs Attorney General

[2006] (3) Sri.L.R. 107 where it was held inter alia that corroboration is essential

before there can be a conviction in a case pf rape, but the necessity of corroboration as

a matter of prudence., except where the circumstances make it unsafe to dispense with

it, must be present to the mind of the Judge.

9. It must be emphasized that there is no hard and fast rule requiring corroboration in

cases of this nature. It is well recognized that offences of this kind are often

committed in secrecy, with the perpetrator deliberately choosing an isolated and

discreet location to ensure that no one is present to witness the act. Consequently, it is

only in rare cases that independent witnesses are available to corroborate the evidence

of the prosecutrix.

10. In offences of grave sexual abuse, corroboration may sometimes be available in the

form of medical evidence, depending on the nature and gravity of the act. In certain

instances, medical evidence may confirm the commission of the offence; however, in

many cases, particularly where the act complained of involves intercrural sexual acts,

no physical traces may be present.

11. What is of paramount importance is a careful examination and analysis of the

testimony of the prosecutrix. If such testimony is found to be cogent, credible, and

free from material discrepancies, the Court is entitled to act upon it even in the

absence of corroborative evidence.

12. In State of Maharashtra v. Chandraprakash Kewalchand Jain, (1990) 1 SCC 550]

the Indian Supreme Court held:

‘that a woman, who is the victim of sexual assault, is not an accomplice to the

crime but is a victim of another person's lust and, therefore, her evidence need

not be tested with the same amount of suspicion as that of an accomplice.

The Court observed at page 559 as follows:

‘A prosecutrix of a sex offence cannot be put on a par with an accomplice. She

is in fact a victim of the crime. The Evidence Act nowhere says that her

evidence cannot be accepted unless it is corroborated in material particulars.

She is undoubtedly a competent witness under Section 118 and her evidence

must receive the same weight as is attached to an injured in cases of physical

violence. The same degree of care and caution must attach in the evaluation of

her evidence as in the case of an injured complainant or witness and no more.

What is necessary is that the court must be alive to and conscious of the fact

that it is dealing with the evidence of a person who is interested in the outcome

of the charge levelled by her. If the court keeps this in mind and feels satisfied

that it can act on the evidence of the prosecutrix, there is no rule of law or

practice incorporated in the Evidence Act similar to Illustration (b) to Section

114 which requires it to look for corroboration.

13. In the present case, the Judicial Medical Officer who examined the victim testified

that, as the victim was presented before him nearly two months after the alleged

offence, he was unable to observe any injuries. The JMO further provided an adequate

explanation as to why such injuries may not have been present, having regard to the

nature of the act complained of. Accordingly, in my view, the absence of injuries on

the prosecutrix cannot be regarded as a factor that would justify absolving the

accused.

14. Our courts have consistently held that a conviction may be based on the sole

testimony of the prosecutrix. It is only where the court is not satisfied with the version

of the prosecutrix that it becomes necessary to seek other evidence, whether direct or

circumstantial, to obtain assurance of her testimony.

15. In the present case, a careful examination of the evidence of the prosecutrix reveals no

material infirmities going to the root of the prosecution case. Accordingly, I see no

necessity to seek further corroborative evidence to substantiate her testimony.

16. In the appellant’s written submissions, it was further contended that the complaint was

belated and that, on that basis, the evidence of the prosecutrix ought not to be

accepted. Although the learned trial Judge did not expressly address the issue of the

alleged belatedness of the complaint in the judgment, the prosecutrix, in her evidence,

provided a satisfactory explanation for the delay. Her responses during cross-

examination clearly evince the fear under which she was labouring, particularly in

view of the violent conduct attributed to the appellant.

17. Thus, even though the learned trial Judge failed to address the issue of the delay in

making the complaint by the prosecutrix, in my view, such omission is not fatal to the

sustainability of the conviction, as the prosecutrix, in her testimony, had adequately

explained the delay.

18. Upon the above analysis of the law and facts, I find no merit in the arguments

advanced by the defence. Accordingly, I see no reason to interfere with the judgment

of the learned High Court Judge.

19. Nevertheless, I shall consider the appropriateness of the sentence imposed on the

appellant by the learned trial Judge. The appellant was sentenced to ten years’

rigorous imprisonment on each count, the sentences to run concurrently. In addition,

the appellant was ordered to pay a sum of Rs. 500,000.00 as compensation to the

prosecutrix, with a default sentence of one year’s simple imprisonment.

20. However, having regard to the nature of the acts complained of, the absence of any

injuries to the victim, the lack of evidence of the use of violence, and the socio-

economic status of the appellant, I am of the view that the amount of compensation

requires revision. Accordingly, the compensation payable by the appellant is reduced

to Rs. 100,000.00.

21. In the circumstances, the conviction of the appellant on all counts is affirmed. The

sentence of ten years’ rigorous imprisonment imposed on each count, to run

concurrently, is also affirmed. However, the order directing the appellant to pay a sum

of Rs. 500,000.00 as compensation to the prosecutrix is varied, and the compensation

is reduced to Rs. 100,000.00. The default sentence of one year’s simple imprisonment

shall stand modified accordingly. Subject to the above variation, the appeal is

dismissed.
