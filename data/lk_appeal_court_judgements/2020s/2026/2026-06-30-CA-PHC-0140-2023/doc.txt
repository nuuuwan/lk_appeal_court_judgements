Judgment Amal Ranaraja. J,

1. The accused petitioner-appellant (hereinafter referred to as the

‘appellant’) has been named as the accused in the Colombo Magistrates

Court in Case No. 8160/01/12. Upon the appellant appearing before

Court as the accused in that case, the learned Magistrate has proceeded

to frame charges against him. Thereafter, the charges have been read

out to the appellant. The appellant has pleaded not guilty.

2. Accordingly, the learned Magistrate has proceeded with the case (trial)

against the appellant. Midway through the proceedings, the appellant

has absconded. The learned Magistrate has conducted an inquiry, and

being satisfied that the appellant was absconding and evading the

3

jurisdiction of the Court, has ordered that the case (trial), proceed in

the appellant’s absence.

3. Upon the conclusion of the case (trial), the learned Magistrate has

convicted the appellant of the charges and issued a sentencing order.

Being aggrieved by both the conviction and the sentencing order, the

appellant has filed an application for revision in the High Court of

Colombo, seeking that the learned Magistrate’s conviction and

sentencing order be revised and set aside. The learned High Court

Judge of Colombo has dismissed the application. Aggrieved by that

order, the appellant has preferred the present appeal to this Court.

4. Contumacious conduct refers to behaviour that is deliberately

disobedient or rebellious, particularly in a legal or authoritative context.

In legal proceedings, such conduct often manifests as contempt of

Court, where an individual wilfully disregards the Court’s authority,

orders or proceedings.

5. Such behaviour can undermine the rule of law and the administration

of justice. For instance, a person who refuses to comply with a Court

order, such as failing to provide necessary documents or not attending

a scheduled hearing, exhibits contumacious conduct. This defiance not

only disrupts the legal process but also poses challenges for judges and

legal practitioners striving to uphold the law.

6. The appellant, by absconding from Court, has wilfully disregarded the

Magistrates Court’s authority. The conduct undermines the rule of law

and obstructs the proper administration of justice. It amounts to

contumacious behaviour, a deliberate and defiant refusal to comply

with the Magistrates Court’s processes and orders. By choosing to evade

the Court rather than participate in the proceedings, the appellant has

placed himself beyond the reach of the legal process and has shown

open disrespect for the judicial system.

4

7. Absconding is not a mere procedural lapse or misunderstanding. It is a

conscious act that frustrates the Court’s ability to manage the case

efficiently. It also creates practical prejudice including delay, increased

costs, and difficulty in enforcing orders, all of which impair public

confidence in the justice system. Such behaviour is properly

characterized as contumacious because, it reflects wilful disobedience

and an attitude of defiance towards the authority of Courts.

8. In these circumstances, the appellant could not have reasonably sought

the High Court’s assistance by way of discretionary relief, while

simultaneously refusing to submit to the Magistrates Court’s

jurisdiction and proceedings. The integrity of the judicial process

requires that parties respect and comply with Court directions.

Deliberate absconding strikes at the heart of that requirement.

9. In Sudharman De Silva vs AG (1986) 1 SLR 9 at pages 13 and 14,

Sharvanana CJ has held as follows;

“The Court of Appeal has endorsed and applied the following

observation of Roskill, C. J. in the case of Robert Edward Wynyard: "To

grant this application at this stage would, in the view of this court, be

to put a premium on prisoners jumping bail; it may even have the effect

of encouraging others to do so. It might also have as a side effect,

increasing the reluctance of a court in a very long trial to grant bail lest

the applicant's conduct be repeated by others. To put a premium on

jumping bail is something which this court is not for one moment

prepared to countenance... the applicant has brought this entirely on

his own head, and he must now take the consequences. The application

therefore is refused." In my view this quotation was appropriate in the

context in which it was uttered, namely where an application to court

was made for the exercise of a discretion i.e. extension of time within

which to apply for leave to appeal, in favour of the applicant.

5

Contumacious conduct on the part of the applicant is a relevant

consideration when the exercise of a discretion in his favour is involved,

but not when he asserts his statutory right to appeal and is not asking

for the favour of any permission.”

10. In such circumstances, I am not inclined to interfere with the disputed

order of the learned High Court Judge of Colombo made in Case No.

HCRA 52/2023, dated 04th July 2023, and proceed to affirm the same.

11. I dismiss the appeal but make no order regarding costs.

Appeal dismissed.

12. The Registrar of this Court is directed to send this Judgement to the
