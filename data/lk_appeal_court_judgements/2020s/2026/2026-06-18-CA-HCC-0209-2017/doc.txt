Judgment

1. This appeal arises from the judgment and sentence of the Learned High Court Judge

of Anuradhapura dated 17.10.2017. The Appellant was convicted of the murder of

Udagedara Narada Tisura Ratnakumara, an offence punishable under Section 296

of the Penal Code.

2. Initially, there were two accused named in the indictment. However, as the 2nd

Accused died prior to the service of the indictment, the charge was subsequently

amended, and the trial proceeded only against the 1st Accused, who is the Appellant

in the present appeal.

3. The prosecution case was based entirely on circumstantial evidence. The principal

contention advanced by the Appellant is that the circumstantial evidence adduced at

the trial does not give rise to the only irresistible inference of the Appellant’s guilt

and, therefore, falls short of the standard required to sustain a conviction.

4. PW1, Arambegedara Siril, was the first witness to testify for the prosecution. The

Appellant is a nephew of PW1 and, during the relevant period, resided in close

proximity to him. According to the evidence of PW1, the Appellant and his father

were the only occupants of the house in which they lived. The deceased was also

known to PW1.

5. On 3rd February 2012, PW1 saw the deceased at the Appellant’s house consuming

liquor together with the Appellant and his father. At around 11.00 p.m. on the same

day, the Appellant came to PW1’s house seeking assistance, although he did not

disclose the reason for doing so.

6. Thereafter, PW1 accompanied the Appellant to the latter’s house. Upon entering the

house, PW1 observed the body of the deceased hanging from a beam of the roof.

The Appellant sought PW1’s assistance to lower the body. However, PW1 declined

to do so and shortly thereafter left the scene and returned home. Upon his return, he

asked his wife to pack a bag, and the two of them then proceeded to a place called

Ambagahagama, where one Somapala resided. There, they knocked on the door and

called for Somapala.

7. When Somapala opened the door, PW1 informed him of the incident, whereupon

arrangements were made to report the matter to the police. The incident was

subsequently reported, and PW1 accompanied the police to the Appellant’s

residence at around 2.20 a.m. on the following day. By that time, the body was no

longer at the house, and the premises had been washed and cleaned.

8. Subsequently, the body of the deceased was recovered from a tank known as

Bilibewa. Since there were no eyewitnesses to the commission of the offence, the

prosecution relied entirely on circumstantial evidence to prove the charge against

the Appellant.

9. Accordingly, the Court must consider whether the evidence adduced by the

prosecution is sufficient to lead to the only reasonable and irresistible inference of

the Appellant’s guilt, and whether such evidence is inconsistent with any other

reasonable inference consistent with the Appellant’s innocence.

10. Since the Appellant's principal contention is that the circumstantial evidence led by

the prosecution does not give rise to the only reasonable and irresistible inference

of his guilt.

11. I think at this stage, it would be apposite to examine the legal principles applicable

to the evaluation of circumstantial evidence in criminal trials.

12. In King vs. Gunarathne 47 NLR 145, it was held that:

“In a case of circumstantial evidence, the facts given in evidence may, taken

cumulatively, be sufficient to rebut the presumption of innocence, although each

fact, when taken separately, may be a circumstance only of suspicion.

The Jury are entitled to draw inferences unfavorable to an accused where he is

not called to establish an innocent explanation of evidence given by the

prosecution, which, without such explanation, tells for his guilt.”

13. In Don Sunny vs. Attorney General [1998] (2) Sri. L.R. 1 it was held that:

1. When a charge is sought to be proved by circumstantial evidence the proved

items of circumstantial evidence when taken together must irresistibly point

towards the only inference that the accused committed the offence.

On a consideration of all the evidence the only inference that can be arrived at

should be consistent with the guilt of the accused only.

2. If on a consideration of the items of circumstantial evidence if an inference can

be drawn which is consistent with the innocence of the accused, then one cannot

say that the charges have been proved beyond reasonable doubt.

3. If upon a consideration of the proved items of circumstantial evidence if the only

inference that can be drawn is that the accused committed the offence then they

can be found guilty.

The prosecution must prove that no one else other than the accused had the

opportunity of committing the offence, the accused can be found guilty only and

5 | P a g e

only if the proved items of circumstantial evidence is consistent with their guilt

and inconsistent with their innocence.

14. In Krishantha De Silva vs. The Attorney General [2003] (1) Sri. L.R, 162 it was

held inter alia that:

“Circumstantial evidence can be acted upon only if from the circumstances relied

upon the only reasonable inference to draw is the inference of guilt. If the

circumstances are consistent both with guilt and with innocence then the case is

not proved on circumstantial evidence.”

Per Edirisuriya, J.

“The hypothesis of innocence must be excluded by the circumstance relied upon

and the circumstances must point to one conclusion alone, i.e. the guilt of the

accused"

15. In Premawansha vs. Attorney General (2009) 2 Sri. L.R. 205 it was held that:

“In a case of circumstantial evidence if an inference of guilt is to be drawn, such

an inference must be the one and only irresistible and inescapable conclusion that

the accused committed the offence.”

16. When considering a case founded on circumstantial evidence, the Court must bear

in mind that the prosecution is required to prove beyond reasonable doubt that the

circumstances established at trial point precisely to the guilt of the accused. The

circumstantial evidence must be consistent only with the hypothesis of guilt and

inconsistent with any other reasonable hypothesis, including that of innocence.

17. At this point, I wish to set out a set of rules out lined in the case of Junaiden

Mohamed Haaris vs. Hon. Attorney General (SC Appeal 118-2017) decided on

9.11.2018 by Justice Aluwihare, which are as follows,

“1) The inference sought to be drawn must be consistent with all the proved facts,

if it is not, then the inference cannot be drawn.

CA/HCC/0209/2017

6 | P a g e

2) The proved facts should be such that they exclude every reasonable inference

from them, save the one to be drawn. If they do not exclude other reasonable

inferences, then there must be a doubt whether the inference sought to be drawn

is correct (per Watermeyer J. in R v Blom 1939 A.D.188) The rule regarding the

exclusion of every hypothesis of innocence before drawing the inference of guilt

was laid down way back in 1838 in the case of R vs Hodges 1838 2 Lew, cc.227).

The circumstances must be such as to produce moral certainty to the exclusion of

every reasonable doubt.” In the same judgment further reference has been made

to the definition of circumstantial evidence in the book by E. R. S. R.

Coomarswamy “The Law of Evidence” where it has stated as follows, “The chain

or strand of proved facts and circumstances must be so complete that no link in it

is missing. If any vital factor which is necessary to make the chain or strand

complete is missing or has remained unproved, it must be held that the prosecution

has failed to establish its case. A vital link should never be inferred.”

18. With these legal principles in mind, I shall proceed to consider the present appeal.

In the impugned judgment, the Learned Trial Judge identified ten items of

circumstantial evidence and concluded that those circumstances, when considered

cumulatively, led to the irresistible inference of the Appellant’s guilt.

19. However, a careful examination of those circumstances reveals that some of the

matters treated by the Learned Trial Judge as items of circumstantial evidence do

not, in fact, emerge from the prosecution evidence. Consequently, the inclusion of

such unsupported circumstances weakens and severs vital links in the chain of

circumstantial evidence upon which the prosecution case rests.

20. In the impugned judgment, the Learned Trial Judge observed that the prosecution

had proved that the short trousers worn by the Appellant when he met PW1 bore

stains resembling blood. However, a careful examination of the testimony of PW1

reveals no such evidence. PW1 did not state that he observed any bloodstains, or

stains resembling blood, on the Appellant’s clothing. Accordingly, this circumstance

7 | P a g e

appears to have been relied upon by the Learned Trial Judge without any evidentiary

foundation in the prosecution case.

21. Item No. 06 relates to the recovery of a knife believed to belong to the deceased.

However, that circumstance has little, if any, probative value in establishing the

charge against the Appellant, as it does not connect the Appellant to the commission

of the offence, nor does it constitute a vital link in the chain of circumstantial

evidence relied upon by the prosecution.

22. Furthermore, the Learned Trial Judge’s conclusion that the body of the deceased

was recovered pursuant to information furnished by the Appellant and rendered

admissible under Section 27 of the Evidence Ordinance is factually incorrect. The

prosecution failed to mark any portion of the Appellant’s statement allegedly

leading to the discovery of the body. In the absence of such evidence, it cannot

properly be said that the recovery of the body was a consequence of information

supplied by the Appellant within the meaning of Section 27 of the Evidence

Ordinance. Accordingly, that circumstance could not have been relied upon as an

incriminating factor against the Appellant.

23. The DNA report marked P21 was admitted in evidence under Section 420 of the

Code of Criminal Procedure Act. According to the DNA report marked P21, nine

items, namely P1 to P9 and P11 to P13, were submitted to Genetech for DNA

analysis.

24. P1 consisted of a blood sample collected on a cotton swab from the scene of the

crime. P2 and P3 were blood samples collected on cotton swabs from the motorcycle

found at the scene. P4 was a bloodstain recovered from a curtain at the crime scene,

while P9 was a bloodstained knife.

25. P11 was the bloodstained pair of shorts allegedly worn by the Appellant. P12

consisted of a piece of gauze said to contain a blood sample obtained from the body

of the deceased, Narada Rathnakumara.

26. The conclusions reached by Genetech are set out at page 5 of the report marked P21.

According to the report, the stains found on items P1, P2, P3, P4, and P11 originated

from the same male individual. The report further states that the stains found on P9

also originated from that same male individual. Accordingly, the DNA analysis

establishes only that the biological material recovered from those items came from

a single male source.

27. However, it does not establish that the stains found on the items sent for DNA

analysis were from the deceased or from the Appellant. The report merely indicates

that the samples were derived from the same male individual. In the absence of

evidence identifying that individual as either the Appellant or the deceased, the

report is of limited probative value. It does not establish any nexus between the

Appellant and the offence and, therefore, cannot be relied upon as a material link in

the chain of circumstantial evidence.

28. To elaborate further, the DNA report not only failed to complete the chain of

circumstantial evidence but also gave rise to the possibility of the presence of

another male individual at the scene of the crime. Consequently, rather than

strengthening the prosecution's case, the DNA evidence introduces an additional

uncertainty into the evidence of the prosecution.

29. Surprisingly, the Learned Trial Judge concluded that the DNA report established a

link between the scene of crime, the offence, and the Appellant. In my view, such a

conclusion is illogical in the absence of any evidentiary link connecting the analyzed

material either to the deceased or to the Appellant.

30. In these circumstances, the circumstantial evidence adduced by the prosecution

lacks the requisite cogency and completeness necessary to establish the Appellant’s

guilt beyond reasonable doubt. Accordingly, the prosecution has failed to present a

chain of circumstances leading exclusively to the inference of the Appellant’s guilt

and excluding every other reasonable hypothesis consistent with his innocence.

31. While assessing the probative strength of the circumstantial evidence relied upon

by the prosecution, I am mindful of the oft-cited observations of Pollock C.B. in

Regina v. Exall at 929, subsequently approved and adopted by Cannon J. in King

v. Gunarathne at 149, which provide valuable guidance on the manner in which

courts should evaluate a chain of circumstantial evidence.

“It has been said that circumstantial evidence is to be considered as a chain, and

each piece of evidence as a link in the chain, but that is not so, for them, if any

one link broke, the chain would fall. It is more like the case of a rope composed of

several cords. One strand of the rope might be insufficient to sustain the weight,

but three stranded together may be quite of sufficient strength.”

32. However, in the present case, the infirmities discernible in the prosecution evidence

do not persuade this Court to apply the principle enunciated in Regina v. Exall and

King v. Gunarathne (supra). The deficiencies identified are not confined to

peripheral or insignificant strands of the rope of circumstantial evidence. Rather,

they affect some of the most vital and indispensable links upon which the

prosecution's case is founded.

33. Consequently, the breakdown of those strands has the effect of severing the very

rope of circumstantial evidence relied upon by the prosecution. In such

circumstances, it cannot be said that the remaining evidence forms a complete and

unbroken rope leading exclusively to the inference of the Appellant’s guilt.

34. At the trial, the prosecution led the evidence of PW8, Sub-Inspector Ubhaya

Nandana of the Nochchiyagama Police. According to his testimony, he recovered

certain items pursuant to information allegedly furnished by the Appellant in a

statement made to him. Among the items so recovered were a knife, a T-shirt, and

the body of the deceased.

35. The specific portions of the Appellant’s statement pursuant to which the said

recoveries were allegedly made were marked in evidence as P11. It is noteworthy

that the learned Trial Judge treated the recovery of the deceased’s body as an

incriminating circumstance admissible under Section 27 of the Evidence Ordinance.

36. However, a careful examination of P11 reveals that no portion of the Appellant’s

statement leading to the discovery of the deceased’s body was marked by the

prosecution. In the absence of such a marked statement, there is no evidentiary basis

upon which the recovery of the body could be attributed to information furnished

by the Appellant within the meaning of Section 27 of the Evidence Ordinance.

Accordingly, the Learned Trial Judge erred in treating the recovery of the body as a

circumstance admissible and relevant against the Appellant under that provision.

37. The foregoing facts demonstrate that the Learned Trial Judge, in arriving at his

conclusion, took into account certain extraneous matters as items of circumstantial

evidence, notwithstanding that such matters were never established by the evidence

adduced at the trial. Consequently, reliance on those unsupported circumstances

materially affected the evaluation of the prosecution case and also undermines the

accuracy of the conclusion reached.

38. As stated elsewhere in this judgment, in order to sustain a conviction based on

circumstantial evidence, the evidence adduced by the prosecution must lead to the

one and only inference of the Appellant’s guilt. It must also be inconsistent with any

other reasonable inference that is compatible with the Appellant’s innocence.

39. Where the Learned Trial Judge has taken into consideration certain items of

evidence which do not establish any link between the Appellant and the commission

of the offence, in arriving at a conclusion of guilt, such an approach undermines the

very foundation of the legal principles governing the evaluation of circumstantial

evidence.

40. The Supreme Court of India, in the judgment of Sharad Birdhichand Sarda vs.

State of Maharashtra, (1984) 4 SCC 166, laid down a five-point test, which it

CA/HCC/0209/2017

11 | P a g e

described as the “panchasheel of the proof of a case based on circumstantial

evidence”, which must be satisfied before a conviction is entered against an accused

in such a case. Paragraph 153 of the said judgment states: “[A] close analysis of this

decision would show that the following conditions must be fulfilled before a case

against an accused can be said to be fully established;

i. the circumstances from which the conclusion of guilt should be fully

established,

ii. the facts so established should be consistent only with the hypothesis of the

guilt of the accused, that is to say, they should not be explainable on any

other hypothesis except that the accused is guilty,

iii. the circumstances should be of a conclusive nature and tendency,

iv. they should exclude every possible hypothesis except the one to be proved,

and

v. there must be a chain of evidence so complete as not to leave any reasonable

ground for the conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and

must show that in all human probability the act must have been done by the

accused.”

41. It is also pertinent to observe that the Learned Trial Judge appears to have attached

significance to the Appellant’s failure to respond to certain incriminating evidence

adduced by the prosecution, noting in the judgment that the Appellant had neither

addressed nor offered any explanation in respect of that evidence.

42. Our apex courts have repeatedly emphasized that the burden of proving every

element of the offence beyond reasonable doubt rests at all times upon the

prosecution. The accused bears no burden to establish his innocence or to offer any

CA/HCC/0209/2017

12 | P a g e

explanation in respect of the incriminating circumstances relied upon by the

prosecution. The burden never shifts from the prosecution throughout the trial.

43. If the accused offers an explanation which is accepted by the Court, or which at least

raises a reasonable doubt as to the prosecution case, that would be sufficient to

entitle him to an acquittal. It is not necessary for the accused to prove his explanation

beyond reasonable doubt; it is sufficient if the explanation creates a reasonable

doubt regarding the prosecution's version of events. In this regard, the following

judgments provide valuable guidance.

44. In the case of Pantis vs. The Attorney General [1998] 2 SLR 148, where Justice

Wijeyaratne held that,

“The burden of proof is always on the prosecution to prove all ingredients of the

charge beyond reasonable doubt and there is no burden in our law for the accused

to give any explanation (unless in certain cases where specific provision is made

by law). In my view it is sufficient if the accused gives an explanation which

satisfies the court or at least is sufficient to create a reasonable doubt as to his

guilt.”

45. In the present case, the prosecution evidence is marred by material discrepancies

and infirmities which sever vital strands in the rope of circumstantial evidence relied

upon by the prosecution. Consequently, the prosecution has failed to establish a

complete and unbroken chain of circumstances leading exclusively to the guilt of

the Appellant. In that event, the question of evaluating the credibility of the defence

evidence does not arise, for the weakness of the defence cannot cure the deficiencies

in the prosecution case.

46. Upon a consideration of the foregoing facts and the applicable legal principles, I am

of the view that it would be unsafe to allow the conviction to stand. Accordingly,

the appeal is allowed, and the conviction and sentence dated 17.10.2017 are hereby

set aside. The Appellant is acquitted of the charge.
