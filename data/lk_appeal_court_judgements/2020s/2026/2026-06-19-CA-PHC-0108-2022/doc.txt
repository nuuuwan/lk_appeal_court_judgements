When this matter came on before us for argument on 12.12.2025, the learned

Counsel for the parties of the 2nd Part Respondents-Respondents-Respondents

(hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Respondents’) indicated to Court that

he would be taking up several preliminary legal objections as to the

maintainability of the instant appeal and the Counsel for the Respondents as

well as the Counsel for the Parties of the 1st Part Respondent-Petitioner-

Appellants (hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Appellant’) desired this

Court to give a ruling on the preliminary legal objections on the strength of the

written submissions and the parties were thus, directed to furnish written

submissions to this Court within the period stipulated by this Court. However,

it is to be observed that the written submissions were furnished to this Court by

the Respondents only. Hence, we are proceeding to hear and determine the

preliminary legal objections on the strength of the written submissions as urged

by Counsel.

The preliminary legal objections so raised to Court by the learned Counsel for

the Respondents are two-fold and they may be re-produced verbatim the same

as follows;

I.) The impugned Order by the Provincial High Court of Embilipitiya

dated 2nd June 2022, sought to be challenged before Your Lordships'

Court, is purely Interlocutory in nature and does not amount to a

final determination of the rights of the parties. Nevertheless, the

Appellant has purported to invoke the Appellate Jurisdiction of this

Honourable Court by filing an appeal (final appeal) against the said

Order. An appeal under Article 154P(6) lies only in respect of final

judgments, decrees, or orders that amount to a final determination

of the rights of the parties delivered by the High Court. The proper

procedure to challenge an Interlocutory Order is by way of an

application for Leave to Appeal. Accordingly, the present appeal is

misconceived in law and is liable to be dismissed in limine as it is

misconceived and contrary to law.

II.) The Petition of Appeal filed by the 1 Part Respondents-Petitioners-

Appellants does not bear the mandatory certificate of an Attorney-at-

Law certifying that the questions of law identified are fit for

adjudication by this Honourable Court, as required by Rule 4(2) of

the Court of Appeal (Procedure for Appeals from the High Courts)

Rules, 1988. The said non-compliance renders the Petition of Appeal

fatally defective.

I will firstly, deal with the preliminary legal objection No I).

I) The impugned Order by the Provincial High Court of Embilipitiya

dated 2nd June 2022, sought to be challenged before Your Lordships'

Court, is purely Interlocutory in nature and does not amount to a

final determination of the rights of the parties. Nevertheless, the

Appellant has purported to invoke the Appellate Jurisdiction of this

Honourable Court by filing an appeal (final appeal) against the said

Order. An appeal under Article 154P(6) lies only in respect of final

judgments, decrees, or orders that amount to a final determination

of the rights of the parties delivered by the High Court. The proper

procedure to challenge an Interlocutory Order is by way of an

application for Leave to Appeal. Accordingly, the present appeal is

misconceived in law and is liable to be dismissed in limine as it is

misconceived and contrary to law.

The facts and circumstances which led to the institution of this appeal before

this Court by the parties of the 1st Part Respondent-Petitioner-Appellants

(hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Appellants’) as is discernible from

the petition of appeal, may now, be examined.

Proceedings commenced before the Magistrate Court of Embilipitiya in case

bearing No. 88290/22 upon filing the information by the Officer-In-Charge of

the Police Station-Embilipitiya-the Complainant-Respondent-Respondent to

the instant appeal (hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Complainant’)

under section 66(1)(a) of the Primary Court Procedure Act of No. 44 of 1979

(hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Act’) and the learned Magistrate of

Embilipitiya had in her order dated 04.03.2022 (hereinafter called and

referred to as ‘the MC order’), refused to assume the jurisdiction under Part

VII of the Act on the premise that no breach of peace is threatened or likely.

Being aggrieved by the MC order the Appellants had invoked the revisionary

jurisdiction of the Provincial High Court of Embilipitiya and the learned

Provincial High Court Judge had in his order dated 02.06.2022 (hereinafter

called and referred to as ‘the HC order’) refused to issue notices on the

Respondents named therein, on the premise that, petition does not disclose

exceptional circumstances warranting the intervention of the Court in

revision of the MC order. Being aggrieved by the HC order, the Appellants has

preferred the instant appeal to this Court from the HC order seeking to set

aside the same.

The Constitution of the Democratic Socialist Republic of Sri Lanka

(hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Constitution’) in its Article 154P(6)

provides for a right of appeal and it reads thus;

“(6) subject to the provisions of the Constitution and any law, any person

aggrieved by a final order, judgement or sentence of any such Court, in

the exercise of its jurisdiction under paragraphs (3)(b) or (3)(c) or (4) may

appeal there from to the Court of Appeal in accordance with Article 138.”

In the light of the Article 154P(6) of the Constitution, appeal will lie from a

final order, judgement or sentence of a provincial High Court made in the

exercise of its jurisdiction under paragraphs (3)(b) or (3)(c) or (4) of Article

154P of the Constitution.

It is not in dispute that the impugn order is one made by the learned

Provincial High Court Judge of Embilipitiya in the exercise of its revisionary

jurisdiction vested in it under paragraphs (3)(b) of the Article 154P of the

Constitution.

Upon a plain reading of Article 154P(6) of the Constitution in conjunction with

Article 154(3)(b) of the Constitution, it becomes abundantly, clear that appeal

will lie only from a final order, judgement or sentence of a provincial High

Court made in the exercise of its jurisdiction under paragraphs (3)(b) or (3)(c)

or (4) of Article 154P of the Constitution.

Now, the pertinent question is whether the order sought to be impugned in

the instant appeal can be considered as one falling within the ambit of a “final

order, judgement or sentence” of a provincial High Court made in the exercise

of its jurisdiction under paragraphs (3)(b) or (3)(c) or (4) of Article 154P of the

Constitution as envisaged by Article 154P(6) thereof?

I would think it pertinent at this juncture to examine the criterion laid down

by a fuller bench of the Supreme Court comprising of seven judges in SC

Appeal No.41/2015 - decided on 04.08.2017 in determining whether an

order is a final judgment or not, and it may be re-produced verbatim the same

as follows;

“In order to decide whether a order is a final judgment or not. it is

my considered view that the proper approach is the approach

adopted by lord Esher in Salaman vs Warner (supra) which was cited

with approval by Lord Denning in Salter Rex vs Gosh (supra).It

stated:

“If their decision which ever way it is given, will if it stands

finally dispose of the matter in dispute, I think that for the

purpose of these Rules it is final. On the other hand, if their

decision, if given in one way, will finally dispose of the matter

in dispute , but, if given in the other, will allow the action to

go on, then I think it is not final, but interlocutory”.”

Although the said pronouncement was made by the Supreme Court under

section 754(1) and (2) of the Civil Procedure Code as amended, the criterion laid

down therein, would in my view, be applicable to a situation arisen in this case

too, with equal force in determining whether an order sought to be impugned is

a final judgment or not within the meaning of Article 154P(6) of the Constitution.

In this instance, the learned Provincial High Court Judge of Embilipitiya had in

the HC order, refused to issue notices on the Respondents named therein, on

the premise that, petition does not disclose exceptional circumstances

warranting the invocation of the extraordinary revisionary jurisdiction vested in

it by Article 154P(3)(b) of the Constitution in revision of the MC order.

Hence, it becomes abundantly, clear that the learned Provincial High Court

Judge of Embilipitiya had in the HC order, not in any manner, proceeded to

finally, determine and adjudicate upon the rights of the parties to the application

before it in revision upon its merits, instead he had merely, proceeded to refuse

to issue notices of the application on the Respondents named therein, solely, on

the premise that the application in revision had not disclosed any exceptional

circumstances warranting the invocation of the extraordinary revisionary

jurisdiction vested in it by Article 154P(3)(b) of the Constitution in revision of the

MC order.

Applying the criterion laid down by the Supreme Court in the SC Appeal

No.41/2015 (Supra) to the facts of the instant appeal, it becomes abundantly,

clear without an iota of doubt that, if the impugned decision whichever way it

given, will if it stands finally, dispose of the matter in dispute, order impugned

is final. On the other hand, if the impugned decision, if given in one way, will

finally, dispose of the matter in dispute but, if given in the order, will allow the

application to go on, then it is not final, but interlocutory.

If the impugned order, if given in one way, namely; allowing the application to go

on the premise that it discloses exceptional circumstances warranting the

invocation of the extraordinary revisionary jurisdiction vested in it by Article

154P(3)(b) of the Constitution in revision of the MC order, application will go on

to its final adjudication on its merits thereby, finally, determining the rights of

the parties thereto but, if given in the other, namely; rejecting the application

on the premise that it does not disclose exceptional circumstances warranting

the invocation of the extraordinary revisionary jurisdiction vested in it by Article

PHC/0108/22

154P(3)(b) of the Constitution in revision of the MC order, as done in the instant

application in revision by the learned High Court Judge of Embilipitiya, it will be

dismissed without going to its final adjudication on its merits.

Hence, the impugned order cannot in any manner, be considered a final order

within the meaning of by Article 154P(6) of the Constitution but, an interlocutory

order.

In the result, an appeal will not lie under Article 154P(6) of the Constitution to

this Court from the impugned order but an appeal with leave first had and

obtained from this Court in terms of the procedure laid down by section 331 of

the Criminal Procedure Code as amended to be read with section 340 thereof.

Hence, the instant appeal is misconceived in law and therefore, cannot sustain

in law. I would thus, uphold the preliminary legal objection as enumerated

above.

In the result, I would dismiss the appeal with costs.

In view of my findings with regard to the preliminary legal objection No. I) as

discussed above, I would think it not necessary to consider any further the

preliminary legal objection No. II) raised by the Respondents as enumerated

above, for; preliminary legal objection No. I which will go to the root of the instant

appeal, will finally, and effectively, decide on the fate of the instant appeal.
