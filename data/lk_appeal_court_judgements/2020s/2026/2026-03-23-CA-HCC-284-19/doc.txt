JUDGEMENT

B. Sasi Mahendran, J.

The 1st, 2nd,4th, and 5th Accused Appellants (herein after referred to as the 1st, 2nd,

4th, and 5th Appellants), along with the 3rd Accused, Nijabdeen Mohemed Rimsan,

were indicted before the High Court of Galle on the following counts,

1. On or about 2.12.2008, committed an offence which is punishable under

Section 140 of the Penal Code by being members of an unlawful assembly

with the common purpose of causing injury to Stephen Jeyawandran alias

Sinnappu.

2. Causing the death of Stephen Jeyachandran alias Sinnappu, being

members of an unlawful assembly, and committed the offence of murder

punishable under Section 296 read with Section 146 of the Penal Code.

3. Causing the death of the said Stephen Jeyachandran alias Sinnappu

which is punishable under Section 296 read with the section 32 of the

Penal Code.

At the trial, the prosecution presented evidence through five witnesses and

marking productions P1-P9 and thereafter closed its case. All the Accused, in their

defence, made dock statements.

Upon conclusion of the trial, the Learned Judge of the High Court delivered

judgment on 7th May 2019. The 1st, 2nd, 4th, and 5th appellants were found guilty

on the third count of the indictment and sentenced to death, and were acquitted

of the first and second counts. The 3rd accused was acquitted and discharged from

all three charges.

Being dissatisfied with both the conviction and the sentence imposed by the

Learned High Court Judge, the Appellants preferred an appeal before this Court,

articulating the following grounds in support of their challenge.

1. Evidence of the sole eyewitness namely sandamali (PW/3), is fraught with

serious infirmities which renders the conviction wholly unsafe.

2. Assessment of the testimonial trustworthiness of the sole eyewitness by

the trial court is deficient and flawed.

3. Trial court has flawed on the principles relating to Sec.27 recoveries

consequently causing serious prejudice to the accused appellants.

The facts and circumstances of this case are as follows,

PW 3, R.M.P.Rathnawali, the sole eyewitness of the case, who is a resident in the

same area, Status Road, where the deceased and the accused were residing. She

states that she had known the appellants and the deceased for over 15 years and

that the deceased was a friend of her brother. On 02.12.2008, at around 11.00 p.m.,

the deceased visited the residence of the witness and requested a sum of Rs. 200/-, but

shortly thereafter, upon the arrival of his wife, he departed. Later that night, PW 3 stated

that she went to a boutique to obtain a reload, where she again encountered the deceased,

who was standing across a bridge and requested money once more. In order to hand over

the money, the witness accompanied the deceased back towards her residence.

On their way she saw the 4th appellant hit the deceased's head from behind with

a hockey stick. After the blow on his head, the deceased had fallen on the ground

screaming අම්මෝ, and the 1st appellant came and attacked the deceased on his

neck with a Manna Knife. After the first appellants’s attack, the witness stated

that the 2nd and 5th , appellants, along with the 3rd, Accused, arrived carrying two

poles and a Manna knife. She testified that the 2nd and 5th Appellants assaulted

the deceased with the poles. The witness observed the incident under the presence of

light, and PW 7, who subsequently visited the scene, confirmed that there was indeed

illumination at the location.

The witness stated that when she attempted to intervene, the 4th Appellant

shouted at her to stop. PW 1 further claimed that after she rushed to the

Grandpass Police to report the incident. In her testimony, PW3 added that the

police did not record any statement on that day; instead, her statement was taken

after 14 days, as she had been hiding due to the Appellants searching for her. She

further stated that the accused attacked and caused damage to her house.

The Learned High Court Judge observed the opinion formed by the JMO injuries

were inflicted by the Appellants. I note that the Learned High Court Judge has

correctly evaluated the evidence of PW 3, which was corroborated by the doctor’s

testimony, PW 11.

The principal grievance advanced by the defence was that the evidence of PW3

should be rejected on the ground that her statement to the police was made after

a delay of 14 days. In addressing this contention, it is necessary to consider

whether the testimony of a witness who comes forward belatedly can be accepted.

Our courts have held that while delay may raise concerns regarding credibility, it

does not automatically render the testimony inadmissible. The Court must

evaluate whether the delay has been satisfactorily explained and whether the

explanation is justifiable with the circumstances of the case.

At this juncture, reference must be made to judicial decisions that have examined

the admissibility and reliability of delayed statements.

In Sumanasena Vs Attorney General [1999] 3 SLR 137 at page 140, His Lordship

Justice Jayasuriya held as follows.

"just because the witness is a belated witness the Court ought not to reject

his testimony on that score alone and that a Court must inquire into the

reason for the delay and if the reason for the delay is plausible and

justifiable the Court could act on the evidence of a belated witness.”

Page 8 of 12

In Ajith Samarakoon Vs the Republic [2004] 2 SLR 209 at page 220, His Lordship

Justice Jayasuriya held as follows.

“Just because the statement of a witness is belated the Court is not entitled

to reject such testimony. In applying the Test of Spontaneity the Test of

Contemporaneity and the Test of Promptness the Court ought to

scrupulously proceed to examine the reasons for the delay. If the reasons

for the delay adduced by the witness are justifiable and probable the trial

Judge is entitled to act on the evidence of a witness who had made a belated

statement.”

In the case of Dhannasiri vs. The Republic of Sri Lanka 2012 (1) SLR 268

Tilakawardane, J held inter alia;

"Two critical tests before considering belated evidence as reliable " evidence

are: firstly reasons for delay and secondly, whether those reasons are

justifiable."

Further in the case of Dharmasiri v Republic of Sri Lanka 2010 (2) SLR 241, Sisira

de Abrew, J held inter alia;

"Because the witness is a belated witness, Court ought not to reject his

testimony on that score alone. Court must inquire into the reason for the

delay and if the reason for the delay is plausible and justifiable, the Court

could act on the evidence of the belated witness."

Further, in the case of Udagam Vs. Attorney General 2000 (2) SLR 103 relied by

the learned Counsel for the Appellant it was held that;

“Evidence is infirm, unsafe and unreliable to act upon considering the

following;

(i) the belated statement made to the Police with delay not explained with

acceptable reasons.”

Page 9 of 12

According to the above judicial literature, when assessing the evidentiary value of

the belated statement made by PW3 to the police, this Court must first consider

the reasons for the delay, and secondly, whether such reasons are legally and

factually justifiable. The testimony of the witness demonstrates that the delay in

PW3’ s statement was occasioned by fear for her personal safety. Learned Counsel

for the Appellant argued that PW3 had made her statement to the police only after

a lapse of 14 days from the incident. While the delay is undeniable, the explanation

provided that PW3 refrained from immediately implicating the Appellant due to

apprehension for her own security appears credible.

On the perusal of the judgment of the learned Trial Judge, it is apparent that

adequate consideration has been given to the contradictions and omissions

highlighted by the defence, and the finding was rightly made that they do not go

to the root of the evidence of PW 3. Although PW 3 did not make a spontaneous

statement to the police, there is no reason to reject her testimony. An analysis of

her evidence leads to the conclusion that she has not fabricated the story, as her

version is corroborated by the medical evidence, which is independent and reliable.

Also, I note that the learned High Court Judge has correctly disregarded the

appellant's version and has given the reasons for that. Perusal of the dock

statements of the appellants it reveale a brief denial of involvement. There is no

specific plea of evidence, such as alibi, which has been raised by any of the

appellant. PW 3 has stated how these accused were involved with the murder. In

the light of the cogent incrimination evidence adduced by the prosecution, there is

no attempt to explain.

For the above said reasons, I hold that there was sufficient material for the learned

trial judge to come to the finding that all four accused were acting in furtherance

of a common murderous intention to murder Stephen Jeyawandran alias

Sinnappu.

I am also mindful that the appellants did not dispute the fact that they attacked

PW 3’s house, and that they absconded from the police for 14 days without offering

any explanation. I hold that the prosecution has proved the case beyond

Page 12 of 12

reasonable doubt, the simultaneous participation of all four accused. The learned

High Court Judge has correctly identified the role of each accused separately and

concluded that they all shared a common intention to commit the offence.

Considering these factors, I am of the view that none of the grounds of appeal

presented by the appellant's counsel warrant interference with the findings of the

Learned High Court Judge. The judgment dated 7 May 2019 is upheld, and the

conviction, along with the sentence, remains in effect.

The appeal is dismissed.
