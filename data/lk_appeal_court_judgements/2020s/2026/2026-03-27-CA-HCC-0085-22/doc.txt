Judgment Amal Ranaraja. J,

1. The accused-appellant (hereinafter referred to as the appellant) has

been indicted in High Court of Colombo in High Court Case No. HCB

2059/14. The charges in the indictment are as follows;

01. On or around the 03rd of February, 2012, within the jurisdiction

of this Court, in Thalathuoya, you as a public servant, the

Chairman of the Pathahewaheta Pradeshiya Sabawa, solicited a

gratification of LKR 25,000.00 from Subrameniyam Puvanendru

Raja, in order to be deterred from invalidating the license relating

to the application, bearing No. 15777, for a license to sell fish

from a mobile vehicle issued for the year 2012, by the

Pathahewaheta Pradeshiya Sabawa to one Osaka Olivia

Nanayakkara. Thereby, you have committed an offence

punishable under Section 19(b) of the Bribery Act.

02. In the time, place, and course of action of the above first charge,

you as a public servant, the Chairman of the Pathahewaheta

Pradeshiya Sabawa, by soliciting a sum of LKR 25,000.00 from

Subrameniyam Puvanendru Raja, have committed an offence

punishable under Section 19(c) of the Bribery Act (as amended).

03. On or around the 03rd of February, 2012, within the jurisdiction

of this Court, in Thalathuoya, you as a public servant, the

Chairman of the Pathahewaheta Pradeshiya Sabawa, accepted a

gratification of LKR 25,000.00 from Subrameniyam Puvanendru

Raja, in order to be deterred from invalidating the license relating

to the application, bearing No. 15777, for a license to sell fish

from a mobile vehicle issued for the year 2012, by the

Pathahewaheta Pradeshiya Sabawa to one Osaka Olivia

Nanayakkara. Thereby, you have committed an offence

punishable under Section 19(b) of the Bribery Act.

04. In the time, place, and course of action of the above third charge,

you as a public servant, the Chairman of the Pathahewaheta

Pradeshiya Sabawa, by soliciting a sum of LKR 25,000.00 from

Subrameniyam Puvanendru Raja, have committed an offence

punishable under Section 19(c) of the Bribery Act (as amended).

2. At the conclusion of the trial the Learned High Court Judge has

convicted the appellant of the second and fourth charges and acquitted

him of the first and third charge. Upon conviction the appellant has

been sentenced as follows;

i. Fine of Rs. 5000/- each in respect of the second and fourth

charges with a term of 1 year simple imprisonment each in

default. Further, has also imposed a term of three years rigorous

imprisonment each, with such terms of imprisonment to run

concurrently. The appellant has also been directed to pay a sum

of Rs. 25,000 as a fine as per the terms in Section 26 of the

Bribery Act, with a term of 1 year simple imprisonment in default.

3. The appellant being aggrieved by the conviction, disputed judgement

together with the sentencing order has preferred the instant appeal t0

this Court.

Case of Prosecution

4. PW01 a mobile fishmonger has been operating from a lorry in the

Marassana town, when he had been approached by the appellant, the

chairman of the Pathahewaheta Pradeshiya Saba, on the day of the

incident. The appellant has questioned PW01 about his business

operations and whether PW01 possessed a valid license.

5. PW01 has informed the appellant that the business operated under a

license obtained by his wife. Subsequently, the appellant has allegedly

threatened to revoke the license if PW01 did not comply with his

demand (solicitation) for a sum of LKR 25,000.00.

6. Following this PW01 has made a complaint to the Commission to

Investigate Allegations of Bribery or Corruption (CIABOC). CIABOC

officers have then conducted a raid resulting in the appellants arrest

upon accepting the demanded sum of LKR 25,000.00 from PW01.

Case of the Appellant

7. The appellant has asserted that he held the position of chairman of the

specific Pradeshiya Saba during the period in issue. That while enroute

to work, he has observed PW01 engaged in the occupation of a mobile

fishmonger. Upon inquiry the appellant has discovered that PW01 was

conducting this business without a license. Consequently, he has

cautioned PW01 against continuing such an unlicensed activity. The

appellant has denied soliciting any gratification from PW01 and has

maintained that he has been falsely implicated in the matter.

8. The learned PC appearing on behalf of the appellant has asserted the

circumstances outlined in the charges pertain to an alleged threat to

cancel a license. However, given the High Court’s prior finding that

such a license was never actually issued, the learned PC maintains that

the appellant cannot be convicted of the second and fourth charges

either. This contention rests on the argument that there can be no

engagement by an individual acting as a public servant in the

cancellation of a license that does not exist.

9. Section 19(c) of the Bribery Act No. 11 of 1954 (As Amended) is as

follows;

19. A person –

(c) who, being a public officer, solicits or accepts any

gratification shall be guilty of an offence punishable with rigorous

imprisonment for a term of not more than seven years and a fine

not exceeding five thousand rupees:

Provided, however, that it shall not be an offence for a public

officer to solicit or accept any gratification which he is authorized

by law or the terms of his employment to receive;

Provided further that section 35 of the Medical Ordinance shall

not entitle a medical practitioner who is a public officer to solicit

or accept any gratification.

10. Mr. R. K. W. Goonesekere in his book “BRIBERY A STUDY IN

LAWMAKING AND OF THE CRIMINAL PROCESS”, has discussed the

application of the provisions in section 19(c) of the Bribery Act detailing

it as follows;

“Taken literally the prohibition here stated on accepting a gratification,

as defined in the Act is almost absolute and can cover innocent presents.

TS Fernando J in Karunaratne v Queen (1966) 69 NLR 10, was

somewhat troubled by the wide sweep of the acts which would become

criminal and attempted to control the operation of the clause by

holding that the word ‘gratification’ throughout the Act carries with it

a sinister and not innocent connotation. He added,

“If the words ‘any gratification which he is not authorised by law or

the terms of his employment to receive’ are given the widest possible

interpretation of which they are capable, then a public servant who

accepts a personal gift from a friend, relative or neighbour, or for

that matter a birthday gift from his wife, would be guilty of an

offence under the Act. It would be absurd to have to reduce oneself to

the position that such gifts are within the mischief which the Act was

designed to punish.”

Here is an echo of the reasoning which swayed the framers of the Draft

Code not to have a law against taking of presents, more than 100 years

earlier. What Fernando J thought was penalised by s.19 (c) was

soliciting or accepting a gratification other than legal remuneration.

By way of further explanation, he said:

“The mischief which this part of the section was designed to prevent

is that of soliciting or accepting a gratification when acting as a

public servant in his capacity of a public servant upon the

performance of his duties as such public servant.”

11. Also, a general rule of law, the burden of establishing that the proviso to

a law applies to a given situation lies on the party seeking to rely on that

proviso.

12. While the prosecution bears the initial burden of proving that an offence

was committed, or a right was violated, the proviso acts as a special

exemption, exception, or qualification to the main rule. Therefore, the

person seeking to escape liability or gain a benefit from that exception

must prove that they fall within its scope.

13. He who alleges must prove; the party asserting a special, favourable or

exceptional circumstance (the proviso) has the obligation to produce

evidence of it. If the facts required to prove the proviso are specifically

within the knowledge of one party, that party bears the burden of proving

them. Further a proviso is generally read inrelation to the main clause to

which it is a proviso. It is not a separate, independent clause, but a

qualification of the main rule.

14. In these circumstances, the prosecution would need to establish that an

appellant (accused), while acting as a public servant in the performance

of his duties solicited and accepted the gratification. Conversely, the

appellant (the accused), would then have to demonstrate that the specific

conditions outlined in the proviso in s. 19 (c) apply to his situation.

15. PW05, Mrs. R.M.P Rathnayake, who served as the secretary of the

Pathehewaheta Pradeshiya Saba during the relevant period has testified

that the appellant was elected as the Chairman of the Pradeshiya Saba

in March 2011. Furthermore, PW05 has identified an application

(marked පැ 01) that has been submitted to the Pradeshiya Saba by the

wife of PW01. By this application, she has sought a license to operate a

mobile fish trading business in the year 2012. PW05 has also identified

the corresponding recommendation (marked පැ 02) to issue the requested

license. She has also testified that she, as the secretary, was not

authorised to sign the license, but the appellant as the Chairman was

authorised to do so.

16. It is clear that neither PW01, nor his wife have possessed a license to

operate a mobile fish trading business when PW01 was confronted by the

appellant as detailed in the charges. However, an application for such a

license had been submitted and duly processed. The appellant’s

signature was the only remaining requirement for the license to be

issued. Given these circumstances it could be inferred that the appellant

referenced to such pending license, as well as solicited and accepted, as

a gratification, the sum of money in exchange for affixing his signature.

17. Accordingly, the prosecution has established that the appellant in his

capacity as a public servant solicited and accepted a gratification of LKR

25,000.00 to perform a duty as such public servant.

18. Furthermore, although the incident mentioned in the charges is not

detailed, it has not misled the appellant. This becomes evident when the

court delves into circumstances that can inferred from the HC

proceedings. This allowance is provided for in Section 166 of the Code of

Criminal Procedure Act No. 15 of 1979 as follows;

166. Any error in stating either the offence or the particulars

required to be stated in the charge and any omission to state the

offence or those particulars shall not be regarded at any stage of

the case as material, unless the accused was misled by such error

or omission.

19. In those circumstances I am disinclined to interfere with the conviction,

disputed judgement and the sentencing order and proceed to set aside

the same.

20. I dismiss the appeal.

I make no order regarding costs.

Appeal dismissed.
