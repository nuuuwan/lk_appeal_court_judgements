Judgment

1. The Accused-Appellant (hereinafter referred to as “the Appellant”) has preferred the

present appeal against the judgment and sentence dated 19.03.2025 delivered by the

Learned High Court Judge of Kalutara.

2. The Appellant was indicted before the High Court of Kalutara for the murder of

Bamunusinghe Arachchige Sarachandra, alias Harischandra, an offence punishable

under Section 296 of the Penal Code. As opted by the Appellant, the trial was conducted

before the Learned High Court Judge sitting without a jury. At the conclusion of the

trial, the Learned Trial Judge found the Appellant guilty of the charge of murder and

convicted him accordingly. The Appellant was thereafter sentenced to death.

3. Being aggrieved by the said conviction and sentence, the Appellant has preferred the

present appeal. Although the Appellant advanced three grounds of appeal, the principal

contention urged at the hearing was that the evidence adduced by the prosecution was

insufficient to establish the offence of murder. The defense argued that, even looking at

the evidence in the strongest possible light, it only proved culpable homicide not

amounting to murder. Therefore, the Appellant should have been convicted of that

lesser offense under the "sudden fight" exception (Exception 4 to Section 294 of the

Penal Code).

4. The first witness to testify for the prosecution was PW1, Ransaluge Ajith. According to

PW1, on the day of the incident, as the deceased was proceeding towards the

Appellant’s line room, the Appellant was being restrained by his mother and sister.

mother and sister, who were warning him not to quarrel. This circumstance strongly

suggests that a commotion or altercation was already underway at the time of the

incident. Such evidence is relevant to assessing whether the events unfolded during a

sudden confrontation or quarrel between the parties.

9. When the deceased fell to the ground after receiving the blow, PW3 stated that he ran

to fetch a glass of water, but was also slapped by the Appellant. However, it is further

stated that the Appellant later apologized to PW3, stating that the slap had been given

by mistake. It is also in evidence that the attack took place in front of the Appellant’s

line room, when the deceased was running towards the Appellant while armed with an

axe.

10. PW2, Muttiah Ramaiya, also testified for the prosecution. Although PW2 did not

witness the deceased being struck by the Appellant, his evidence nonetheless confirms

that there was a commotion at the relevant time.

11. PW10, Pragathi Lakmini Abeysinghe, was the Officer-in-Charge of Bulathsinhala

Police Station at the relevant time. Upon receiving information, the witness proceeded

to the scene with several officers to conduct the investigation. According to PW10’s

observations, the incident had taken place in front of the line room occupied by the

Appellant’s mother. Furthermore, PW10 recovered an axe near the latrine of the said

line room. The Appellant was also arrested by PW10 and his officers. At the time of

arrest, the Appellant was drunk, naked, and had injuries on his body, and was also

unable to stand properly.

12. According to the evidence of the Judicial Medical Officer, the deceased died two days

after admission to hospital. The deceased had suffered a skull fracture, resulting in an

intracranial haemorrhage. The skull fracture was caused by severe blunt force trauma,

while the other injuries may have been caused due to a fall.

13. Evidently, the deceased was armed with an axe and was advancing toward the

Appellant, at a time when the Appellant was being restrained by his mother and sister.

There is no evidence that the Appellant was armed with a club at that stage. On the

contrary, the evidence indicates that he picked up the club from the vicinity and

thereafter struck the deceased. It is also apparent from the evidence that the deceased

approached the Appellant with the axe during a commotion or altercation. These

circumstances demonstrate that the Appellant had reasonable grounds to apprehend

that, unless he reacted promptly, he was at risk of suffering grievous bodily harm or

even death. Accordingly, the Appellant’s conduct must be assessed in the context of the

imminent threat which he reasonably perceived at the time of the incident.

14. The foregoing factual circumstances necessarily give rise to the question whether the

Appellant acted in the exercise of his right of private defence, notwithstanding the fact

that such a defence was not expressly advanced at the trial.

15. It is also significant to emphasize that, at the trial, Counsel for the Appellant did not

expressly raise any defence based on the general exception stipulated in Section 294 of

the Penal Code. However, where the evidence adduced at trial discloses the availability

of such a defence, the Trial Judge is obliged to take it into consideration. In this regard,

I would like to refer to the case of King vs. Albert Appuhamy, 40 NLR 505, in which

it was held;

“Failure on the part of a prisoner or his counsel to take up a certain line of defence

does not relieve a judge of the responsibility of putting to the jury such defence if it

arises on the evidence.”

16. Similarly, in The King vs. Bellana Vithanage Edvin 41 NLR 345, the court held that;

“Though the accused has not taken up the defence of intoxication if such defence

arises on the evidence, it is the duty of the jury to consider the same.”

17. In Jayathilaka vs The A.G. [2003] 1 SLR 107, the court held that;

“Though the accused had not taken up the defence of intoxication if such

defence arises on the evidence, it is the duty of the jury to consider the same.”

18. Thus, in light of the aforementioned authorities, I shall consider whether the evidence

adduced at trial discloses the availability of any defence under the general exceptions

stipulated in Section 294 of the Penal Code. Since there is evidence that the Appellant

struck the deceased while the latter was approaching him armed with an axe, it is

incumbent upon this Court to consider whether the Appellant is entitled to invoke the

right of private defence.

19. The evidence disclosed that the Appellant was unarmed when the deceased advanced

towards him while armed with an axe. In these circumstances, the Appellant had

reasonable grounds to apprehend an imminent threat of death or grievous bodily harm.

Consequently, and in response to that apprehended danger, the Appellant acted

promptly by striking the deceased with a nearby club in order to protect himself, and

therefore claims entitlement to the exception under Section 89 of the Penal Code; it is

necessary to consider the legal principles governing the applicability of the right of

private defence.

20. Section 89 of the Penal Code defines the right of private defence as follows;

“Nothing is an offence which is done in the exercise of the right of private defence.”

21. According to G.L. Peiris, in his work “General Principles of Criminal Liability in

Ceylon”, the exercise of the right of private defence is subject to certain limitations,

which he identifies as follows;

I. The right is available only in the event of an offence being committed.

II. The right of private defence may be exercised only in respect of specified

categories of offences.

III. The nature and degree of the harm which may be inflicted lawfully in exercise

of the right of private defence in relation to the harm against which the person

invoking the right is seeking to defend himself or another.

IV. There is no right of private defence in cases in which there is time to have

recourse to the protection of the public authorities and the right of private

defence in no case extends to the inflicting of more harm than it is necessary to

inflict for the purpose of defence.

22. Accordingly, the right of private defence is available only against an act or omission

constituting an offence punishable under the Penal Code or by any law other than the

CA/HCC/0215/2025

7 | P a g e

Penal Code. Further, the exercise of such right is confined to the categories of offences

expressly stipulated in Section 90 of the Penal Code.

Section 90 of the Penal Code reads as follows;

Every person has a right, subject to the restrictions contained in Section 92, to

defend:

Firstly – his own body, and the body of any other person, against any offence

affecting the human body;

Secondly – the property, whether movable or immovable, of himself or of any other

person, against any act which is an offence falling under the definition of theft,

robbery, mischief, or criminal trespass, or which is an attempt to commit theft,

robbery, mischief, or criminal trespass.

23. It should be noted that the voluntary infliction of any harm, including the causing of

death, upon the assailant is permissible in the exercise of the right of private defence

where the offence giving rise to such right falls within any of the six categories of

offences specified in Section 93 of the Penal Code.

I. An assault which may reasonably cause the apprehension that death will

otherwise be the consequence of the assault;

II. An assault which may reasonably cause the apprehension that grievous hurt will

otherwise be the consequence of the assault;

III. An assault with the intention of committing rape;

IV. An assault with the intention of gratifying unnatural lust;

V. An assault with the intention of kidnapping or abducting;

VI. An assault with the intention of wrongfully confining a person under

circumstances which may reasonably cause him to apprehend that he will be

unable to have recourse to the public authorities for his release.

24. According to that Section, the right of private defence of the body commences as soon

as a reasonable apprehension of danger to the body arises from an attempt or threat to

commit the offence may not have been committed, and such right continues so long as

such apprehension of danger to the body continues. The threat, however, must

reasonably give rise to present and imminent, and not remote or distant, danger. This

right rests on the general principle that where a crime is endeavored to be committed

by force, it is lawful to repel that force in self-defence, provided that all other statutory

requirements governing the exercise of that right are satisfied.

25. It is trite law that the right to private defence cannot be used to kill the wrongdoer,

unless the person concerned has a reasonable cause to fear that otherwise death or

grievous hurt might ensue.

26. In the present case, the Appellant was charged with the offence of murder. The evidence

suggests that the deceased attempted to assault the Appellant with an axe, a weapon

capable of causing death or grievous hurt. In such circumstances, the Appellant was

entitled to invoke the right of private defence for the protection of his own body. Where

a person is confronted with an imminent attack by an assailant armed with an axe, it

would be reasonable for that person to use such force as is necessary to avert the danger.

Accordingly, the act of the Appellant in striking the deceased with a club while repelling

the attack is capable of falling within the ambit of the right of private defence, subject

to an assessment of whether the force used was necessary and proportionate to the threat

faced.

27. The following authorities are relevant and provide guidance in determining whether the

legitimate limits of the right of private defence have been exceeded in the circumstances

of the present case.

28. In Tissera vs. Edwin (1929) 30 NLR 408, the court held as follows;

“Under Section 97 of the Penal Code the accused has, in the exercise of the right

of private defence, the right to cause any harm used is subject to the restriction

contained in Section 92, clause 4, of the Penal Code, that is to say, the harm must

be proportionate to the purpose for which the right of private defence is exercised.”

(at page 409)

“What the court has to consider is not the weapon used but the injury inflicted. The

injuries inflicted here were non-grievous, and the fact that they were caused with a

gun does not take away the right the accused had of causing hurt to the complainant

to prevent him from taking away the nuts or till he was arrested.” (at page 410)

29. In Jansz vs. Appuhamy (1905) 6 Tam. 6, held as follows;

“The right of private defence of the body does not arise where a man armed with a

gun sees at night, on property which he is set to watch, a thief armed with a knife,

when the thief shows no intention of using it for the purpose of resistance or attack.

However, it was said, where a thief is seen descending a tree with coconuts, the

watcher, having no time to have recourse to the public authorities, is justified in

firing at the thief so as to prevent the thief’s escape with the stolen property.”

30. According to the above authorities, the courts have consistently emphasized that the

right of private defence must be assessed on the basis of necessity and proportionality,

rather than the mere nature of the weapon used, and that such right may extend even to

the infliction of serious harm where there is no time to seek assistance from public

authorities and the threat is immediate.

31. However, the right of private defence is subject to important limitations. It does not

arise in circumstances where there is sufficient time to seek the protection of the public

authorities. Furthermore, the right extends only to such force as is reasonably necessary

for the purpose of defence; in no event authorizes the infliction of more harm than is

necessary to repel the attack. The exercise of the right must therefore be proportionate

to the nature and gravity of the apprehended danger.

32. In the present case, the Appellant had no reasonable opportunity to have recourse to the

protection of the public authorities. According to the evidence of PW10, the distance

between the locus of the offence and the Police Station is approximately 10 -12 km. In

these circumstances, having regard to the distance involved and the immediacy of the

attack, it cannot be reasonably expected that the Appellant could seek timely assistance

from the public authorities. Accordingly, this factor supports the Appellant’s plea of

private defence.

33. Further, according to the observations of the Judicial Medical Officer recorded at the

post-mortem examination, the deceased had sustained a single blow to the head and

succumbed to his injuries two days after the assault. The medical evidence is consistent

with the inference that the Appellant did not repeatedly attack the deceased. Rather, it

suggests that the force used by the Appellant was confined to what he considered

necessary to avert the imminent threat posed by the deceased. It therefore supports the

conclusion that the force used was not disproportionate to the threat faced.

34. Moreover, it must be emphasized that, in a situation of this nature, the Appellant had

little or no opportunity to weigh with precision the exact degree of force necessary to

repel the attack or neutralize the assailant. The law does not expect a person confronted

with an imminent threat to calculate, with mathematical precision, the quantum of force

required for his defence. The suddenness of the events and the urgency of the situation

must be taken into account when assessing the reasonableness of the Appellant's

response. In this regard, the following judgment of the Supreme Court of India provides

valuable guidance.

35. In Buta Singh vs. The State of Punjab (1991) 2 SCC 612, the court noted that a person

who is apprehending death or bodily injury cannot weigh in golden scales in the spur

of the moment and in the heat of circumstances, the number of injuries required to

disarm the assailants who were armed with weapons. In moments of excitement and

disturbed mental equilibrium, it is often difficult to expect the parties to preserve

composure and use exactly only so much force in retaliation commensurate with the

danger apprehended to him; where assault is imminent by use of force, it would be

lawful to repel the force in self-defence, and the right of private defence commences as

soon as the threat becomes so imminent. Such situations have to be pragmatically

viewed and not with high-powered spectacles or microscopes to detect slight or even

marginal overstepping. Due weightage has to be given to, and a hyper-technical

approach has to be avoided in considering what happens on the spur of the moment on

the spot and keeping in view normal human reaction and conduct, where self-

preservation is the paramount consideration. But, if the fact situation shows that in the

guise of self- preservation, what really has been done is to assault the original aggressor,

even after the cause of reasonable apprehension has disappeared, the plea

of right of private defence can legitimately be negative. The court dealing with the plea

has to weigh the material to conclude whether the plea is acceptable. It is essentially, as

noted above, a finding of fact.

36. In considering the commencement and duration of the right of private defence, it should

be noted that the right of private defence of the body commences as soon as a reasonable

apprehension of danger to the body is formed from an attempt or threat to commit the

offence, even if the offence has not yet been committed. Such right continues as long

as such apprehension of danger to the body persists, as stipulated in Section 95 of the

Penal Code.

37. In Deo Narain vs. The State of U.P., (1973) 3 SCR 57, the Supreme Court of India

stated as follows;

“………. What the High Court really seems to have missed is the provision of law

embodied in Section 102 of the IPC. According to that section the right of private

defence of the body commences as soon as a reasonable apprehension of danger to

the body arises from an attempt or threat to commit the offence, though the offence

may not have been committed, and such right continues so long as such

apprehension of danger to the body continues. The threat, however, must reasonably

give rise to the present and imminent, and not remote or distant, danger. This right

rests on the general principle that where a crime is endeavoured to be committed

by force, it is lawful to repel that force in self-defence.”

38. In this regard, a reasonable expectation of danger is sufficient, and the apprehension of

danger may arise from an attempt or a threat. However, it is equally well settled that an

accused cannot justify the commencement of the exercise of the right of private defence

merely on the basis of a subjective belief of imminent danger. The test is an objective

one; namely, whether a reasonable person placed in the circumstances of the accused

would have had adequate grounds to believe that an attack was imminent.

39. In the present case, the evidence of PW3 clearly demonstrates that the deceased was

assaulted by the Appellant when the deceased approached him while armed with an axe.

Furthermore, it is evident that the Appellant was not pre-armed; rather, he picked up a

club from the vicinity and struck the deceased with it.

40. The evidence of PW1 also confirms that, when the Appellant was being restrained by

his mother and sister, he was unarmed. It was only after he broke free from their

restraint that he picked up a club and assaulted the deceased. The evidence of PW3

clearly establishes that the deceased was struck with a club by the Appellant when the

deceased was approaching him armed with an axe. Thus, it is evident that the Appellant

acted without any premeditation, but in the exercise of the right of private defence.

These circumstances, taken in their entirety, indicate that the Appellant acted

spontaneously in response to an imminent threat, without premeditation, and in exercise

of his right of private defence.

41. In Perera (1889) 9 S.C.C I, the accused, with a gun in his hand, was walking in his

garden at night, watching his fruit. He observed a man running away and a ladder

standing against one of his coconut trees. He pushed the ladder away and, discharging

the barrel of his gun in the air, called out to the deceased who was on the tree, to come

down. The deceased, without replying, took a knife in his hand and began to descend

the tree, when the accused fired and hit him in the foot. The accused was charged with

voluntarily causing grievous hurt to the deceased. It was held that the accused had acted

in the exercise of the right of private defence, and he was entitled to be acquitted.

42. The decision is defensible on the ground that the act of reasonable apprehension in the

accused of death or of grievous hurt, so that the firing of the shot amounted to a

legitimate exercise of the right of private defence.

43. The principle emerging from this decision is that where an imminent threat exists and

the accused acts in reasonable response to such threat, the right of private defence may

lawfully justify the resulting harm.

44. In relation to the continuance of the right of private defence, it is well established that

where there is a reasonable apprehension of danger to the body is anticipated, the right

may be exercised as long as such apprehension of danger to the body persists. Thus,

once the assailant is rendered incapable of causing further harm, for instance by being

struck unconscious, any subsequent use of force cannot be justified under the plea of

private defence, as no imminent danger would then exist. Acts done after the cessation

of danger, particularly those prompted by vindictiveness or revenge, fall outside the

protective scope of the said right.

45. In the present case, the evidence of PW3 indicates that the deceased was running

towards the Appellant’s line room armed with an axe when the Appellant struck him

with a club obtained from the vicinity and thereafter fled from the scene. These

circumstances corroborate that the Appellant acted in response to a reasonable

apprehension of imminent danger and without premeditation. The evidence further

suggested that the Appellant used a single blow to neutralize the immediate threat, and

once the deceased became incapacitated, the Appellant ceased further action and left

the scene. This conduct is inconsistent with any revengeful or vindictive motive after

the cessation of danger, and instead supports the conclusion that the Appellant acted

within the limits of the right of private defence.

46. In considering the pleading of the right of private defence, while the burden of proving

the applicability of an exception generally rests upon the accused, the evidence adduced

by the prosecution in the present case itself suggests that the Appellant acted in the

exercise of his right of private defence. The facts and surrounding circumstances

emerging from the evidence are more consistent with the exercise of that right than with

a case of sudden fight.

47. Accordingly, once the prosecution evidence itself gives rise to a reasonable inference

that the Appellant acted in self-defence, no further burden rests upon the Appellant to

adduce additional evidence in support thereof. The issue must therefore be determined

on the totality of the evidence already before the court.

48. In The King vs. Kirinelis, (1946) 47 NLR 443, where in a trial for murder, the accused’s

defence was that he acted under grave and sudden provocation, but the trial judge went

further and put before the jury the defence that the accused was acting in the exercise

of the right of private defence.

49. In James Martin vs. State of Kerala AIRONLINE 2003 SC 829, held that;

“Under Section 105 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (in short 'the Evidence Act'),

the burden of proof is on the accused, who sets up the plea of self-defence, and, in

the absence of proof, it is not possible for the Court to presume the truth of the plea

of self-defence. The Court shall presume the absence of such circumstances. It is

for the accused to place necessary material on record either by himself adducing

positive evidence or by eliciting necessary facts from the witnesses examined for the

prosecution. An accused taking the plea of the right of private defence is not

necessarily required to call evidence; he can establish his plea by reference to

circumstances transpiring from the prosecution evidence itself. The question in such

a case would be a question of assessing the true effect of the prosecution evidence,

and not a question of the accused discharging any burden. Where

the right of private defence is pleaded, the defence must be a reasonable and

probable version satisfying the Court that the harm caused by the accused was

necessary for either warding off the attack or for forestalling the further reasonable

apprehension from the side of the accused. The burden of establishing the plea of

self-defence is on the accused and the burden stands discharged by showing

preponderance of probabilities in favour of that plea on the basis of the material on

record.”

50. In the present case, it is to be noted that the Appellant did not take up the defence of

right of private defence at the trial. Nonetheless, if the evidence adduced is suggestive

that the act complained of occurred in circumstances consistent with the exercise of the

right of private defence, the Court is nonetheless duty bound to consider such a

possibility in determining the guilt or otherwise of the Appellant.

51. Accordingly, when the surrounding circumstances leading up to the incident are

considered in their entirety, they do not disclose a premeditated attack by the Appellant.

Rather, the evidence suggested that the Appellant acted in response to an imminent

threat posed by the deceased, and in the exercise of his right of private defence for the

purpose of protecting himself. There is also nothing in the evidence to suggest that the

Appellant acted in a cruel, excessive or unusual manner. On the contrary, the evidence

indicates that he fled the scene immediately after delivering a single blow.

52. Foregoing evidence on record is capable of supporting the inference that the Appellant

acted in response to an imminent threat posed by the deceased rather than pursuant to

any premeditated intention to cause his death.

53. Further, it is also pertinent to observe that the learned Trial Judge erred in concluding

that the evidence of PW1, PW3, and PW2 mutually corroborated each other. The

evidence of PW1 relating to the assault and the manner in which the deceased

approached the scene stands contradicted, if not negated, by the testimony of PW3.

Accordingly, it is incorrect to hold that their evidence is mutually corroborative.

54. In the aforesaid circumstances, I conclude that it is not safe to affirm the conviction

against the Appellant.
