Judgment

1. The case giving rise to this appeal was tried before the High Court of Hambantota by a

Judge sitting without a jury. The Attorney-General initially indicted 21 accused on

charges arising out of the death of Wijesingha Kankanamge Nandathissa and the

injuries caused to Wijesingha Kankanamge Jayatissa, Abewikrama Kankanamge

Emalihamy, and Wijesingha Kankanamge Ajith Priyantha. The indictment alleged that

the offences were committed by the accused as members of an unlawful assembly and,

in the alternative, in furtherance of a common intention.

2. At the conclusion of the trial, the Learned Trial Judge acquitted the remaining accused

of all charges. However, the 5th, 6th, 10th, 13th, 15th, and 21st accused were convicted on

the 1st and 2nd counts, namely, for being members of an unlawful assembly and

committing the murder of Wijesingha Kankanamge Nandatissa. In addition, the 5th and

6th accused were convicted under Section 314 of the Penal Code for causing hurt to

Abewikrama Kankanamge Emalihamy.

3. Being aggrieved by the said convictions and sentences, the 5th, 6th, 10th, 13th, 15th, and

21st accused (hereinafter referred to as "the Appellants") preferred the present appeal.

During the pendency of the appeal, the 5th Appellant died.

4. The grounds of appeal urged on behalf of the Accused-Appellants, several of which are

common to all, may be summarized as follows;

a. Prosecution witness No. 5 failed the test of credibility, and the Learned Trial

Judge has failed to appreciate the deficiencies of the testimony of the PW5 as it

failed the test of consistency as well;

b. Whether the prosecution has proved that the 21st Accused-Appellant was a

member of the unlawful assembly; and,

35. Accordingly, it is significant that PW5 lacks credibility and his evidence fails to satisfy

the test of consistency when compared with the testimonies of other prosecution

witnesses. The material contradictions and omissions revealed in his evidence, when

read in conjunction with the inconsistencies between his version and that of PW2, PW7,

and other witnesses, seriously undermine the reliability of his account.

36. E.R.S.R. Coomaraswamy, in his work ‘The Law of Evidence’, has described how a

Court should evaluate contradictions and omissions;

“ …. Another test that is applied in the evaluation of evidence is the test of

inconsistency, contradictions per se and discrepancies in the evidence of the

witness. A witness is often contradicted by his statements to the police under section

110 of the Code, 5 or by his depositions in the magistrate’s court. Some may be

positive contradictions, while others may be omissions, which may be of material

facts or immaterial facts. In evaluating discrepancies, contradictions and

omissions, it is undesirable for a court to pick out sentences and consider them in

isolation from the rest of the statements. The entire statement should be taken into

consideration to ascertain whether they are due to a deliberate attempt to suppress

or depart from the truth. Witnesses should not be disbelieved on the basis of trifling

discrepancies and omissions”

37. Further, it is noteworthy that a serious doubt arises as to whether PW5, who is projected

by the prosecution as the sole eyewitness to the incident, was in fact in a position to

witness the events as he has narrated in his evidence. If PW5 was not present throughout

the material time, the safety of relying upon his testimony to sustain a conviction against

the Appellants is gravely undermined. In such circumstances, his evidence cannot be

treated as wholly reliable or sufficient, and it would be unsafe to found convictions

against the Appellants solely or substantially upon his testimony in the absence of

independent corroboration.

38. To elaborate further, where material witnesses give inconsistent evidence on material

particulars, their testimony loses its probative value and cannot safely be relied upon.

Such inconsistencies inevitably cast serious doubt on the credibility of the prosecution

case.

39. Further, it was contended that the prosecution has failed to establish, beyond reasonable

doubt, that the 21st Accused-Appellant was a member of the alleged unlawful assembly.

40. In this regard, it is significant that only PW5 made reference to the presence of the 21st

Accused-Appellant and attributed to him participation in the assault on the deceased.

However, none of the other prosecution witnesses, namely PW2, PW4, and PW7, have

made any reference whatsoever to the presence or involvement of the 21st Accused-

Appellant at the material time. This account creates a serious lacuna in the prosecution

case against the 21st Accused-Appellant.

41. Moreover, as stated earlier in this judgment, PW5, during his testimony, identified the

21st Accused-Appellant as ‘Kumara.’ However, it is pertinent to note that the actual

name of the 21st Accused-Appellant is not ‘Kumara.’ This discrepancy raises a serious

question regarding the correctness of PW5’s identification of the 21st Accused-

Appellant and further undermines the reliability of his evidence on this crucial issue.

42. Material discrepancies in a witness's evidence concerning the identification of an

accused strike at the very root of the reliability of that evidence. In such circumstances,

it would be unsafe for the Court to act upon the testimony of that witness without

satisfactory corroboration.

43. In Sucha Singh Vs. State of Punjab, (2003) 7 SCC 643, it was held inter alia:

“.......normal discrepancies in evidence are those which are due to normal errors of

observation, normal errors of memory due to lapse of time, due to mental

disposition such as shock and horror at the time of occurrence and those are always

there however honest and truthful a witness may be. Material discrepancies are

those which are not normal, and not expected of a normal person. Courts have to

label the category to which a discrepancy may be categorized. While normal

discrepancies do not corrode the credibility of a party's case, material discrepancies

do so......Where it is not feasible to separate truth from falsehood, because grain

and chaff are inextricably mixed up, and in the process of separation an absolutely

new case has to be reconstructed by divorcing essential details presented by the

prosecution completely from the context and the background against which they are

made, the only available course to be made is to discard the evidence in toto.”

44. Further, since there exists a serious doubt as to whether PW5 actually witnessed the

assault on the deceased in the manner he has described, his testimony regarding the

presence of the 21st Accused-Appellant at the material time becomes highly doubtful

and unsafe to rely upon. In such circumstances, the evidence of PW5, being

uncorroborated on this crucial aspect and suffering from the issues of credibility and

reliability, cannot be accepted as a safe basis to sustain a finding against the 21st

Accused-Appellant. Therefore, it is my view that such identification cannot be regarded

as sufficient to establish, beyond reasonable doubt, the presence or participation of the

21st Accused-Appellant in the alleged unlawful assembly.

45. Additionally, it is noteworthy that the Learned Trial Judge failed to specify whether the

convictions of the 5th and 6th accused for voluntarily causing hurt were entered under

Count 4, which was based on unlawful assembly, or under Count 8, which was founded

on common intention. The judgment is silent on the precise count under which those

convictions were recorded, thereby giving rise to uncertainty as to the legal basis of the

convictions. In the absence of clear findings in this regard, the convictions are rendered

legally uncertain and cannot be sustained without proper judicial reasoning on the

specific foundation of liability.

46. The irrationality of the learned High Court Judge's reasoning is further manifested by

the acquittal of the 14th and 16th accused, notwithstanding the evidence that they too

were present at the scene armed with clubs and knives. The Appellants in the present

appeal were convicted on the basis that they were members of an unlawful assembly. If

the evidence accepted by the Learned High Court Judge established that the 14th and

16th accused were likewise present at the scene armed with clubs and knives, it is

difficult to comprehend the basis upon which they were acquitted. The judgment

discloses no cogent or plausible reasons for treating the 14th and 16th accused differently

from the Appellants, thereby revealing a material inconsistency in the evaluation of the

evidence and the conclusions reached by the Learned High Court Judge.

47. Upon careful consideration of the totality of the evidence and the material infirmities

discussed above, it is my considered view that the convictions of the Accused-

Appellants are unsafe and unsustainable in law. Accordingly, the convictions and

sentences imposed on the Accused-Appellants are set aside, and the Accused-

Appellants are acquitted of all charges.

48. The Appeal is accordingly allowed.
