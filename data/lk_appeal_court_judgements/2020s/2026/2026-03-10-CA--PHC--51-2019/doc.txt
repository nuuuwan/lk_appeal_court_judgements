Factual Matrix

On or about 19.12.2013, the 4th and 5th Respondents–Appellants (hereinafter sometimes referred to as the “4th and 5th Appellants”) obtained a loan in the sum of Rs. 3,000,000/- from the Petitioner–Respondent, namely the Colombo District Co-operative Rural Bank Union Limited (hereinafter referred to as the “Co-operative Rural Bank”). The 6th and 5th Respondents–Appellants (hereinafter sometimes referred to as the “6th and 5th Appellants”) signed the relevant loan documents as guarantors for the due repayment of the said facility.

Subsequently, the 4th and 5th Appellants defaulted in the repayment of the principal sum and the interest due thereon. In consequence thereof, the dispute was referred to the Commissioner of Co-operative Development and Registrar of co-operative societies of Western province (hereinafter referred to as the “Registrar”) in terms of Section 58(1) of the Co-operative Societies Law No. 5 of 1972 (hereinafter referred to as the “Co-operative Law”). In the exercise of the powers conferred by Section 58(2) of the said Law, the Registrar referred the dispute to an Arbitrator for determination.

Following the inquiry conducted into the matter, the Arbitrator made an arbitral award determining the monetary liability of the 4th to 7th Appellants jointly and severally. By the said award, the Arbitrator held that a total sum of Rs. 3,425,753/- was due, comprising Rs. 2,962,955/- as the outstanding principal, Rs. 461,298/- as interest calculated at 18%, together with administrative costs of Rs. 1,500/-. The Arbitrator further directed that interest at the rate of 18% per annum be charged on the remaining financial liability until the full settlement of the debt.

5

The Appeal to the Registrar of Co-operative Societies

Being aggrieved by the said award, an appeal was preferred to the Registrar in terms of Section 58(3) of the Co-operative Law. Upon consideration of the said appeal, the Registrar varied the arbitral award by directing that the accrued additional interest in the sum of Rs. 1,460,000/- be waived, thereby modifying the financial liability determined by the Arbitrator.

Writ Application to the Provincial High Court

Being dissatisfied with the said determination of the Registrar, the Co-operative Rural Bank instituted a writ application before the Western Provincial High Court holden at Colombo, seeking, inter alia, a writ in the nature of certiorari to quash the said decision of the Registrar. Upon consideration of the pleadings and the written submissions tendered by the parties, the learned High Court Judge, by judgment dated 30.04.2019, allowed the writ application and granted relief in favour of the Co-operative Rural Bank.

Being dissatisfied with the said judgment, the 4th to 7th Appellants have preferred the present appeal to this Court, seeking to have the judgment of the learned Judge of the Provincial High Court set aside.

Analysis and Determinations

The principal basis upon which the learned High Court Judge was persuaded to issue a writ of certiorari quashing the decision of the Registrar of Co-operative Development of the Western Province was the conclusion that the Registrar lacked the statutory authority to reduce or waive the interest component of Rs. 1,460,000/- that had been awarded by the Arbitrator. In fact, the learned Judge of the High Court supports his decision stating that the State Counsel who appeared for the Registrar admitted that the Registrar has no legal right to waive the interest component. However, I find myself unable to agree with the determination of the learned Judge of the High Court.

The dispute in the present matter arose under the dispute-resolution mechanism established by Section 58 of the Co-operative Societies Law No. 5 of 1972. In terms of Section 58(1), disputes touching the business of a registered society are required to be referred to the Registrar for determination. The Registrar is empowered under Section 58(2) either to determine the dispute himself or to refer it for disposal to an arbitrator or arbitrators.

6

Pursuant to that provision, the dispute was referred to arbitration and the arbitrator delivered an award which included a sum of Rs. 1,460,000/- as interest.

The statute expressly provides a right of appeal from such an award. Section 58(3) provides that any party aggrieved by the award of the arbitrator may appeal to the Registrar within the prescribed period. A similar provision is contained in the Co-operative Societies Statute No. 03 of 1998 of the Western Province, which likewise provides for an appeal to the Registrar from an arbitral award.

Further, Section 58(5) declares that the decision of the Registrar on such appeal shall be final and shall not be called in question in any civil court.

Accordingly, once an appeal is preferred under Section 58(3), the Registrar assumes appellate authority over the arbitral award and his determination becomes the operative statutory decision in respect of the dispute. The statutory provisions do not restrict the Registrar to merely affirming the award; rather, the appellate jurisdiction necessarily includes the authority to affirm, vary, or set aside the award in whole or in part.

In the present case, the Registrar interfered with the arbitral award in respect of the interest component amounting to Rs. 1,460,000/- and the learned High Court Judge held that the Registrar lacked the statutory authority to reduce or discard that component of the award.

However, in light of the appellate power conferred on the Registrar by Section 58(3), read together with the finality attached to his decision under Section 58(5), the statute does not support the view that the Registrar lacked jurisdiction to vary the award. The Registrar was empowered to determine the appeal and, in doing so, could lawfully affirm or modify the award, including the interest component.

It must, however, be observed that the limitation on the Registrar’s authority in determining an appeal from an arbitral award is not jurisdictional but procedural in nature. In exercising the appellate power conferred by Section 58(3) of the Co-operative Societies Law No. 5 of 1972, the Registrar performs a quasi-judicial function affecting the civil rights of the parties. Consequently, where the Registrar interferes with the findings of the arbitrator, the decision must be reached judicially and must disclose the reasons upon which such interference is founded. (Although document P14, containing the Registrar’s decision, states that the reasons for the decision are forwarded therewith, no reasons have in fact been provided).

7

The requirement to provide reasons ensures that the decision demonstrates proper application of mind to the matters in dispute and enables the parties, as well as a reviewing court, to understand the basis upon which the arbitral award was altered. While a failure to furnish reasons may render the decision susceptible to judicial review, most commonly by way of a writ of certiorari, such a defect relates to the manner in which the power has been exercised. It does not negate the existence of the statutory authority of the Registrar to vary or interfere with the award itself.

It must also be observed that the Registrar, when determining an appeal under Section 58(3) of the Co-operative Societies Law No. 5 of 1972, is required to confine his determination to the matters arising from the appeal and the relief sought by the parties. Where the Registrar proceeds to grant a form of relief which was neither sought nor prayed for in the appeal, the determination extends beyond the issues placed before the appellate authority. Such a course of action deprives the affected party of an opportunity to address the matter and thereby constitutes a breach of the principles of natural justice.

In such circumstances, notwithstanding the statutory finality attached to the Registrar’s decision under Section 58(5), the impugned determination would nevertheless remain subject to judicial supervision. While the statutory finality clause excludes ordinary civil proceedings, it does not exclude constitutional supervisory jurisdiction. Accordingly, where the decision is affected by illegality or procedural impropriety, the matter may fall for consideration within the revisionary jurisdiction of the Provincial High Court under Article 154P(4)(b) of the Constitution or within the writ jurisdiction of the Court of Appeal under Article 140 of the Constitution.

In the present case, it is apparent that in the impugned determination, the Registrar has proceeded to make a substantive variation to the arbitral award without disclosing any reasons for such interference. Further, the relief ultimately granted by the Registrar was not one that had been sought or prayed for by the Appellants in the appeal preferred under Section 58(3). The decision therefore discloses two significant procedural infirmities: first, the absence of reasons for the substantive alteration of the arbitrator’s award, and secondly, the grant of relief beyond the scope of the appeal and outside the matters placed before the appellate authority. Where a statutory authority exercising quasi-judicial power alters the rights of parties without furnishing reasons and proceeds to grant relief not sought by the parties, the decision becomes vitiated by procedural impropriety and breach of the principles of natural justice. In such circumstances, notwithstanding the statutory finality attached to the Registrar’s decision under Section 58(5), the impugned

8

determination remains amenable to constitutional judicial review, by the Provincial High Court under Article 154P(4)(b) of the Constitution, Accordingly, the decision of the Registrar cannot be permitted to stand and is liable to be quashed by way of a writ of certiorari.

Conclusion

Although I am unable to agree with the reasoning adopted by the learned High Court Judge, the impugned determination of the Registrar was nevertheless liable to be quashed on the grounds stated above. In these circumstances, there is no basis to interfere with the final order of the learned High Court Judge. Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed subject to costs.
