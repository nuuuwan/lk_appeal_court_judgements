Adithya Patabendige, J.

The Petitioner, Sri Lankan Airlines Limited, is a duly incorporated company in Sri Lanka and

the national carrier engaged in commercial air transport operations. The Petitioner employs

pilots under written contracts of employment.

This application challenges the determination of the 2nd Respondent, the Assistant

Commissioner of Labour, relating to salary reductions implemented by the Petitioner in 2020

due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Factual Background

Prior to the pandemic, the pilots employed by the Petitioner were remunerated under

contracts that provided for salaries denominated in United States dollars. Such salaries were

payable at the prevailing exchange rate.

With the global spread of COVID-19 in early 2020, international air travel declined

drastically, resulting in severe financial losses for airlines worldwide, including the Petitioner.

In response, the Petitioner introduced salary reductions for employees. From April 2020,

salaries payable in foreign currency were converted using a fixed exchange rate of 188.78

rupees per US dollar.

The 5th Respondent, Sri Lankan Airline Pilots’ Guild, lodged a complaint with the 1st

Respondent, the Commissioner of Labour, dated 02nd March 2021, marked P1, alleging that

these measures were imposed unilaterally and resulted in unlawful deductions from wages.

Acting on that complaint, the 3rd Respondent initiated proceedings and called for

explanations from the Petitioner.

After conducting an inquiry, the 3rd Respondent concluded that the reduction constituted

unauthorized deductions from wages and suggested to recover under the Wages Boards

Ordinance marked 1R11. Acting upon the findings contained in 1R11, the Assistant

Commissioner of Labour, the 2nd Respondent, issued the Order marked P5, directing the

Petitioner to pay the deducted wages of Rs. 1,928,063,281.56.

Aggrieved by the said determination, the Petitioner has invoked the jurisdiction of this court

seeking to quash the impugned decision.

4

Reliefs Sought

The Petitioner seeks, inter alia,

• A mandate in the nature of a writ of certiorari to quash the determination of the 2nd

Respondent, marked P5.

• A writ of prohibition preventing the enforcement of the said decision,

• Other incidental reliefs.

Position of the 1st to 3rd Respondents

The 1st to 3rd Respondents contend that the inquiry was conducted lawfully pursuant to a

complaint of deducting the wages. They assert that the evidence disclosed that the Petitioner

had unilaterally reduced salaries otherwise payable under the contract of employment.

It is their position that the Petitioner failed to produce adequate documentary justification for

the deductions, whereas the Respondents produced a detailed computation of the amounts

due.

The 3rd Respondent further maintains that the inquiry was conducted after affording the

Petitioner an opportunity to present its position, and a determination was issued after

considering material submitted by both parties. Accordingly, they deny any violation of

Natural Justice.

Position of the 4th and 5th Respondents

The 4th and 5th Respondents have filed formal objections opposing the application. Their

position substantially supports the determination made by the 3rd Respondent.

They aver that the salary reductions were imposed unilaterally without the consent of

employees, especially the pilots, and without statutory authority. Particular reliance is placed

on the contractual terms governing remuneration and the salary records produced during the

inquiry proceedings.

5

They further contend that the Petitioner altered the method of salary computation, including

adopting a fixed exchange rate for USD-based remuneration, thereby reducing the salaries

payable to the pilots.

In addition, the 4th and 5th Respondents raised a preliminary objection that the Petitioner has

failed to disclose material facts relating to the inquiry proceedings and the documentary

evidence considered by the 3rd Respondent.

Issues for Determination

The principal issues that arise for determination are,

• Whether the 3rd Respondent acted within the jurisdiction,

• Whether the applicable statute was correctly applied.

• Whether the principles of natural justice were violated.

• Whether the impugned determination is illegal, irrational, or otherwise liable to be

quashed in the exercise of writ jurisdiction.

Alleged Suppression of Material Facts

A principal objection raised by the Respondents is that the Petitioner has not made a full and

frank disclosure of all material facts.

It is contended that the petitioner omits or inadequately discloses,

• The full contents of the complaint lodged by the pilot’s guilt.

• The communications initiating the inquiry.

• The salary data and computations submitted during the Proceedings.

• The extent and method of salary reduction adopted by the Petitioner.

• The participation of the Petitioner in the inquiry process.

It is well established that the Petitioner invoking writ jurisdiction must approach the court

with utmost candor. However, not every omission constitutes suppression. The Court must

6

determine whether the omitted facts are material and whether their non-disclosure would

have affected the exercise of discretion.

Having considered the pleadings and documents, I am of the view that although certain

aspects of the inquiry proceedings are not fully set out in the Petition, the essential nature of

the dispute has been disclosed. Therefore, the alleged nondisclosure does not by itself warrant

dismissal of the application. The Court must be satisfied that any alleged nondisclosure was

both material and deliberate and that it would have affected the exercise of the Court's

discretion.

Jurisdiction and Applicable Law

The Petitioner contends that the proceedings were initiated under the Industrial Disputes Act,

but the determination was made under the Wages Boards Ordinance. Thereby, rendering the

decision without jurisdiction.

Inquiry Initiation under the Industrial Disputes Act

The Petitioner further contends that the proceedings were initiated under the Industrial

Disputes Act and that a determination under the Wages Boards Ordinance is therefore invalid.

I am unable to accept this contention.

The complaint throughout concerned salary reductions implemented by the Petitioner and the

resulting short payment of wages. The Petitioner was aware of the nature of the allegations,

participated in the inquiry, submitted explanations, and produced documentary material

relating to the salary structure and the measures adopted.

In these circumstances, the Petitioner cannot be said to have been misled as to the subject

matter of the proceedings or deprived of an opportunity to present its case and thereby

violates natural justice.

The validity of administrative action must be determined by the substance of the matter rather

than the terminology used in preliminary communications. Even if reference was initially

made to the Industrial Disputes Act, such reference does not invalidate the subsequent

7

decision under the statutory provisions governing payment of wages where the facts disclose

a matter falling within that framework.

In order to examine this contention, it is necessary to consider the scheme of the Wages

Boards Ordinance.

Structure of the Ordinance

The Ordinance is divided into two parts,

• Part I — Provisions relating to all trades.

• Part II — Provisions relating to particular trades.

Part I contains general provisions applicable to workers employed in any trade, whereas Part

II applies only to specific trades for which Wages Boards have been established and decisions

made.

The present dispute does not concern minimum wages or conditions of employment fixed by

a Wages Board under Part II. It concerns alleged reduction and short payment of wages

otherwise payable under contracts of employment. Therefore, the applicability of Part I must

be examined.

Section 2 - Payment of Wages

Section 2(a) of the Ordinance provides that,

The employer of workers in any trade shall comply with the following provisions of this

section regarding the payment of wages to every such worker: -

(a) He shall, subject as hereinafter provided, pay such wages in legal tender directly to the

worker, without any deduction other than an authorized deduction, as hereinafter defined,

made with the consent of the worker:

Provided that the aggregate of the deductions so made at any one time shall not exceed,

(i) seventy-five per centum of the wages due, in the case of a worker in any trade specified

by the Minister for the purposes of this paragraph by Notification published in the Gazette;

and

8

(ii) fifty per centum of the wages due, in the case of a worker employed in any other trade;

Provided, further, that nothing in the preceding provisions of this paragraph shall affect or

be deemed to affect-

(i) any deduction authorized to be made from such wages by the Income Tax Ordinance*

or any other written law; or

(ii) any retention or payment of the whole or any part of such wages made in pursuance of

or compliance with any order, process or decree made or issued by any court of law.

For the purposes of this paragraph a payment which, immediately after the wages are paid

to the worker, is made out of the wages by the worker to the employer or to an agent of the

employer, shall be deemed to be a deduction from the wages.

In this paragraph "authorized deduction" means a deduction made in such manner and

subject to such conditions, if any, as may be prescribed in respect of-

(i) any advance of money made by the employer to the worker,

(ii) any payment which, at the instance of the worker, is made out of the wages of the

worker by the employer to any person other than the employer or an agent of the employer

in order to discharge any obligation of the worker or for any other purpose, or

(iii) any other prescribed matter.

The section sets limits on deductions and recognizes only those authorized by law or made

with the worker's consent.

This provision applies to employers of workers “in any trade.” It is not confined to trades

governed by a Wages Board decision.

In the present case, the complaint before the labour authorities was that the Petitioner

unilaterally reduced salaries otherwise payable under the contracts of employment by

adopting a fixed exchange rate and other measures. Such an allegation directly concerns

deductions from wages and therefore falls within the scope of Section 2.

9

Section 3 - Wage Records

Section 3 requires employers to maintain clear and accurate wage records specifying, inter

alia:

• the wage period,

• the workers paid,

• the wages paid,

• the date of payment, and

• deductions made.

The purpose of this provision is to enable verification of whether wages due have been

properly paid.

Section 3D — Recovery of Short Payment

Section 3D empowers the Commissioner of Labour, where proper wage records are not

maintained or produced, to assess the wages or short payment of wages on the basis of

available evidence and to recover such sums through the statutory mechanism.

This provision is directly relevant to complaints alleging short payment or unlawful

deductions.

The complaint in the present case was precisely that the salaries contractually payable were

reduced. Therefore, the proceedings conducted by the labour authorities fall squarely within

the recovery framework provided by Section 3D.

Interpretation Section (Section 64)

Section 64 defines several key terms.

The term “trade” is defined broadly to include:

“any industry, business, undertaking, occupation, profession or calling…”

An airline company engaged in commercial air transport clearly falls within the ordinary

meaning of an “industry” or “business.”

The term “wages” includes:

10

“any remuneration due in respect of overtime work or of any holiday.”

It indicates that remuneration payable for employment is within the scope of the Ordinance.

The term “worker” is defined as: (within the meaning of employer)

“any person employed to perform any work in any trade.”

Pilots employed by the Petitioner to perform duties in its airline operations fall within this

definition.

Accordingly, the definitions contained in Section 64 are sufficiently wide to encompass the

employment relationship in question.

Section 65 - Effect of the Ordinance

Section 65 provides that, subject to express provisions to the contrary, the Ordinance shall

have effect notwithstanding anything contained in any other written law, and in case of

conflict, its provisions shall prevail.

While this section does not itself create jurisdiction, it reflects the legislative intention that

the provisions relating to payment of wages operate with overriding force where applicable.

Having regard to the structure of the Ordinance and the nature of the complaint, I am of the

view that,

• The provisions of Part I of the Wages Boards Ordinance are applicable to all trades,

including the employment in question.

• The complaint of unilateral salary reduction and short payment falls within Sections

2, 3, and 3D.

• The definitions contained in Section 64 encompass the Petitioner’s undertaking and

the employees concerned.

• The Petitioner was aware of the nature of the inquiry and suffered no prejudice.

• The 2nd Respondent did not act without jurisdiction in issuing the determination

marked P5 based on the findings contained in document 1R11.

11

Whether the Principles of Natural Justice were Violated

The Petitioner contends that the inquiry conducted by the 3rd Respondent was procedurally

unfair and that the determination was issued in violation of the principles of natural justice.

The material placed before the Court, especially the document dated 23rd March 2021,

marked 1R2, discloses that upon receipt of the complaint lodged by the 5th Respondent, the

3rd Respondent informed the Petitioner of the date of the inquiry and sent a copy of the

complaint of the 5th Respondent. Both parties participated in the inquiry dated 09th April

2021, as reflected in the proceedings of that date, marked 1R3.

The 3rd Respondent, by his letter dated 01st November 2021, marked 1R5, informed the

Petitioner to submit all the documents relevant to the complaint made by the 5th Respondent.

The document dated 15th November 2021, marked 1R6, indicates that both parties had

participated on that date.

The Petitioner and the 5th Respondent filed their written submissions on 22nd November

2021, marked 1R7 and 06th of May 2021, marked 1R4, respectively.

The inquiry report marked 1R11 reflects that the 3rd Respondent considered the positions of

both parties, examined the salary data, and made findings regarding the unilateral reduction

of wages.

There is no material to suggest that the Petitioner was denied notice of the allegations, an

opportunity to be heard, or the opportunity to place relevant material before the inquiry

officer. Nor is there evidence that any relevant submissions made by the Petitioner were

disregarded without consideration.

The Petitioner has made a general allegation that the 3rd Respondent acted in breach of the

principles of natural justice. However, the Petition does not specify which component of

natural justice was allegedly violated or how the alleged violation occurred.

A party alleging a breach must demonstrate, with particularity, how these principles have

been infringed and how such infringement has caused prejudice.

A bare assertion of violation of natural justice, unsupported by specific facts, is insufficient to

invoke the supervisory jurisdiction of this Court. Judicial review is concerned with

demonstrable procedural unfairness, not speculative or generalized allegations.

12

Natural justice does not require a formal trial-type hearing unless expressly provided by

statute. What is required is a fair opportunity to present a case.

In the present case, the Petitioner actively participated in the proceedings and cannot now

contend that the fair hearing was not given.

Accordingly, I find no violation of the principles of natural justice.

02 ඒ අනුව එම හිඟ මුදල් අයකර ගැනීම සඳහා නියේදන යැවීම සුදුසු ය.

A careful examination of paragraphs 1 to 7 of the inquiry report marked 1R11 reveals that the

3rd Respondent has identified the essential factual and legal components of the dispute and

recorded findings thereon. The report notes that the complaint concerned the reduction of

pilots’ salaries from April 2020 without their consent, notwithstanding that the contracts of

employment provided for remuneration in United States dollars, calculated at the prevailing

exchange rate. It further observes that, instead of paying salaries in accordance with those

contractual terms, the Petitioner had adopted a fixed exchange rate of Rs. 188.78 per US

dollar from April 2020, resulting in reduced payments. The 3rd Respondent characterizes this

conduct as a unilateral departure from the contractual basis of remuneration and therefore not

legally justified. Significantly, the report records that, while the Petitioner acknowledged the

reduction in salaries, no agreement between the parties had been reached authorizing such a

variation. The 3rd Respondent also notes that the Petitioner failed to submit detailed

particulars of the deductions, whereas the complainant party furnished documentary material

enabling computation of the alleged shortfall. On that basis, the inquiry concludes that

unauthorized deductions had occurred and recommends that steps be taken to recover the

arrears calculated from the information supplied by the complainant.

Accordingly, the report cannot be regarded as devoid of reasons. It discloses the complaint

considered, the contractual framework, the factual findings regarding unilateral reduction, the

14

evidentiary basis relied upon, and the rationale for the recommended course of action. The

correctness or merits of the conclusion reached by the 3rd Respondent cannot be examined in

proceedings for judicial review.

Administrative decisions are not required to contain elaborate judicial reasoning. It is

sufficient if the decision discloses the basis upon which it was made.

The House of Lords, in the case of South Bucks District Council and another v Porter

(FC) [2004] UKHL 33, considered this issue, and Lord Brown stated that the reasons for a

decision must be intelligible, and they must be adequate. They must enable the reader to

understand why the matter was decided as it was and what conclusions were reached on the

"principal important controversial issues", disclosing how any issue of law or fact was

resolved. Reasons can be briefly stated, the degree of particularity required depending

entirely on the nature of the issues falling for decision. The reasoning must not give rise to a

substantial doubt as to whether the decision-maker erred in law, for example by

misunderstanding some relevant policy or some other important matter or by failing to reach

a rational decision on relevant grounds. But such adverse inference will not readily be

drawn. The reasons need refer only to the main issues in the dispute, not to every material

consideration.

The reasons in the inquiry report (1R11), read together with the formal order (P5), adequately

disclose the factual and legal basis for the determination.

Therefore, the impugned decision cannot be characterized as arbitrary or unreasonable.

Impact of Covid -19 and Salary Reduction

A central argument advanced by the Petitioner is that salary reductions were implemented

across the global aviation industry during the COVID-19 pandemic and that the measures

adopted were necessary for survival.

There is no doubt that the pandemic caused unprecedented disruption to international air

transport and severe financial hardship to airline operators worldwide. Many airlines

implemented temporary cost-cutting measures, including reductions in remuneration.

15

However, the legality of such measures in Sri Lanka must be determined in accordance with

applicable domestic law and the contractual rights of employees.

The fact that similar measures were adopted in other countries or by other airlines does not,

by itself, justify unilateral reductions as lawful under Sri Lankan law.

Where contracts of employment specify the remuneration payable, any alteration of those

terms ordinarily requires consent or statutory authority unless the contract itself permits

variation.

Accordingly, the global practice relied upon by the Petitioner cannot, by itself, override the

contractual rights of employees or the statutory protections governing the payment of wages

under the Wages Boards Ordinance.

In this regard, the decision of the House of Lords in Rigby v Ferodo Ltd [1988] ICR 29 is

instructive. In that case, the employer, faced with financial difficulties, imposed a wage

reduction without the consent of the employee. The employee continued to work under

protest and later claimed the difference between the contractual wage and the reduced amount

paid. The employer contended that the employee had accepted the new terms by continuing in

employment or that the reduction terminated the original contract.

Rejecting those arguments, Lord Oliver of Aylmerton held that it is common ground that the

unilateral imposition by an employer of a reduction in the agreed remuneration of an

employee constitutes a fundamental and repudiatory breach of the contract of employment

which, if accepted by the employee, would terminate the contract forthwith.

The decision further establishes that even where an employee continues to work, such

conduct does not necessarily amount to acceptance of the new terms, particularly where the

reduction is disputed or accepted under protest.

Accordingly, while the economic consequences of the COVID-19 pandemic are undeniable,

such circumstances cannot, without more, displace contractual obligations or statutory

protections governing the payment of wages. The critical inquiry remains whether the

reduction was effected in a manner authorized by law or by agreement of the parties.

16

Suggestion of Reference for Industrial Arbitration by the 3rd Respondent

The Petitioner states that during the inquiry proceedings, the 3rd Respondent suggested that

the dispute be referred for industrial arbitration, but that the 1st and 2nd Respondents failed to

take steps in that regard (vide. P3).

Under Section 3 of the Industrial Disputes Act, the power to refer a dispute for arbitration lies

with the Minister and is exercised at his discretion.

The failure to make such a reference does not invalidate proceedings otherwise lawfully

conducted under another statute, nor does it confer a right upon a party to insist upon

arbitration.

The 1st to 3rd Respondents have taken the position that, since the Petitioner admitted

implementing the salary reductions, the dispute concerned the recovery of short-paid wages

rather than an unresolved industrial dispute warranting arbitration.

In any event, the absence of a reference for arbitration does not affect the validity of the

determination made under the Wages Boards Ordinance.

Rationality and Legality of the Decision

In an application for judicial review, the intervention of the Court is warranted only where the

decision is

• illegal or made without jurisdiction.

• irrational or unreasonable.

• procedurally unfair.

In the present case,

• The inquiry was conducted by a Labour Officer, the 3rd Respondent.

• The subject matter concerned alleged unlawful deductions from wages.

• The Petitioner participated in the proceedings.

• Reasons for the determination are available.

17

• No procedural unfairness is established.

The Court cannot interfere merely because another view may be possible on the merits.

Conclusion

Having considered all the material placed before the Court, I hold that,

• The 3rd Respondent, Labour Officer, acted within his jurisdiction.

• The applicable statutory provisions were correctly invoked.

• The Petitioner was afforded a fair opportunity to present its case.

• The determination is supported by reasons.

• No ground has been established for the exercise of writ jurisdiction.

Accordingly, the application must fail.

Order

For the foregoing reasons, I find no basis to interfere with the impugned determination.

Accordingly, the application for Writs of Certiorari and Prohibition is refused and dismissed.

In the circumstances of the case, I make no order as to costs.
