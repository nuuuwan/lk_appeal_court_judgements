JUDGMENT

P. Kumararatnam, J.

The above-named Accused-Appellant (hereinafter referred to as the

Appellant) was indicted by the Attorney General in the High Court of

Colombo for committing the following charges:

1. Between 01.06.2001 and 30.06.2001, the accused falsely represented

to the Director of the Meteorological Department that the total salaries

payable to the clerical officers amounted to Rs. 196,155.96, when in

fact the actual amount payable was Rs. 174,578.96, thereby

dishonestly causing a loss of Rs. 21,577/- to the State which is an

offence punishable under Section 5(2) of the Offences Against Public

Property Act No. 12 of 1982), read with Section 400 of the Penal Code.

2. Between 01.07.2001 and 30.07.2001, the accused falsely represented

that the total salaries payable were Rs. 188,379.94, when in fact the

actual amount payable was Rs. 177,792.97, resulting in a loss of Rs.

10,587/- to the State which is an offence punishable under Section

5(2) of the Offences Against Public Property Act No. 12 of 1982), read

with Section 400 of the Penal Code.

3. Between 01.10.2001 and 30.10.2001, the accused falsely represented

that the total salaries payable amounted to Rs. 256,563.77, while the

actual amount due was Rs. 234,986.77, thereby causing a further loss

of Rs. 21,577/- to the State which is an offence punishable under

Section 5(2) of the Offences Against Public Property Act No. 12 of 1982),

read with Section 400 of the Penal Code.

4. During the period relevant to Counts 01, 02, and 03, the accused

dishonestly misappropriated a total sum of Rs. 53,741/-, constituting

an offence under Section 5(1) of the Offences Against Public Property

Act No. 12 of 1982, read with Section 386 of the Penal Code.

After leading evidence of PW1, PW2, PW3, PW07+4, PW05, PW07, PW08,

PW10 and PW11 and marking productions P1 to P5 the prosecution had

closed their case.

The learned High Court Judge had called for the defence and the Appellant

had given evidence from witness box and called a witness and closed his

case.

After considering the oral and documentary evidence provided, the Learned

High Court Judge of Colombo, by way of her judgment dated 21.01.2016,

found the Appellant guilty on all four counts and sentenced the Appellant as

follows:

In respect of Charges 01, 02 and 03: Two (02) years of rigorous imprisonment

on each count, with a fine equivalent to three times the value of the amount

cheated, as follows:

• Count 01 - Rs. 64,731/-

• Count 02 – Rs. 31,761/-

• Count 03 – Rs. 64,731/-

The default sentence for each fine is six (06) months of simple imprisonment.

These sentences for Counts 01, 02 and 03 were ordered to run concurrently.

In respect of Charge 04: Two (02) years of rigorous imprisonment.

Being aggrieved by the aforesaid conviction and sentence, the Appellant

preferred this appeal to this court. The Appellant, who was on bail, was

present in Court when the matter was argued.

At the very outset the learned President’s Counsel for the Appellant brought

to the notice of the Court that in the connected matter the Appellant was

acquitted by the learned High Court Judge of Colombo.

The learned State Counsel in keeping with the highest traditions of the

Attorney General’s Department, conceded that the conviction is bad in law.

The learned State Counsel made submissions to substantiate his position.

As per the State Counsel, at all material times, the Appellant served and

worked only as a Salaries Clerk attached to the respective division of the

Meteorological Department, thereby, only serving in a subordinate

administrative position. Notably, the Accountant (PW02) was responsible for

the overall financial control, administration and supervision in respect of the

payments of salaries, along with the aid of the Shroff (PW04). Both the

Accountant and the Shroff held the primary supervisory responsibility to

overlook the entire salary disbursement process.

In such circumstances, the commission of an offence of Cheating,

punishable under Section 400 of the Penal Code read with Section 5(2) of the

Offences Against Public Property Act No.12 of 1982 (as amended) could not

have taken place in isolation. In relation to such a severe offence, in its

inherent nature, one can reasonably assume that coordination and

concurrence between multiple officers involved in the processes of

preparation, handling, supervision and administration of the salary

payments, enabled such an offence to take place.

After a careful consideration of the record, it is quite evident that such a

falsification could not have only affected the Appellant. This is due to the

existing established administrative procedure that requires the relevant

documents to be subjected to various levels of scrutiny and approval, after

the initial preparation of the salary summary. This proves includes

verification by the Accounts Division, examination by the Accountant,

certification by the Assistant Director General and a final approval by the

Director General. As such, these documents which are now alleged to have

been falsified, were approved and authorised at these specified levels by the

specified senior officers who have been granted the authority and power to

make decisions and approve.

In such a situation, the sole responsibility for the alleged offence cannot be

put merely on the Appellant, as the Appellant operated at the lowest level of

this overall administrative hierarchy. When such senior officers have affixed

their signatures and approved and authorised such documents, it implies

that such authorization carries accountability for the content of the

documents. Therefore, it is legally not plausible to impose exclusive criminal

liability on merely the Salaries Clerk, while allowing superior officers, who

were clearly responsible for exercising due diligence and oversight, to escape

and not face any legal liability whatsoever. The material would at least

disclose the negligence and failure of supervision of these officers; therefore,

it would be unsafe and unjust to subject only the Appellant to criminal

culpability. It should be noted that when the prosecution alleged that in the

respective charges within the indictment that the Appellant had cheated, the

Director General (PW1) had not given evidence to the effect that he was

cheated by the Appellant. His position in this regard was correct as he had

not been given any document for payment approval by the Appellant.

In respect of the collection and handling of cash withdrawn from the bank,

it must be noted that PW04, the Shroff, was the senior officer responsible for

collecting cheques, withdrawing cash and maintaining the safe custody of

such funds. Although the accused Appellant would have accompanied PW04

on certain instances, PW04 bore the legal and administrative responsibility

for the collection, custody and handling of cash. Additionally, the Accountant

was responsible for ensuring that the Accounts Division duly receives the

total sum withdrawn and properly supervises the process of packeting

individual salaries. Such functions were carried out under the authority and

supervision of both the Accountant and the Shroff, who occupied positions

of seniority within the Department.

It is also important to note that the Accountant had a continuing duty to

verify that the cash withdrawn was correctly packed and disbursed to each

employee who is entitled to receive a salary, while also being responsible for

ensuring that such disbursements corresponded with the approved salary

summary. As per the available evidence, any remaining balances after the

packeting of individual salaries were retained by PW04. Further, the custody,

authority and handling of such funds remained with the Accountant and the

Shroff. Therefore, the overall salary disbursement process was based upon a

framework of collective responsibility, which involved various officers such

as the Accountant, the Shroff, the Assistant Director, and other supervisory

personnel. Considering such a context, it is understandable how the blame

may fall upon the smallest or weakest link in the chain, thus enabling the

officers with the power and authority to escape accountability and evade

scrutiny. Such an outcome, is undoubtedly, unjust and unreasonable.

In situations where such an authorization was conferred at various different

supervisory levels, legally, it would be unsafe to hold merely the Appellant

accountable and criminally responsible. As per the settled principles of

criminal jurisprudence, where a doubt may arise in respect of the culpability

of the Appellant, such a doubt should have been resolved in the Appellant’s

favour. As such, his appeal is allowed. He is acquitted from all the charges.

The Registrar of this Court is directed to send this judgment to the High

Court of Colombo along with the original case record.
