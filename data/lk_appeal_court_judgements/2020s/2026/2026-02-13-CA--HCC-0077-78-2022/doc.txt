Background facts

4. Two days prior to the alleged incident, there had been a fight between the 1st and 2nd

Accused and the deceased. On the date of the incident, some of the Accused came to

the residence of PW1, PW2, and PW3 and engaged in an argument; however, the

matter was subsequently settled. The incident that resulted in the death of the

deceased and injury to PW1 is alleged to have occurred between 7.30 p.m. and 8.00

p.m.

5. In view of the grounds advanced by both Appellants, the following grounds of appeal

may be considered together, as they are common to both Appellants. These grounds

relate to the credibility of the prosecution witnesses, whether a proper and reliable

identification of the Appellants was made, and whether the defence evidence was

adequately considered, or if not, whether it was rejected on unreasonable or improper

grounds.

6. The question whether a proper identification was made by the witnesses is a factual

issue closely intertwined with the credibility of those witnesses. Accordingly, both

matters will be considered together.

7. It is evident that PW1, PW2, PW3, and the deceased are related to one another. It is

also apparent that PW1, PW2, and PW3 had known the Appellants from childhood

and were not strangers to them.

8. The appellants strenuously contended that the evidence relating to their identification

by PW1 and PW2 is unreliable due to the darkness that prevailed at the scene of the

alleged offence. It was further argued that, in view of the inconsistencies discernible

in the evidence of PW1, it would be unsafe to secure a conviction based solely on his

testimony.

9. When a conviction is sought to be founded wholly or substantially on visual

identification, especially under difficult lighting conditions, the court must approach

such evidence with the utmost caution. The surrounding circumstances, including the

source and intensity of light, the distance between the witness and the accused, the

duration of observation, prior familiarity, and the absence or presence of obstruction,

must be carefully evaluated.

10. In the present case, although it is contended that darkness prevailed at the scene, the

evidence must be assessed in its entirety. If PW1 and PW2 had prior acquaintance

with the appellants, the degree of caution required may differ from a case involving

identification of strangers.

11. Therefore, the determinative question is whether, notwithstanding the lighting

conditions and the inconsistencies highlighted, the identification evidence is of such

quality and reliability as to safely sustain a conviction beyond reasonable doubt.

12. While the fact that PW1, PW2, and PW3 were familiar with the Appellants from

childhood may reduce the risk of mistaken identity, such familiarity does not dispense

with the need for the Court to exercise caution in evaluating their evidence.

13. Moreover, such evidence must be subjected to careful scrutiny, particularly where the

incident occurred under conditions of poor visibility and where prior animosity

between the parties is evident. In such circumstances, the possibility of exaggeration,

embellishment, or false implication cannot be entirely ruled out and must be

examined in the light of the totality of the evidence.

14. As evidenced from the testimonies of two Judicial Medical Officers, both PW1 and

the deceased sustained stab injuries. It is evident that the fatal injuries received by the

deceased were inflicted by a sharp, pointed cutting weapon, and some of these injuries

were classified as necessarily fatal.

15. Furthermore, the injuries sustained by PW1 were also classified as injuries

endangering life. Accordingly, the nature of the injuries, the weapons used to inflict

them, and the cause of death have been established by medical evidence beyond

reasonable doubt.

16. The question of cardinal importance, therefore, is whether the witnesses correctly

identified the Appellants as the persons who committed this crime, particularly in

light of the prevailing circumstances at the time, including the lighting conditions at

the scene.

17. In this case, PW1, PW2, and PW3 are lay witnesses. PW1, who sustained injuries and

survived, was the principal eyewitness. According to PW1, the incident occurred at

around 7.30 p.m. On that day, PW1 went to a nearby boutique to purchase cigarettes,

where he encountered the deceased. After buying the cigarettes, PW1 was returning,

accompanied by the deceased, when they were attacked near the entrance to the

compound of the 5th Accused. The witness identified the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, and

6th Accused as the persons who assaulted them. PW1 further stated that there were

approximately seven persons present, but he could only identify these six accused due

to the darkness at the scene.

18. PW1 further stated that the 1st and 2nd Appellants were armed with knives, while the

others carried pieces of wood. According to PW1, the 1st Appellant slapped the

deceased and then stabbed him. The 2nd Appellant was present and, while armed with

a knife, threatened PW1 not to shout. At that time, PW1 attempted to shield the

25. It is also pertinent to note that PW1 was examined by Dr. Kithsiri Jagath Kumara at

the Hambantota Hospital. Upon examination, Dr. Jagath Kumara prepared the

Medico-Legal Report. In the history provided by PW1, none of the appellants were

named as the persons who stabbed him. Instead, PW1 stated that he had been stabbed

by a group of individuals. PW1 was examined on 06.07.2000, that is, 14 days after the

alleged incident, and there is no evidence to suggest that he was in an unstable

condition at the time of giving the history. The failure of PW1 to mention the names

of any of the appellants when providing the history to the doctor raises further doubt

as to the accuracy and reliability of his subsequent identification of the appellants.

26. As emphasized earlier, identification evidence, particularly when it relates to an

incident alleged to have taken place at night, in an isolated location, and under

conditions of extremely poor visibility, must be approached with the greatest caution.

In the present case, the evidence clearly establishes that the area was pitch dark, that

there were no street lamps, that nearby houses had no electricity supply, and that the

police themselves had to rely on the headlights of their vehicles and handheld torches

to make their observations. In such circumstances, the assertion by PW1 that he

identified the appellants visually, and additionally by their voices, cannot be accepted

without corroboration.

27. Moreover, PW1’s own admission that he was intoxicated at the time of the incident

further diminishes the reliability of his perception and recall. His evidence that he

30. It is also significant to note that PW1 never stated whether any of the appellants other

than the 2nd appellant, whom he claimed to have identified by their voices, had uttered

anything that enabled such identification. PW1 merely asserted that he identified the

appellants by their voices, without specifying what was said by each appellant at the

relevant time that assisted him in making that identification.

31. The law is well settled that identification by voice, particularly in conditions of poor

visibility, is inherently weak unless supported by clear and cogent evidence. In order

for such identification to be reliable, the witness must state with precision the words

allegedly spoken, the familiarity of the voice, and the circumstances under which the

voice was heard.

32. In the present case, PW1 failed to specify any words spoken by any of the appellants

or explain how such utterances enabled him to identify each appellant individually.

PW1 merely stated that the 2nd appellant threatened him not to shout, which, in my

view, would be hardly sufficient to enable any reliable voice identification.

33. In the absence of such particulars, PW1’s assertion that he identified the appellants by

their voices remains a bare assertion, unsupported by objective or verifiable evidence.

Consequently, this alleged identification lacks probative value and cannot safely be

relied upon to sustain a conviction. In this regard following authorities would give

some guidelines:

highlighted the dangers inherent in voice recognition evidence. Gage LJ, having

reviewed the evidence of an expert, stated:

“In general terms the expert evidence before us demonstrates the following:

1) Identification of a suspect by voice recognition is more difficult than visual

identification.

2) Identification by voice recognition is likely to be more reliable when carried

out by experts using acoustic and spectrographic techniques as well as

sophisticated auditory techniques, than lay listener identification.

3) The ability of a lay listener correctly to identify voices is subject to a number

of variables.

There is at present little research about the effect of variability but the following

factors are relevant:

(i) the quality of the recording of the disputed voice or voices;

(ii) the gap in time between the listener hearing the known voice and his

attempt to recognise the disputed voice;

(iii) the ability of the individual lay listener to identify voices in general.

Research shows that the ability of an individual to identify voices varies

from person to person.

(iv) the nature and duration of the speech which is sought to be identified is

important. Obviously, some voices are more distinctive than others and the

longer the sample of speech the better the prospect of identification.

(v) the greater the familiarity of the listener with the known voice the better his

or her chance of accurately identifying a disputed voice. However, research

shows that a confident recognition by a lay listener of a familiar voice may

nevertheless be wrong.”

38. It is worthy to mention that there are some authorities in Indian jurisprudence that

provide a different perspective when the issue of identification arises under

circumstances similar to that of the case in hand.

39. The below mentioned cases consistently establish that eyewitness identification in

low-light or night-time conditions is reliable when the witnesses are familiar with the

accused and attentive, particularly i*n rural settings where the villagers’ eyesight and

perception are adapted to dim light from lanterns, earthen lamps, or starlight, and that

identification can be made by facial features, voice, silhouette, shadow, or gait.

40. Even minimal light is generally sufficient for recognition, especially when the

accused are known to the witnesses, and the proximity and attentiveness of the

witnesses at the time of the incident further support the credibility of their

identification. Thus, poor lighting alone does not render identification unreliable.

41. In the case of Kalilro Tiwari V. State of Bihar AIR 1997 SC 2186 Indian Supreme

Court held:

"where the only source of light at place of occurrence which was village

where dacoit in question took place where an earthen lamp, the identification

of Accused even in such light would not be problem for villagers especially

when many of dacoits were direct relations of complainant. The visibility

capacity of urban people who are acclimatized to fluorescent lights or in

candescent lamps is not the standard to be applied to villagers whose optical

potency is attuned to country- made lamps. Their visibility is conditioned to

such lights and hence it would be quite possible for them to identify men and

matters in such light.

42. In the case of Kirpal Singh V. T State Uttar Pradesh AIR (1965) 712 the Indian

Supreme Court held thus

"It is true that the evidence about identification of a person by the timbre of his

voice depending upon subtle variation in the overtones when the person

recognizing is not familiar with the person recognized may be somewhat risky

in a criminal trial. But where the accused is intimately known to the witness

We have considered the said contention from all its angles. Ever assuming that

proximity at which the assailants would have confronted with the injured, the

possibility of some light reaching there from the glove of stars, and the fact

that the murder was committed on a roofless terrace are germane factors to be

born in mind while judging whether the victims could have had enough

visibility to correctly identify the assailants. Over and above those factors, we

light was not enough for the injured who would certainly have pointedly

focused their eyes on the faces of the intruders standing in front of them. What

is sauce for the goose is sauce for the gander.

45. In light of the aforementioned authorities, the question of whether PW1 was able to

identify the Appellants accurately cannot be determined solely by reference to the

lighting conditions at the scene. It is evident that the Appellants were known to PW1

from childhood, that they come from the same village, and that they are related to

each other. Furthermore, prior to the incident, they had met at a function held at

PW1’s residence. Accordingly, the accuracy of PW1’s identification of the Appellants

must be assessed in the context of these factors.

46. However, in evaluating the reliability of the evidence relating to the identification of

the appellants, the aforementioned facts must be considered alongside the glaring

discrepancies and infirmities present in the prosecution case, particularly in the

testimony of PW1. Where the evidence of PW1 lacks consistency and reliability on

material aspects of the case, it would be unsafe to act upon such evidence. Where the

evidence of identification is coupled with vagueness or material contradictions, the

court must be slow to act upon such testimony unless it is corroborated by other

reliable evidence. In the present case, the vagueness of PW1’s evidence, particularly

the inconsistencies discernible therein, raises serious questions regarding the

trustworthiness and credibility of his testimony concerning the identification of the

appellants.

47. More importantly, the uncertainty, coupled with the inconsistencies that cast doubt on

whether PW1 in fact clearly identified the appellants or merely narrated what he

overheard, does not inspire sufficient confidence in this Court to affirm the

conviction.

48. It is also apparent from the impugned judgment that the learned trial Judge arrived at

certain findings against the 2nd appellant that are wholly unsupported by the evidence

led at trial. Throughout the judgment, the learned trial Judge observed that the 2nd

appellant had also stabbed PW1, although no such evidence was adduced. At pages

288, 405, 406, 414, and 417 of the brief, the learned trial Judge repeatedly states that

the 2nd appellant stabbed PW1. This constitutes a clear misapprehension and

misrepresentation of the evidence on record.
