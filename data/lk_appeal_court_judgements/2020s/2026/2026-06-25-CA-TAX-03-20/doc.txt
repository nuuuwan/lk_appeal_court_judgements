ANALYSIS

The Appellant contends that it carries on a leasing business whereby it acquires assets,

including motor vehicles and equipment, and provides such assets to its customers under

financial lease arrangements. Under these arrangements, the customer is entitled to use the

asset for an agreed period, during which the Appellant derives income by way of lease rentals

payable by the customer.

It is the position of the Respondent that the assets acquired by the Appellant for the purposes

of its leasing business constitute stock-in-trade and, accordingly, that the cost incurred in

acquiring such assets cannot be deducted in the computation of distributable profits under

section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006.

The Appellant, however, contends that the assets in question are not assets held for sale in the

ordinary course of trade. Rather, they are assets acquired and retained by the Appellant for

the purpose of generating income through its leasing operations. Accordingly, the Appellant

maintains that such assets cannot be characterised as stock-in-trade for the purposes of

section 61(1)(b) of the Act.

Page 6 of 24

On this basis, the Appellant has formulated the above twelve questions of law in front of this

court. However, the central issue in this appeal is whether the assets purchased by the

Appellant and leased to its customers, constitute capital assets for the purpose of determining

distributable profits under section 61(1)(b) of the IRA 2006.

Therefore, I shall deal with the question of law I separately, and the since the rest of the

questions are in relation to the application of section 61(1)(b) of the IRA 2006, I shall address

the questions of Law II, III, IV, V, VI, VII, VIII, IX, X, XI, and XII together.

Question of Law I

The question of law I which has been raised by the Appellant is reproduced below;

Has the Tax Appeals Commission erred in law in determining that the Commissioner General

of Inland Revenue has not made the determination within two years, as required by section

165 (14) of the Inland Revenue Act, No. 10 of 2006?

However, this court finds that the above question has not been formulated properly.

Therefore, this court takes the necessary steps to formulate the said question of law I in the

following manner;

Has the Tax Appeals Commission erred in law in determining that the Commissioner General

of Inland Revenue has made the determination within two years, as required by section 165

(14) of the Inland Revenue Act, No. 10 of 2006?

At the hearing of the appeal before the TAC, the Appellant raised a preliminary objection on

the basis that the Respondent had not issued the determination within the two-year period

prescribed under Section 165(14) of the IRA 2006.

It is the Appellant’s position that the appeal was submitted to the Respondent on 24th of

December 2014. However, the Respondent did not acknowledge the receipt of the appeal.

Instead, the appeal was acknowledged by a Deputy Commissioner, who, according to the

Appellant, lacked the authority to acknowledge the appeal.

Page 7 of 24

On this basis, the Appellant argues that, in the absence of a valid acknowledgment by the

Respondent, Section 165(6) of the IRA 2006 deems the date of receipt of the appeal to be the

date of the receipt. Accordingly, the Appellant contends that the appeal was received by the

Respondent on 24th of December 2014, while the Respondent issued its determination only

on 18th of January 2017.

Therefore, the Appellant submits that the determination was not made within the two-year

period prescribed under Section 165(14) of the IRA 2006 and, consequently, the appeal is

deemed to have been allowed.

However, the Respondent contends that Section 165(6) of the IRA 2006 merely requires that

an appeal be acknowledged and does not specify the person or officer who must provide such

acknowledgment. Accordingly, the Respondent argues that the letter titled Form I.R. 131

A.P.L.1, dated 19th of January 2015 and issued by a Deputy Commissioner S. Jayasinghe,

constitutes a valid acknowledgment of the appeal.

The Respondent further submits that the said form was issued by a Deputy Commissioner of

the Large Taxpayers Appeal Unit with the approval of the Respondent and the Senior

Commissioner and, therefore, amounts to a sufficient acknowledgment of the appeal for the

purposes of Section 165(6) of the IRA 2006.

In consideration of the foregoing, the TAC in its determination has held as follows;

“As referred to above, sending of the 1.R.131 A.P.L.1 form is sufficient

acknowledgement of the appeal. Further, it would appear that this procedure has

been accepted by the tax payers as sufficient acknowledgement of their appeals. With

regard to this matter, it is to be mentioned that, in the case of Lanka Ashok Leyland

PLC. Vs the Commissioner General of Inland Revenue, Case No. CA/TAX/14/2017,

Tax Appeals Commission Case No. TAC/IT/016/2015, decided on 14.12.2018, the

Court of Appeal with regard to the acknowledgement of the appeal stated as follows;

………

Therefore, it is to be observed that, the letter of acknowledgement has been sent to the

Appellant by the Deputy Commissioner S. Jayasinghe, Large Tax Payers Appeal Unit

Page 8 of 24

on 19.01.2015, within 30 days of the receipt of the appeal which was dated

24.12.2014, as required by Section 165(6) of the Inland Revenue Act. The Respondent

in this case, has made his determination on 09.01.2017, and that was within two years

from the date of the receipt of the appeal. Under these circumstances it is very clear is

determination of the Respondent has been made within two Year as required

by Section 165(14) of the Inland Revenue Act. The date 18.01.2017 that was given by

the Representative for the Appellant as the date of the determination of the appeal,

may have been the date on which the Appellant received the determination. Therefore,

there is no merit in the preliminary objection raised by the Representative for the

Appellant stating that the Respondent has not complied with Section 165(14) of the

Inland Revenue Act.”

The section 165(6) of the IRA 2006, which deals with the acknowledgement of appeal made

to the Respondent, reads as follows;

“(6) The receipt of every appeal shall be acknowledged within thirty days of its

receipt and where so acknowledged, the date of the letter of acknowledgement shall

for the purpose of this section, be deemed to be the date of receipt of such appeal.

Where however the receipt of any appeal is not so acknowledged, such appeal shall

be deemed to have been received by the Commissioner General on the day of which

it is delivered to the Commissioner-General. ”

Section 165(6) of the IRA 2006 provides that the receipt of an appeal shall be acknowledged

within thirty days of its receipt. Where such acknowledgment is issued, the date appearing on

the letter of acknowledgment is deemed to be the date of receipt of the appeal for the

purposes of the section. Conversely, where the receipt of an appeal is not acknowledged

within the prescribed period, the appeal is deemed to have been received by the

Commissioner-General on the date on which it was delivered to him.

Accordingly, this provision creates a statutory mechanism for determining the date of receipt

of an appeal, with the applicable date depending upon whether an acknowledgment has been

duly issued. It is the Appellant’s position that as the appeal was not properly acknowledged

under section 165(6), therefore it shall be deemed to have been received by the

Page 9 of 24

Commissioner-General on 24th of December 2014 and as the appeal has been determined by

the Respondent on 9th of January 2017, the determination of the Appeal was time barred

under section 165(14) of the Inland Revenue Act.

It is the contention of the Respondent that the aforesaid provision does not specify the person

or officer who is required to issue the acknowledgment of an appeal. This position of the

Respondent cannot be agreed upon since the appeal under section is itself directed to the

Commissioner-General. Therefore, any acknowledgment of such appeal must emanate from

the office of the Commissioner-General, either directly or through a duly authorized officer

acting on his behalf.

Section 165 is the first provision under Chapter XXIII of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of

2006, which governs appeals made to the Commissioner-General of Inland Revenue.

Furthermore, the section does not refer to any person other than the Commissioner-General as

the authority to whom such an appeal must be addressed. This is further supported by the side

note to the provision itself, which is titled “Appeals to the Commissioner-General.”

It is very well observed by this court that the Commissioner-General, as the head of the

Department of Inland Revenue, cannot reasonably be expected to personally discharge every

duty entrusted to him under the Act. In the ordinary course of administration, he may act

through officers of the Department who have been duly authorized to perform functions of an

administrative or routine nature. Accordingly, where an officer has been duly authorized by

the Commissioner-General, any act performed by such officer within the scope of that

authority is, in law, deemed to be an act of the Commissioner-General himself.

The interpretation section to the IRA 2006 under section 217, defines the term

‘Commissioner General’ in the following manner.

“ “Commissioner-General” means the Commissioner-General of Inland Revenue

appointed or deemed to be appointed under this Act, and :—

(a) in relation to any provision of this Act, includes the Senior Deputy Commissioner-

General, a Deputy Commissioner General, Senior Commissioner, a Senior

Commissioner and Commissioner who is specially authorized by the Commissioner-

Page 10 of 24

General either generally or for some specific purpose, to act on behalf of the

Commissioner General; ”

The definition of “Commissioner-General” under the Act extends beyond the person holding

the office of Commissioner-General. Paragraph (a) expressly provides that, in relation to any

provision of the Act, the term includes a Senior Deputy Commissioner-General, Deputy

Commissioner-General, Senior Commissioner, or Commissioner who has been specially

authorized by the Commissioner-General, either generally or for a specific purpose, to act on

his behalf. Accordingly, the actions of the above mentioned personnel’s and any act

performed by the authorized officer within the scope of that authority is deemed, for the

purposes of the Act, to be an act of the Commissioner-General himself.

In the present matter, the TAC determined that the letter dated 19th of January 2015, issued by

Deputy Commissioner S. Jayasinghe of the Large Taxpayers Appeal Unit, constituted a valid

acknowledgment of the Appellant's appeal.

When considering the above provisions of law, it is apparent that the Commissioner General

includes the Deputy Commissioner, who is named in the Act itself. Therefore, I am of the

view that the acknowledgment issued by Deputy Commissioner S. Jayasinghe on 19th of

January 2015 constituted a valid acknowledgment within the meaning of Section 165(6) of

the IRA 2006.

In consideration of the circumstances of the case, I am of the view that the letter dated 19th of

January 2015 sent by the Deputy Commissioner S. Jayasinghe is a valid acknowledgement

and the Respondent has made the determination within two years, as required by section 165

(14) of the Inland Revenue Act, No. 10 of 2006. Accordingly, this court finds that the TAC

has not erred in law in treating the said letter as a valid acknowledgment of the appeal.

Therefore, I answer the question of law I in the Negative.

Questions of Law II – XII

The questions of law II – XII, deals with the same substantive matter, whether the assets

purchased by the Appellant and leased to its customers, constitute capital assets for the

purpose of determining distributable profits under section 61(1)(b) of the IRA 2006.

Therefore, I shall discuss the questions of law II – XII together.

It is the contention of the Appellant that, in the course of carrying on its leasing business, it

acquires assets such as motor vehicles and other equipment. These assets are then provided to

customers under leasing agreements, pursuant to which the customers obtain the right to

possess and use the assets for an agreed period, in return for the payment of periodic lease

rentals to the Appellant.

The Appellant further contends that the income generated from its leasing business arises

from the lease rentals paid by customers for the use of those assets. Accordingly, it is the

Appellant's position that the assets remains the property of the Appellant throughout the lease

period and continue to form part of its capital employed in the conduct of its leasing business.

On this basis, the Appellant and filed its return for the year of Assessment 2011/2012 on 26th

of November 2012. The said assessment was rejected by the Assessor and a new assessment

dated 28th of November 2014, was issued by increasing the tax liability on the Appellant on

four aspects, namely;

1) Tax on Distributable profit

2) Changes in the value of Forward Contracts

3) Bad Debt Written Off

4) Provisions of bad debt

Page 12 of 24

Being aggrieved by the said assessment, the Appellant lodged an appeal before the

Respondent. Upon hearing the appeal, the issues relating to the taxation of the value of

forward contracts, the disallowance of bad debts, and the specific provisions made for bad

debts in respect of the leasing business were determined in favor of the Appellant.

It is further observed that, upon hearing the appeal, the Respondent amended the Assessor’s

assessment in favour of the Appellant by allowing: (i) the gain on the value of forward

contracts; (ii) the bad debt write-off; and (iii) the specific provisions made for bad debts

relating to the leasing business. Accordingly, the only issue that is before this Court relates to

imposition of tax under Section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act, 2006.

The Respondent in its determination dated 9th of January 2017, has accepted the Assessor’s

calculation of distributable profits for the following reasons;

“It is your company's argument that acquisition of leased assets can be identified as

acquisition of any land on any capital asset and is deductible from the book profit in

the calculation of distributable profits

My view of that the word "Capital asset" is used here to mean the accounting term

rather than to denote any concept established in taxation. It is my opinion that leased

assets cannot be identified as acquisition of any capital assets due to following

reasons.

a) A capital asset is wished for the strengthening the profit earning power of the

circulating capital, comparably for a long period, where the trading stocks are

intended to create profits within a non long period. Lease out of an asset under a

finance lease agreement is a providing of funds to fund seeker which is a trading

activity of the company and therefore, reduce of such leased assets is recovered

periodically within a shorter period of time compared to the lifetime of the capital

asset. A capital asset generates profits by helping to circulate the trading stocks, but

not as direct recovery of cost

b) A leased asset creates for the lessor an entitlement for future cash flow. Logically,

a more right to future cash flows for a non- long period cannot be classified as a

Page 13 of 24

capital asset and it is only a receivable. The cash follows are attached to the

receivable account. In reality, what is happening in a finance lease situation is that

a lessee is exchanging its right to use the asset for a series of payment that is

appropriate consideration. It is transferring of the lessor's existing rights to use

thecapital asset to the lessee converting the capital nature of the asset into a

circulating nature for the lessor.

c) Further, practically, the disposition of a capital asset effects an erosion of the

profit deriving base of the company whereas, in case of the leased assets, disposition

or termination of a leased stock does not result in such an effect.

Therefore, I determine that the Assessor's Calculation of distributable profit is

correct.”

Being dissatisfied with the said determination of the Respondent, the Appellant has made an

appeal to TAC. The TAC after hearing the submissions made by both parties upheld the

decision of the Respondent in their determination dated 6th of December 2019. The TAC has

upheld the decision of the Respondent for the reasons set out below.

“It is clear that the intention of Section 61 is to encourage the Appellant Company to

declare their dividends. There is no intention to impose a tax burden on companies

which are distributing their dividends. However, if a company is not declaring their

dividends, to the shareholders, tax on distributional profit will be levied. The

Commissioner General of Inland Revenue has to be satisfied that the relevant

distributable profit has been deemed as distributed in such instances. The argument of

the Representative for the Appellant was that the acquisition of the lease stock can be

identified as acquisition of land or any capital asset and is deductible from the book

profits in the calculation of distributable profit. However, Representative for the

Respondent submitted that the lease stock cannot be identified as an acquisition of

any capital asset due to the following reasons.

I. The Intention of the above provision is to encourage the distribution of dividends to

develop the capital market. If the lease stock is allowed as a deduction to calculate

Page 14 of 24

the relevant distributional profit which is not a part of book profit, then the

distributional profits of the leasing company will decrease significantly, because the

value of lease stocks is representing an amount of capital which is almost similar to a

capital debt or a loan. Further, there is no addition to the book profit with regard to

the capital portion of lease rentals when calculating the distributable profit. The

capital recovered through the lease rentals are added in calculating the profits from

the business for normal income tax purposes.

II. The words both "capital" and "asset" and the classification of assets as current

assets or capital assets are used in accounting and accordingly the collective term

that is "capital asset" is used in the above section to mean an accounting term rather

than to denote any concept established in taxation. The adjustment in relation to

capital assets is made to the book profit and not to the taxable profit. In the

calculation of book profits of the Appellant, allowance for depreciation in respect of

lease stock has not been deducted as it is recorded as the lease receivable (debtor) in

the financial statements and not as capital assets.

III. Capital expenditure or Capital asset is made, not only once for all, but with a

view to bringing into existence an asset or an advantage for the enduring benefit of a

trade. Accordingly, capital asset is intended - for the strengthening the profit earning

power of the circulating capital, comparable for a long period, whereas the trading

stocks are intended to create profits within a short period. Leasing of an asset under a

finance lease agreement is providing of funds to the fund seeker which is the trading

activity of the Appellant (lessor) and, therefore, such leased stock is recognized as the

trading stock of the business. Further, the Section 61 referred to above, does not

intend to deduct the cost of trading stock in calculating the distributable profit.

IV. The cost of a leased stock is recovered periodically within a shorter period of time

compared to the lifetime of the capital asset. A capital asset generates profits by

helping to circulate the trading stocks, but not as direct recovery of cost, as it

happens in the leasing business.

Page 15 of 24

V. A leased stock creates for the appellant (lessor) an entitlement for future cash flow.

Logically, a right to future cash flow for a non-long period cannot be classified as a

capital asset and it is only a receivable.

VI. Practically, the disposition of a capital asset effects an erosion of the profit

deriving base of the company whereas, in the case of leasing companies, disposition

or termination of a leased-out asset does not result in such an effect. Disposal of

leased out assets is only a normal and compulsory business transaction of the leasing

business.

Additionally, the Representative for the Respondent, quoted the budget proposals for

the year 2007 and the ruling of the Commissioner General on this matter. In the

budget proposals it was stated that, "The distributable profits for any year would in

this context, be the after tax profits for that year reduced by the excess of the cost

incurred in that year, on the acquisition of land or other capital assets over the

depreciation charged thereon". The Commissioner Generals' ruling stated that

"The assets given on finance lease by a leasing company, can be treated as capital

assets of the company, if such assets form part of the fixed assets of the company".

Therefore, the Representative for the Respondent submitted that, for the income tax

purposes, capital portion of lease rentals are treated as gross income while allowing

the capital allowances. The total deduction to the business profit in the form of

capital allowances and the total additions to the business profits in the form of capital

portion of the lease rentals will be equal at the time, that is at the end of the lease

period. Hence, he submitted that, lease stocks cannot be treated as capital assets to

calculate the distributable profits, just because the Appellant company has claimed

the cost of the lease stock without addition to the book profit.

It is to observed that the Appellant company as required in terms of Section 61 (1) (b)

of the Inland Revenue Act, No. 10 of 2006, as amended by Amendment Act. No 10 of

2007, No. 9 of 2008 and No. 22 of 2011, has not distributed the distributable profits

for the year of assessment 2010/2011 by way of dividends and has therefore became

liable for income tax. Even though it has been argued on behalf of the Appellant

Company that, in the leasing business, the assets purchased by leasing companies

should be considered as "capital assets" and deductible in computing distributable

Page 16 of 24

profits, the intention of Section 61 (1) of the Inland Revenue Act, No. 10 of 2006, as

amended, would be defeated if such an interpretation is to be given. Hence, we are of

the view that the lease stock cannot be treated as capital assets which could be

deducted, when calculating the distributable profits.

In this case, we have carefully considered both the oral and written submissions of the

Representatives for the Appellant and the Respondent. For the reasons stated above,

we confirm the determination of the Respondent and accordingly, dismiss the appeal

of the Appellant.”

The TAC has held that the lease stock cannot be treated as a capital asset for the purpose of

calculating distributable profits under Section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act. The

Commission found that the purpose of the provision is to encourage the distribution of

dividends, and allowing leasing companies to deduct the cost of lease stock would

significantly reduce distributable profits and undermine that objective. The TAC further

considered the lease stock to be part of the ordinary trading activities of a leasing company

rather than an enduring asset forming part of its capital structure. It also noted that, under

accounting principles, leased assets are reflected as lease receivables rather than fixed assets,

and therefore do not constitute capital assets for the purpose of the section.

Accordingly, the TAC concluded that lease stock does not qualify as a capital asset for the

purposes of Section 61(1)(b), and therefore its cost cannot be deducted in calculating

distributable profits. Since the Appellant had not distributed the required distributable profits

as dividends, the Commission upheld the determination of the Inland Revenue Department

and dismissed the appeal.

(c) any notional profit computed on the basis of a revaluation of any capital asset and

included in such book profits,

increased by the aggregate of the allowance for depreciation deducted in respect of

such capital asset in calculating such book profits and any notional loss computed on

the basis of a revaluation of any capital asset and included in such book profits;”

For the purposes of Section 61(1) of the IRA 2006, distributable profits are not the same as a

company's taxable income. Instead, they are calculated based on the company's book profits

(i.e., accounting profits shown in the financial statements), subject to certain adjustments.

The calculation begins with the company's book profits for the relevant year of assessment.

From the said amount, the company must deduct: the income tax payable for that year, the

cost incurred in acquiring land or other capital assets during the year, and any unrealized

gains arising from the revaluation of capital assets that have been included in the book

Page 19 of 24

profits. These deductions are made because such amounts do not represent profits that are

readily available for distribution.

At the same time, certain amounts are added back to the book profits. These include

depreciation charged on capital assets and any unrealized losses arising from the revaluation

of capital assets. Such amounts are added back because they are accounting adjustments

rather than actual cash outflows.

Therefore, in calculating the distributable profits of a company, the following amounts must

be deducted from its book profits:

1. The income tax payable by the company for the relevant year of assessment;

2. The cost incurred by the company during that year of assessment in acquiring any

land or capital asset; and

3. Any notional profit arising from the revaluation of a capital asset that has been

included in the company's book profits.

The issue in the present case primarily concerns the second category of deductions, namely,

the cost incurred by a company in the acquisition of land or capital assets. Accordingly, the

analysis will focus on this aspect.

The deductions prescribed for ‘distributable profits’ under Section 61(1)(b) are intended to

exclude amounts that do not represent profits freely available for distribution to shareholders.

Based on the plain and ordinary meaning of the provision, it is clear that the cost incurred by

a company during a particular year of assessment in acquiring any land or capital asset must

be deducted in calculating its distributable profits. This is on the basis that the funds used to

acquire such assets have been reinvested in the business and are therefore no longer available

for distribution as dividends. The purpose of the deduction is to ensure that a company is not

compelled to distribute profits that have been committed towards the acquisition of long-term

assets necessary for the operation and growth of its business. In this way, the provision seeks

to preserve the company's capital base while identifying the profits that are genuinely

available for distribution to shareholders.

Page 20 of 24

Accordingly, the availability of this deduction depends on whether the asset in question

qualifies as a "capital asset" within the meaning of Section 61(1)(b). Although Section 61 of

the IRA 2006 does not itself define the term "capital asset", the Act contains a definition

elsewhere which is relevant to its interpretation.

The Respondent and the Tax Appeals Commission, in their respective determinations, have

relied on various definitions of the term “capital assets”. However, the term is specifically

defined in section 25(7)(b) of the IRA 2006, and therefore the statutory definition ought to

prevail.

Further, in the interpretation of tax legislation, the definitions expressly provided by the Act

must be given effect. It is impermissible to depart from such statutory definitions and

substitute foreign or artificial interpretations for terms that have been specifically defined by

the legislature.

Section 27(7)(b) of IRA 2006 defines a capital asset in the following manner;

“(b) “capital asset” in relation to a trade, business, profession or vocation means any

plant, machinery, fixture, fitting, utensils, articles or equipment, including computer

software or intangible assets other than goodwill, used for the purpose of producing

the income in such trade, business, profession or vocation or building constructed

for the purposes of such trade, business, profession or vocation;”

As for the above provision, the capital asset, in relation to a trade, business, profession, or

vocation, shall include any plant, machinery, fixture, fitting, utensil, article, equipment,

computer software, or intangible asset (other than goodwill), used for the purpose of

producing income from such trade, business, profession, or vocation, as well as any building

constructed for such purposes.

Therefore, in determining whether the cost of a particular asset to be deducted in computing

distributable profits under Section 61(1)(b), it is necessary to examine whether that asset falls

within the statutory definition of a "capital asset". If the asset satisfies that definition, its

acquisition cost is deductible from book profits in arriving at distributable profits.

Conversely, if the asset does not constitute a capital asset, no such deduction is available.

In line with the above I would like to bring the attention to the determination of the TAC in

the page 5, where it was held in the following manner;

“In dealing with the substantive issue, the Representative for the Appellant submitted

that the assets purchased by leasing companies for the purpose of the leasing business

should be considered "as capital assets" in computing distributable profit. He

submitted that leasing companies purchase assets and these assets are given on hiring

contracts, normally for periods ranging from 3 to 7 years. The assets are continuously

owned by the leasing companies, until the last leasing installment is paid by their

clients. Therefore, the rental income received by the leasing company is considered as

income liable for income tax. Accordingly, due to this reason, the Department of

Inland Revenue has allowed the depreciation allowance on such lease assets. Hence,

the Department of Inland Revenue has confirmed and accepted the fact that, assets

purchased by leasing companies for leasing business are "capital assets" for

purposes of taxation. However, in this case, the Respondent has disputed the cost of

the acquisition of such capital assets for the purposes of computation of

distributable profit, as required in Section 61 (1) (b) of the Inland Revenue Act…..”

The above passage demonstrates that both the Respondent and the TAC has accepted that the

assets purchased by the Appellant for the purpose of its leasing business constitute capital

assets. However, in the latter part, the Respondent illogically contends that the Appellant’s

use of those capital assets in leasing transactions, and the transfer of certain rights in such

assets to the lessees, transforms the nature of the assets from capital assets into circulating

assets in the hands of the lessor. Consequently, the Respondent argues that, for the purposes

of Section 61(1)(b) of the IRA 2006, the assets held by the Appellant should be regarded as

trading stock.

This position cannot be sustained under Section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act, 2006.

The relevant consideration under that provision is whether the asset in question constitutes a

capital asset of the company and whether the expenditure incurred in acquiring that asset was

included for the purpose of generating distributable profits. Once it is accepted that the assets

acquired by the Appellant for the purposes of its leasing business are capital assets, their

Page 22 of 24

character cannot be altered merely because they are employed in the ordinary course of that

business to generate income. The fact that the assets are leased to customers and that certain

possessory or contractual rights are granted to lessees does not divest the Appellant of

ownership, nor does it convert the assets into trading stock or circulating capital.

It is abundantly clear that leasing is the very means by which the Appellant utilizes its capital

assets to earn revenue. The income generated by utilizing a capital asset does not change the

inherent nature of the asset itself. If such reasoning were to be accepted, every capital asset

used in a profit-making activity would lose its capital character merely because it is employed

to generate income, which is contrary to established principles distinguishing fixed capital

from circulating capital.

Furthermore, the Respondent's position is inherently contradictory. On one hand, the

Respondent accepts that the assets acquired by the Appellant for its leasing business are

capital assets for the purposes of income taxation and accordingly grants depreciation

allowances in respect of such assets. On the other hand, the Respondent seeks to deny their

character as capital assets for the purpose of computing distributable profits under Section

61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act, 2006. There is no legal or logical basis for attributing

two different characters to the same assets for these purposes.

Accordingly, I am of the view that the Respondent's attempt to recharacterize the leased

assets as trading stock is inconsistent with its own treatment of those assets as capital assets

for tax purposes and it is incompatible with the proper interpretation and application of

Section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006.

For these reasons, I answer Questions of Law II to XII in the affirmative. Consequently, the

Appellant is entitled, in terms of Section 61(1)(b) of the Inland Revenue Act No. 10 of 2006,

to deduct the cost incurred during the relevant year of assessment in acquiring any land or

capital asset for the purpose of calculating its distributable profits.
