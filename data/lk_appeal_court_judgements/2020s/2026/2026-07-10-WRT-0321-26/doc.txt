The Petitioners have preferred to this Court the instant application under

Article 140 of the Constitution of the Democratic Socialist Republic of Sri

Lanka (hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Constitution’), seeking inter-

alia, a mandate in the nature of a writ of certiorari quashing the Resolution

annexed to the petition furnished to this Court by them marked as 29(a) and

29(e) adopted by the Board of Directors of the Bank of Ceylon-1st Respondent

(hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the 1st Respondent’) under section 21 of

the Bank of Ceylon Ordinance as amended (hereinafter called and referred to

as ‘the Ordinance’) and a writ of prohibition preventing the 1st Respondent

and all those acting under it from selling the properties referred to in the

aforesaid Resolution and morefully, described in the schedule to the aforesaid

petition by way of Public auction.

The learned Counsel for the Petitioner made submissions in support of the

application for notice as well as the application for interim relief sought in the

prayer to the petition and the learned Counsel for the 1st Respondent also

made submissions in opposition thereto, and the matter was then, fixed for

order thereon, for today.

As is discernible from the averments in the petition of the Petitioners,

averments in paragraphs 33 and 34 thereof, in particular, the principle

contention advanced by the learned Counsel for the Petitioners in support of

the application for notice as well as the application for interim relief sought in

the prayer to the petition is that the so called Resolution adopted by the Board

of Directors of the 1st Respondent under section 21 of the Ordinance to sell

the properties described in the schedule thereto, by way of Public auction, is

illegal and therefore, no force or avail in law in view of provisions of Act No. 1

of 2011 and Act No. 19 of 2011 which according to the Petitioners, imposes

an explicit prohibition for the recovery of any loan in respect of which default

is made where the principle amount borrowed of such loan is less than Rs. 5

Million.

The original section 5 of the Recovery of Loans by Banks (Special Provisions)

Act, No. 4 of 1990 (hereinafter called and referred to as ‘the Act’) had been

amended by the Recovery of Loans by Banks (Special Provisions)

(Amendment) Act, Nos. 1 of 2011 and 19 of 2011 and those are the

enactments referred to in paragraph 33 of the petition of the Petitioners and

the section 5A of the Act as amended enacts thus;

“5A. (1) No action shall be initiated in terms of section 3 of the principal

enactment for the recovery of any loan in respect of which default is

made, nor shall any steps be taken in terms of section 4 or section 5 of

the aforesaid Act, where the principal amount borrowed of such loan is

less than rupees five million :

Provided however, at the time of default when calculating the principal

amount borrowed due and owing to the Bank on the loan granted to

such defaulter, the interest accrued on such loan and any penalty

imposed thereon, shall not be taken into consideration.

(2) The provisions of section 5A of this Act, shall also apply in relation

to any bank established by an Incorporation Order made under the

provisions of any written law for the time being in force,

notwithstanding any provisions relating to the recovery of loans by any

such bank.”.

A simple and plain reading of their own averments contained in the paragraph

5 of the petition of the Petitioners makes it manifest without any doubt that

the principal amounts of such loans so borrowed by them and referred to in

paragraph 5 thereof, from the 1st Respondent are more than rupees five

million.

In the circumstances, the Board of Directors of the 1st Respondents had legal

authority and or the power by virtue of section 21 of the Ordinance to be read

with section 5A of the Act to initiate action under section 21 of the Ordinance

for the recovery of the loans admittedly, granted by it to the Petitioners in

respect of which default is admittedly, made by the Petitioners.

Hence, I would hold that the adoption of the Resolution by the Board of

Directors of the 1st Respondent by virtue of the powers vested in it under and

in terms of section 21 of the Ordinance to be read with section 5A of the Act

the Act for the recovery of the loans admittedly, granted by it to the Petitioners

is intra-vires and therefore, entirely, legal and it is of force and avail of law.

I would therefore, hold that the application of the Petitioners is fundamentally,

flawed in law and cannot therefore, sustain in law and as such it ought to be

dismissed in-limine, on this ground alone.

There is a further ground which would in my opinion, tend to vitiate the

application of the Petitioners and it may now, be examined.

When this matter came on before us on 02.06.2026, the learned Counsel for

the Petitioners had informed Court that the Public auction that the Petitioners

sought to prevent the 1st Respondent from holding by way of a mandate in the

nature of a writ of prohibition had already, taken place and the properties

mortgaged by the Petitioners to the 1st Respondent as security for the

repayment of the said loans had thus, been already, sold at that Public

auction, which by itself shows that the application of the Petitioners has now,

become redundant and futile.

I would therefore, hold that the instant application ought to be dismissed on

this ground too.

In the circumstances, I would hold that the instant application is not entitled

to succeed in law as it is fundamentally, flawed as enumerated above.

In the result, I would refuse to issue notices of the application on the

Respondents.

I would thus, dismiss the instant application with costs.
