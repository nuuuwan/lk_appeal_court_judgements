Adithya Patabendige, J.

Introduction

The Petitioners, who are presently serving as Sub Inspectors of Police in the Sri Lanka

Police, have invoked the writ jurisdiction of this Court under Article 140 of the Constitution,

challenging a scheme of promotions to the rank of Inspector of Police effected by the 1st

Respondent through R.T.M Message No. 142 dated 17th May 2018 marked P12.

The 1st Respondent is the Inspector General of the Sri Lanka Police, and the 2nd Respondent

is the Senior Deputy Inspector General of Police - Administration. The Petitioners state that

the interview board was composed of the 3rd to 7th Respondents.

The principal grievance of the Petitioners is that, in the process of awarding marks under the

applicable marking scheme marked P11, the 1st to 7th Respondents have failed to take into

account the period of service rendered by the Petitioners in the Sri Lanka Reserve Police,

thereby unjustly depriving them of marks under the category of “Years of Service.” It is their

case that this exclusion has resulted in less meritorious officers being promoted over and

above them.

The Petitioners further contend that the impugned actions are arbitrary and unreasonable, and

as such, violative their legitimate expectations and the principles of fairness in administrative

decision-making.

Reliefs Sought

The petitioners seek, inter alia;

1) A mandate in the nature of a writ of certiorari to quash the purported promotions

granted to the 8th to 545th Respondents, as reflected in the R.T.M. message No. 142

issued by the 1st Respondent, marked P12,

2) A mandate in the nature of a writ of certiorari to quash the decision of the 3rd to 7th

Respondents and/or the 1st and 2nd Respondents to deny marks to the Petitioners under

the category of “service period” in respect of their service rendered in the rank of

Sub-Inspector in the Reserve Police Service under the round of promotions in terms

of R.T.M. No. 769,

25

3) A mandate in the nature of a writ of mandamus directing the relevant Respondents to

award marks to the Petitioners for their period of service in the Reserve Police

Service, including the full complement of marks calculated at four marks per year, or,

in the alternative, to award such marks subject to any applicable conditions and

promote in terms of the Law, to the rank of Inspector of Police,

4) A mandate in the nature of a writ of mandamus directing the Respondents to conduct

fresh interviews and make selections in accordance with the applicable law and

marking scheme, and to promote the Petitioners to the rank of Inspector of Police with

effect from 31st May 2016, together with all consequential benefits.

Factual Background

The material facts, as borne out by the pleadings and documents, may be briefly set out as

follows.

The Petitioners had initially joined the Sri Lanka Police as Reserve Sub Inspectors, some of

them as far back as 1990. It is not in dispute that during this period they were engaged in

active service and performed duties and functions comparable to those carried out by officers

in the regular service.

By a Cabinet decision made in 2006, the Reserve Police Service was abolished, and its

members, including the Petitioners, were absorbed into the Regular Police Service. This

position is admitted by the Respondents.

Following such absorption, Police Communication bearing No. RTM 1002 marked P3, and

the Circular dated 08th March 2006 marked P4 were issued in relation to the absorption of

reserve officers into the regular cadre. Thereafter, by Circular dated 06th July 2007 marked

P9, directions were issued with regard to the determination of the period of service of officers

so absorbed, which, inter alia, provided that the period of service should be reckoned from

the date of appointment to the Reserve Police.

Applications were called for promotion to the rank of Inspector of Police in respect of

vacancies prevailing as at 31st May 2016, in terms of RTM No. 769 dated 20th September

2016 marked P10 and P11. The said scheme provided for the allocation of marks in several

categories, including “period of service.”

26

The Petitioners, being eligible officers, applied for the said promotions and were called for

interviews. It is not in dispute that the selection was based on a system of marks and that the

candidates who secured marks above the cut-off were selected and promoted.

However, according to the Petitioners, in computing marks under the category of “period of

service,” the Respondents had taken into account only the period of service in the Regular

Police Service from 2006 onwards, thereby excluding the period of service rendered by them

in the Reserve Police Service prior to their absorption.

The Petitioners state that this resulted in a significant reduction in the marks awarded to them

under the said category. It is their position that the maximum marks awarded (40 marks)

demonstrate that only the period between 2006 and 2016 had been considered.

As a consequence, the Petitioners contend that they failed to secure promotion, whereas other

officers who had obtained higher marks under the impugned computation were selected and

promoted, as reflected in RTM Message No. 142 marked P12.

Being aggrieved by the said conduct of the Respondents, the Petitioners have instituted the

present application seeking the reliefs set out in the prayer to the Petition.

Position of the 1st to 7th Respondents

The 1st to 7th Respondents (hereinafter referred to as Respondents) have filed a Statement of

Objections denying the material averments of the Petitioners, save and except those

specifically admitted.

It is the position of the Respondents that all Petitioners had initially joined the Sri Lanka

Police as Sub Inspectors in the Reserve Police Service, and that by a Cabinet decision taken

in the year 2006, the Reserve Police Service was abolished, and its members were absorbed

into the Regular Police Service.

The Respondents state that, following such absorption, the Petitioners became part of the

regular cadre, and that the subsequent scheme of promotions was governed by directives

issued by the National Police Commission, which is the appointing authority under the

Constitution.

With regard to the impugned promotions, the Respondents state that applications were called

for the post of Inspector of Police for vacancies prevailing as at 31st May 2016, in terms of

27

RTM No. 769 marked P10 and P11, and that interviews were conducted in accordance with

the prescribed marking scheme. It is their position that candidates were selected strictly on

basic qualifications and order of marks, and that those who secured the highest marks were

appointed.

The Respondents further state that, in awarding marks under the category of “period of

service,” the computation was carried out in accordance with the applicable scheme marked

P8 and directives, and that there was no illegality or arbitrariness in the process adopted.

It is also the position of the Respondents that the determination of seniority and service in the

regular cadre is governed by directives issued following the absorption of reserve officers,

including the letter dated 02nd August 2007 marked R3, and that promotions are to be based

on the date on which an officer is made permanent in the regular cadre.

The Respondents, however, acknowledge that in certain instances the period of service in the

Reserve Police Service has been considered at the time of promotions.

The Respondents further contend that the entire application is misconceived in law, in that the

Petitioners have no legal right to the reliefs sought, and that the Petitioners have failed to

come before the Court with clean hands. It is also averred that the necessary parties have not

been properly added, especially the National Police Commission.

It is to be noted that in their written submissions, the Respondents have also adverted to the

constitutional framework under Article 155C of the Constitution and contended that this

Court lacked jurisdiction to hear this application on the basis of the constitutional ouster

embodied in the said Article. However, this Court observes that this contention was neither

taken up in the Statement of Objections nor was it taken up or pursued in the oral arguments

at the hearing of this application.

The Respondents, however, prayed that this application be dismissed with costs.

Analysis

Preliminary Observation on Jurisdiction Based on Article 155C (1) of the Constitution

The Respondents, in their pre-argument and post-argument written submissions, have raised a

contention regarding the jurisdiction of this Court by reference to Article 61A of the

Constitution, read with Article 155. However, as stated earlier, it is to be noted that this

28

contention does not appear in the Statement of Objections nor was it pursued in oral

submissions at the hearing of this application.

In any event, the relevant constitutional provisions form part of the legal framework within

which this Court is required to determine the present application.

It is also important to observe that the constitutional framework governing the Public Service

Commission and the National Police Commission has undergone several changes through the

Seventeenth to Twenty-First Amendments to the Constitution.

However, it is a well-established principle that, in general, the rights of parties and the

applicable legal framework must be determined as at the date of the institution of

proceedings.

Accordingly, it is abundantly clear that for the purpose of analysing the issues arising in the

present application, only the 17th, 18th, and 19th Amendments were applicable at the relevant

time when this application was filed.

Upon a careful consideration of the pleadings, documents, and submissions of the parties, this

Court observes that the principal grievance of the Petitioners relates to the manner in which

their period of service in the Reserve Police Service has been treated in the process leading to

the impugned decision marked P12. The Petitioners rely on the directive marked P8, which

provides that in promoting officers from rank to rank, due recognition should be accorded to

the past service of Reserve Police Officers. It is also to be noted that in paragraph 22 of the

Statement of Objections, the Respondents have stated that in certain instances, such prior

service had been taken into account. In that context, the Petitioners' contention raises an

arguable issue as to whether the said directive has been applied consistently and

transparently.

However, the determination of the present application cannot be made in isolation from the

constitutional framework governing the Police Service. Under Article 155G of the

Constitution, the power of appointment, promotion, transfer, disciplinary control, and

dismissal of police officers (other than the Inspector General of Police) is vested in the

National Police Commission. The exercise of these powers is further insulated by Article

155C (1) of the Constitution, which provides that, subject only to the jurisdiction of the

Supreme Court under Article 126, no court or tribunal shall have the power or jurisdiction to

inquire into, pronounce upon, or in any manner call in question any order or decision made

29

by the Commission in the exercise of its powers. The language used in Article 155C (1) is of

the widest scope and clearly excludes the jurisdiction of the courts, including the writ

jurisdiction of this Court under Article 140.

Article 155C (1) of the Constitution states as follows:

Subject to the jurisdiction conferred on the Supreme Court under paragraph (1) of

Article 126, no court or tribunal shall have the power or jurisdiction to inquire into, or

pronounce upon or in any manner call in question any order or decision made by the

Commission or a Committee, in pursuance of any power or duty, conferred or imposed on

such Commission or Committee under this Chapter or under any other law.

When considering the above provision, the scope and interpretation of Article 61A of the

Constitution are of considerable assistance.

Article 61A provides:

Subject to the provisions of paragraphs (1), (2), (3), (4) and (5) of Article 126, no

court or tribunal shall have power or jurisdiction to inquire into, or pronounce upon or in

any manner call in question any order or decision made by the Commission, a Committee,

or any public officer, in pursuance of any power or duty conferred or imposed on such

Commission, or delegated to a Committee or public officer, under this Chapter or under

any other law.

A comparison of Article 61A and Article 155C(1) of the Constitution shows that both

provisions use substantially identical language, precluding any court or tribunal from

inquiring into, or calling into question, decisions made by the respective Commissions.

The nature and scope of Article 61A has been authoritatively considered by the Supreme

Court in Athauda Arachchige Patrine Dilrukshi Dias Wickremasinghe v Dr. Prathap

Ramanujam and Others S.C. Appeal No. 88/2024, decided on 04.06.2025, wherein His

Lordship Achala Wengappuli upon a detailed examination of the language of the provision,

including its marginal note and by comparison with other constitutional provisions conferring

immunity, arrived at the conclusion that Article 61A operates as a constitutional ouster of the

jurisdiction of courts. The Supreme Court emphasised that the provision is directed at

limiting the power of courts and tribunals, including the writ jurisdiction of the Court of

Appeal, to inquire into or call in question decisions of the Commission, and is therefore

30

concerned with the exclusion of jurisdiction rather than with the protection of individuals

from legal proceedings.

In view of the similarity in language, structure, and constitutional purpose between Articles

61A and 155C (1), I am of the view that the same interpretation must necessarily apply to

Article 155C. Accordingly, where a matter relates to appointment, promotion, or other

service-related decisions within the Police Service falling within the powers of the National

Police Commission, the jurisdiction of this Court under Article 140 is correspondingly

excluded.

The scope of such constitutional limitations has also been considered by this Court in

Hemantha Chamindra Ovitigama v Inspector General of Police and Others, CA (Writ)

1009/2008, decided on 10th May 2019, where His Lordship Janak de Silva held that the

Court cannot do indirectly what it is precluded from doing directly, and that relief in respect

of matters relating to promotion within the Police Service cannot be granted in the exercise of

writ jurisdiction.

In the present case, although the Petitioners have framed their application to challenge the

legality of the decision-making process, the substance of the relief sought, and the effect of

any order this Court might make would necessarily involve an inquiry into how promotions

are to be determined within the Police Service, including the recognition of prior service.

Such an inquiry would inevitably encroach upon matters within the exclusive constitutional

mandate of the National Police Commission.

In these circumstances, I am of the view that the jurisdiction of this Court is constitutionally

excluded. The Petitioners are not without remedy, as the constitutional framework provides

for recourse to the National Police Commission, thereafter to the Administrative Appeals

Tribunal, and, where appropriate, to the Supreme Court under Article 126. The decisions of

the Administrative Appeals Tribunal can be challenged by invoking the writ jurisdiction of

the Court of Appeal.

Although the Respondents have not specifically pursued the objection based on Article 155C

of the Constitution in their Statement of Objections or during oral submissions, the question

of jurisdiction is a matter which this Court is obliged to consider ex mero motu. It is well

settled that jurisdiction cannot be conferred by consent or waiver of parties, and where a

31

constitutional provision expressly limits the jurisdiction of this Court, such limitation must be

given effect to, irrespective of whether it has been raised by the parties.

In view of the foregoing finding on jurisdiction, it is neither necessary nor appropriate for this

Court to enter into a detailed determination of the other issues raised by the parties. However,

the concerns raised by the Petitioners regarding the application of the directive marked P8

and the treatment of their prior service may appropriately be pursued within the framework of

the remedies available under the above law.

Conclusion and Order

For the reasons set out above, I hold that the subject matter of this application concerns

promotion and service within the Sri Lanka Police, matters that fall within the constitutional

mandate of the National Police Commission under Article 155G of the Constitution. In view

of the express provisions of Article 155C (1) of the Constitution, which operate as a

constitutional ouster of the jurisdiction of courts, including the writ jurisdiction of this Court

under Article 140, I am of the view that this Court is precluded from entertaining the present

application.

Accordingly, the application is dismissed.

In the circumstances of this case, I make no order as to costs.
