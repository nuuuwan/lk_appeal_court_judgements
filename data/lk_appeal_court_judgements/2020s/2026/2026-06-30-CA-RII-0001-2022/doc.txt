Dr. Sumudu Premachandra J. 1] The Petitioner details a long-standing Partition Action regarding a plot of land

called "Gabadawaththa", initiated in the District Court of Kalutara (DC Kalutara

Case No.7099/Partition) in February 2002, with a subsequent judgement

delivered on 28/11/2008, and the Interlocutory Decree entered accordingly.

2] The conflict arose on 29/01/2021, when a Licensed Surveyor attempted to

execute a final survey of said partition action but allegedly entered the

Petitioner's land on a pretext, removing concrete boundary posts. The Petitioner

and his wife objected because a portion of their land, separately registered under

the Registration of Title Act No. 21 of 1998 with a First Class Title of Absolute

Ownership for lot No. 152, was wrongfully included in the Partition Corpus.

3] This resistance prompted Court summons and contempt charges under

Section 793 of the Civil Procedure Code, initiated by the 9th Plaintiff-Respondent

(annexed as F and G, alongside responding documents R1 and R2).

4] In response, the Petitioner filed an application under Section 839 of the Civil

Procedure Code seeking the exclusion of his registered land from the Corpus

and requesting a commission to superimpose Plan No. 299 onto the Map

(supported by annexed documents P1-P8, H, P3, P7, I, and J).

5] On 26/11/2021, the Additional District Judge of Kalutara dismissed the

Petitioner's Section 839 application, holding Per Incuriam that the Petitioner

and his wife should be named as accused parties in the contempt matter, and

ruling that the application could not be maintained under the Registration of

Title Act (with the proceedings and Order annexed as K, L, and journal entries

as M).

Page 10 of 17

6] The Petitioner seeks Revision or Restitutio in Integrum from the Court,

asserting that proceeding with the Partition scheme would lead to the

irreversible demolition of his residence's western boundary wall and the loss of

roughly 2 perches of land.

7] Along with the Petition, certified copies of the original Plaint and amended

caption marked as A and A1, a preliminary survey plan (No. 3434) and report

marked as B and B1 (and further detailed as P1, P2, and H), alongside the

judgement and amended Interlocutory Decree marked as C and D are annexed.

Following objections to an initial final scheme of Partition (Plan No. 4005), a

settlement led to an alternative Partition scheme prepared by Licensed Surveyor

D. Lasantha Hewagama (Plan No. 299 dated 10/09/2012), which is annexed to

the Petition as document E.

8] In the above backdrop, the Petitioner prays that this Court be pleased to;

a) Issue Notice on the Respondents;

b) Grant an Interim Order staying further proceedings in the case bearing

No. 7099/P in the District Court of Kalutara until the final determination

of this application;

c) Make Order revising and/or setting aside the Order dated 26/11/2021

which had been delivered by the learned Additional District Judge of

Kalutara in the above case;

d) Make Order adding the Petitioner as a Defendant to this action for the

limited purpose of the Petitioner been permitted to take the necessary

steps required to be granted an exclusion of the land he is entitled to in

terms of the Registration of Title Act No.21 of 1998 from the Corpus sought

to be partitioned in this case;

e) Make Order permitting the Petitioner to take the necessary steps in the

above case to issue a commission to identify the extent of his land that

had been erroneously included into the Corpus;

f) Make Order amending or modifying the judgement and the Interlocutory

Decree entered in the above case in so far as to exclude the extent of the

Petitioner’s land which is more fully described in the third schedule of the

Petition filed on behalf of the Petitioner as prayed for and in terms of his

(Document “H”);

g) Make Order in this case restoring and/or recognising the legal rights that

h) Make Order revising and/or setting aside all steps/ proceedings/ Orders

above case;

i) Grant costs;

j) For such other and further relief Your Lordships’ Court shall deem fit.

9] The Respondents deny the claims made in the Petitioner's application. They

outline numerous preliminary legal objections asserting that the application is

misconceived in law, represents an abuse of the court process to harass the

Respondents, and should be dismissed in limine (at the threshold). They argue

the Petitioner is guilty of laches (unreasonable delay) and has deliberately

suppressed and misrepresented material facts from a related Partition case (No.

7099/P) in the District Court of Kalutara.

Page 12 of 17

10] They show an escalation of this application, where a charge sheet for

contempt of court was issued against the Petitioner and his wife following an

illegal encroachment on an access road. To support their arguments, the

Respondents submit the evidence, including a Primary Courts Procedure Act

Magistrate’s Order (marked V1) and a Village Plan (marked V2), which confirms

the separate nature of their adjoining lands.

11] The Petitioner says that he only discovered the attachment of his land to the

Partition action when a Surveyor and Police Officer entered his land in January

2021 and attempted to remove concrete posts on his Eastern boundary. He

stressed that the Certificate of Title that grants him the right to exclude his land

from the Partition, an alleged contempt of court charge under Section 793 of the

Civil Procedure Code, and a separate revision application (Case No. RV 11/2022)

filed in the High Court of Kalutara challenging a Primary Courts Procedure Act

Order.

12] On perusal of the Partition action No. 7099/P, instituted by the Original

Plaintiffs in the District Court of Kalutara, it is seen that all statutory steps

under the Partition Law, including the registration of “Lis Pendens” and the

preparation of the preliminary plan, were duly followed.

13] The Judgement was delivered on 28/11/2008, determining the Corpus and

shares, against which no Appeal, Revision, or Restitutio-in-Integrum application

was preferred against the said Decree. However, during the final survey, the

Petitioner objected, claiming that the survey encroached upon his land, leading

to contempt of court proceedings against him. The Petitioner then sought relief

in the District Court under Section 839 of the Civil Procedure Code to exclude

his land, leading to the current Revision and Restitutio-in-Integrum application

challenging the District Judge's refusal.

Page 13 of 17

14] The Petitioner’s primary contention is that a Certificate of Title issued under

the Registration of Title Act, No. 21 of 1998, takes supremacy over the Partition

Action and that his land was unlawfully included in the Corpus.

15] This court sees that the Petitioner's claims are uncertain, as his own Petition

and Affidavit conflict regarding the exact extent of land allegedly encroached

upon, varying from "approximately 2 perches" to other irreconcilable extents. It

is further seen that the Petitioner has failed to obtain a commission and prepare

a superimposed plan to identify the overlapping portions, and it would be a mere

assertion that his land, subject to a first-class title, has been encroached upon

in the Partition action.

16] The Petitioner admits that the impugned Partition action was filed in the

year 2000, and his first-class Title Certificate was given well after 07/03/2019.

(vide I). The preliminary survey was carried out on 08-09th June 2001. It is seen

that, at the preliminary survey, the new claimant, Eron Silva, had made his

claim. (Vide B1). Further, when the Partition action was filed, his title had not

been cleared under Registration of Title Act.

17] Moreover, if the Petitioner’s land was encroached, he would have objected at

the preliminary survey. Now, after the Interlocutory Decree was entered, the

Petitioner has stated that approximately two perches were encroached upon.

This claim was made at the final survey, well after the Interlocutory Decree.

Thus, the Petitioner is guilty of laches.

Page 14 of 17

18] In Sri Lanka Insurance Corporation Ltd. v. Shanmugam and Another,

[1995] 1 SLR 55, His Lordship Ranaraja J noted the following, where the party

slept over his rights as;

“The principle on which this court has to act is not whether the court that

gave judgment was tricked into it, but whether one party to action was

deceived by the conduct of the opposing party. Clearly it was not the case

in this instance. It was entirely due to the lack of due diligence on the

part of the petitioner that it took no steps to file answer. Thereafter it

failed to file the necessary papers within the stipulated period to have the

decree set aside. Had the petitioner filed its answer at the proper time, it

could have taken up all the defences which it claims would have deprived

the respondents of the judgment obtained in their favour. Thus, it cannot

now complain of a denial of justice.” [Emphasis is added]

19] It should be noted that the Interlocutory Decree was entered in 2011. The

effect of Interlocutory Decree is considered in Madurapperuma v Wijesundara

and Others 2019 (1) SLR 512, which established that a District Court has no

Jurisdiction to vary its own judgement or restrict a settled Corpus using

inherent powers once an Interlocutory Decree has achieved finality under

Section 48(1) of the Partition Law. His Lordship SAMAYAWARDHENA, J. noted;

“Section 48(1) of the Partition Law invests Interlocutory and Final Decrees

of partition with finality notwithstanding any omission or defect of

procedure (subject to sub-sections (4) and (5) of section 48; and any decision

on any appeal which may be preferred).”

20] The Petitioner is not a party to the Partition action. In Perera vs

Wijewickrama 15 NLR 411, Pereira J. held that;

“the remedy of restitutio in integrum can only be availed of by one who is

actually a party to the contract or legal proceeding in respect of which

restitution is desired.”

21] Thus, the Petitioner cannot directly dispute the final scheme of Partition. In

Menchinahamy vs Munaweera, 52 NLR 409, held as follows;

“The remedy by way of restitutio in integrum is an extraordinary remedy

and is given only under very exceptional circumstances. It is only a party to

a contract or to legal proceedings who can ask for this relief. The remedy

must be sought for with the utmost promptitude. It is not available if the

applicant has any other remedy open to him”

22] As noted above, the Petitioner ought to have made the claim at the

preliminary survey; thus, this remedy is not available for him.

23] The documents show that the District Court of Kalutara action is filed to

Partition a land called “Gabadawaththa” (Document "A"). After a judgement and

an amended Interlocutory Decree were delivered in November 2008 (Documents

"C" and "D") and an alternative Partition scheme was settled in September 2012

(Document "E").

24] I now consider the statutory protection afforded to the Petitioner under the

Registration of Title Act, No. 21 of 1998. The Petitioner holds a Certificate of Title

granting him a First Class Title of Absolute Ownership over Lot No. 152 on Map

Page 16 of 17

No. 530164, issued by the Surveyor General and declared by the Commissioner

of Title Settlement. Under Section 32(1) of the Act, such registration vests

absolute ownership of the land parcel along with all rights and privileges free

from all unexpressed interests. Furthermore, Section 33(1) and 33(2) dictate that

entries in the Title Register serve as conclusive evidence of ownership that

cannot be questioned or assailed in a court of law except as provided directly

within the Act.

25] However, the Petitioner’s land was not subjected to Partition. It was a

boundary dispute between the Petitioner’s land and the land sought to be

Partitioned. Thus, the issue of the title cannot be considered. Though, the

Petitioner emphasizes the supremacy of the Registration of Title Act over the

Partition Act by citing explicit statutory provisions, it has no direct impact when

the issue of boundaries is concerned.

26] Under Section 63 of the Registration of Title Act, No. 21 of 1998, the Partition

Law does not apply to land parcels registered with a First Class Title of Absolute

Ownership or a Second Class Title. If a land is already governed by the Act,

Partition actions cannot be filed to exclude or partition it. The section says;

“63. The Partition Act shall not apply to land parcels registered under this

Act with a First Class Title of Absolute Ownership, or Second Class Title of

Ownership.”

27] This is to say that any disputes regarding boundaries, rights, or exclusion

of lands that hold a Title Certificate must be resolved through the Commissioner

of Title Settlement, rather than through a Partition lawsuit in the District Court.

If any party dissatisfied the decision of the Commissioner of Title Settlement,

that party can appeal to the pertinent District Court.(See section 22 and 29 of

Registration of Title Act, No. 21 of 1998)

28] In the instant application, when the Partition Decree was entered in 2011,

the Petitioner’s title was in dispute; that is, no Title Certificate was granted.

Thus, section 63 of Registration of Title Act, No. 21 of 1998 has no application

Page 17 of 17

to this as the issue is to be decided at the date of institution of the Partition

case. (Vide Talagune v De Livera, [1997] 1 Sri LR 253). Moreover, the dispute

is not for title of each land, it is for boundaries. Thus, supremacy under the Title

Registration Act is immaterial.

29] It is to be noted that in Title Registration Act cases, the District Court is

vested with Appellate Jurisdiction. The Title Registration Act provides a scheme

for dispute resolution. Thus, the Petitioner has an alternative remedy. On the

other hand, the issue of a conflict between the two Acts does not arise due to

this factual uncertainty, and the Petitioner must suffer the consequences of his

own folly. Further, this court is of the view that the inherent powers under

Section 839 of the Civil Procedure Code cannot be widely invoked to displace the

strict statutory framework of the Partition Law.

30] The impugned Partition subject matter and the Petitioner’s land are two

distinct, adjacent lots; no title dispute is involved. Granting relief is a

discretionary remedy of this court. Considering the finality of the Partition

Decree, the guilt of the Petitioner to claim the land at the preliminary survey and

laches for 11 years, for not filing this suit, we decline to exercise the discretion.

31] Therefore, the application is dismissed. No costs.
