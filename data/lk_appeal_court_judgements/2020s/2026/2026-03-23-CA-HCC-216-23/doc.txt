1. The accused-appellant (herein after referred to as the appellant) has

been indicted in the High Court of Colombo in High Court case no 342/2017. The charge in the indictment is as follows;

i. Between the dates of 01st of August, 2009 and 31st of August, 2009, within the jurisdiction of this Court, in Kalaniya, you have conducted an incestuous relationship prohibited under Section 364A (1) of the Penal Code as amended by Penal Code (Amendment) Act No.22 of 1995 by committing the offence of rape on the daughter of your younger sister, namely, Kendagolla Gedara Shahini Nilanthika, who was a person under the age of 16. Thereby you have committed an offence punishable under Section 364(3) of the Penal Code as amended by Penal Code (Amendment) Act No.22 of 1995. 2. At the conclusion of the trial, the learned High Court judge has

convicted the appellant of the charge and sentenced him as follows,

i. 15 years rigorous imprisonment and also imposed a fine of Rs.25,000/- with a term of 1 year rigorous imprisonment, in default. The appellant has also been directed to pay a sum of Rs.250,000/- as compensation to PW01 with a term of two years rigorous imprisonment, in default. 3. The appellant aggrieved by the conviction, the disputed judgment and

the sentencing order has preferred the instant appeal to this Court.

Case of the Prosecution

4. PW01 is the appellant’s niece, specifically the daughter of his maternal

half-sister. This means PW01’s mother and the appellant share a common biological mother but have different fathers.

5. At the time of the alleged incidents in August 2009, PW01 has resided

in Pundaluoya while the appellant has lived in Kiribathgoda. During the school holidays that month, it is alleged that the appellant with the permission of PW01’s parents has brought PW01 to his residence. The stated purpose for this visit had been for the appellant to provide PW01 with English language instruction. PW01’s parents have reportedly agreed to this arrangement without objection largely because, the appellant’s wife was a teacher, lending an air of legitimacy to the educational pretext. 6. It is further alleged that on two separate occasions during PW01’s stay,

when the appellant’s wife was absent from the house, the appellant had engaged in sexual intercourse with PW01. 7. The formal complaint regarding these alleged incidents have been

lodged with the investigating authorities on September 28th, 2010, approximately over a year after the alleged incidents described in the indictment. 8. Following the receipt of the complaint, an investigation has

commenced. PW01 has undergone a medical examination conducted by PW04, i.e. Dr. H.T.D.W. Ariyaratne. The resulting medico-legal report has been tendered as evidence and marked පැ 05. Case of the Appellant

9. The appellant has maintained a firm denial of any sexual involvement

Concerned about PW01’s purported affair with a boy, his sister has asked the appellant to confront PW01 about the same. iii. Did the learned High Court judge err in law by convicting the

11. The process of reporting a crime to the police can often be fraught with

emotional, psychological, and social challenges. Many individuals who experience wrongdoing may hesitate to make a complaint immediately, resulting in delays. Understanding the reasons behind such delays is crucial as well as acknowledging the potential consequences that can arise from them. 12. One of the most prominent reasons individuals delay reporting crimes

is the fear of retaliation from the perpetrator. This fear can be particularly acute if the offender is known to the victim or if the crime has instilled a sense of vulnerability in them. 13. Many victims especially in cases of sexual assault or abuse, may feel

an overwhelming sense of shame or embarrassment, which can prevent them from reporting the incident. Social stigma surrounding certain offences can lead to feelings of isolation or judgement, making victims reluctant to share their experiences with the authorities. 14. Trust plays a critical role in the decision to report a crime. A history of

negative experiences with law enforcement whether personal or observed can lead individuals to doubt that their complaints will be taken seriously or dealt with appropriately. 15. The emotional aftermath of experiencing a crime can be profound.

Victims may experience shock, confusion, or trauma which can cloud their judgement and decision-making abilities causing delays in their realisation and subsequent reporting. 16. Practical obstacles such as lack of transportation, language barriers

or difficulty in accessing information about how to file a complaint can also contribute to delays. 17. The Court having considered the foregoing has examined PW01’s

testimony to ascertain the reasons for the delay in lodging the first complaint. However, this Court found that PW01 has failed to provide a satisfactory explanation for this delay.

18. Courts often assess credibility by reference to the promptness of a

complaint. An unexplained delay can undermine the perceived reliability of the complainant’s narrative particularly where the case depends on oral testimony.

19. In Dr. Ariyaratne’s medico-legal report (පැ 05), he has stated that upon

examination of PW01’s external genitalia, he found no fresh or old injuries. He has further noted that the hymen was fimbriated a normal anatomical variant. 20. These findings do not corroborate PW01’s narrative. However, they

also do not definitively rule out the occurrence of sexual intercourse.

Consequently, the medical evidence is inconclusive meaning it neither confirms nor disproves whether sexual intercourse took place. 21. It is an established principle of evidentiary law, that when a particular

piece of evidence is equally capable of supporting the prosecution’s case and the accused’s narrative, its interpretive ambiguity must be resolved in favour of the accused. Therefore, should Dr. Ariyaratne’s evidence present such vague findings it cannot legitimately be held to corroborate the allegations advanced against the appellant. 22. In Alim vs. Wijesinghe (S.I.Police, Batticaloa) 38 CLW 95, it has been

held,

“Where the same facts are capable of an inference in favour of the accused and also an inference against him, the inference consistent with the accused’s innocence should be preferred.” 23. Accordingly, it not being corroborated by medical evidence or other

credible circumstances, PW01’s narrative submitted after an unexplained delay lacks reliability and cannot be acted upon.

24. I shall now consider the principles relevant to the evaluation of a dock

statement. When evaluating dock statement, the following guidelines must be considered;

i. A dock statement should be considered as evidence subject to infirmities that it was not sworn testimony and not tested by cross-examination. ii. If the docks statement is believed, it must be acted upon. iii. If the dock statement creates a reasonable doubt in the prosecution’s case, the defence of the accused must succeed. iv. The dock statement of one accused person should not be considered against the other accused. 25. In The Queen v D. G. De S. Kularatne, 71 NLR 529, it has been held,

“....when an unsworn statement is made by the accused from the dock, the jurors must be informed that such statement must be looked upon as evidence, subject however to the infirmity that the accused had deliberately refrained from giving sworn testimony. But the jury must also be directed that (a) if they believe the unsworn statement it must be acted upon, (b) if it raises a reasonable doubt in their minds about the case for the prosecution, the defence must succeed, and (c) that it should not be used against another accused.”

26. Although the learned High Court Judge has … the legal principles

governing the evaluation of the dock statement in the disputed judgement, he has failed to apply those principles when assessing the appellant’s own dock statement. Moreover, the disputed judgement does not reveal the learned High Court Judge’s definitive position on the issue.

27. As discussed above, the testimony of PW01 is not convincing and

unreliable. The offence stated in the charge, was allegedly committed approximately fifteen years ago. In those circumstances, I believe that this matter is not suitable for a retrial. 28. In the case of The Queen vs. G.K.Jayasinghe 69 NLR 314, Sansoni, J,

has stated,

“…we have considered whether we should order a new trial in this case. We do not take that course, because there has been a lapse of three years since the commission of the offences, and because of our own view of the unreliable nature of the accomplice’s evidence on which alone the prosecution case rests. We accordingly direct that the judgment of acquittal be entered.” 29. Accordingly, I am not inclined to order a retrial.

30. Accordingly, I set aside the conviction, disputed judgement, and

sentencing order and acquit the appellant of the charge. Appeal allowed I make no order regarding costs. 31. The Registrar of this Court is directed to communicate the judgment

to the High Court of Colombo for compliance.
