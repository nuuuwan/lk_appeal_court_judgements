Judgment

Amal Ranaraja. J,

1. The accused-appellant (hereinafter referred to as the ‘appellant’) has

been indicted in the High Court of Nuwara Eliya in Case No. HCR

21/17. The charge in the indictment is as follows;

01. That between the 01st of June, 2011 and 22nd of September,

2011, within the jurisdiction of this Court, in Ambewela Cattle

Farm, you have committed rape on one Devadasan Nirosha, a

minor under 16 years of age; thereby you have committed an

offence punishable under Section 364(2)(e) of the Penal Code

as amended by Penal Code (Amendment) Act No. 22 of 1995.

2. At the conclusion of the trial the appellant has been convicted of the

charge and sentenced as follows;

i. Imposed a term of 10 years Rigorous Imprisonment and also

fined Rs. 15,000.00 with a term of 3 months Simple

Imprisonment, in default of the payment of the same.

3

ii. The appellant has also been directed to pay a sum of Rs.

500,000.00 as compensation to PW01 with a term of 3 years

Simple Imprisonment, in default of the payment of the same.

3. The appellant, aggrieved by the conviction, disputed judgement and the

sentencing order has preferred the instant appeal to this Court.

Case of the Prosecution

4. At the time the incident referred to in the charge occurred, PW01 has

been alone in the house where she lived, attending to the garden. Close

to noon, she has gone into the house to have lunch and has walked

towards the fridge to fetch some milk. At that moment, she has seen

the appellant, in the room she slept in the house. The appellant has

first questioned PW01 about the whereabouts of the other household

members, and also when they were expected back. Thereafter, the

appellant has proceeded to undress PW01 as described, undressed

himself, and sexually abused PW01 in the room where he was.

5. Following the submission of a complaint, investigations into the

incident have commenced. PW01 presented for a medical examination

which has been conducted by PW11, Dr. N.A.S.P. Wijeratne, an

additional Judicial Medical Officer. The resulting medico-legal report

has been produced as evidence marked පැ 05.

Case of the Appellant

6. The appellant has maintained that a dispute arose between him and

the members of PW01’s household over a sum of Rs. 5000.00, which he

had borrowed and failed to return, and such dispute escalated to a

quarrel.

4

7. One of the fundamental objectives of cross-examination is to test the

accuracy of a witness’s testimony. This is achieved through strategic

questioning designed to expose contradictions or weaknesses in the

testimony. By eliciting clarifications or alternative narratives, the cross-

examiner seeks to highlight any potential distortions in the witness’s

account, thereby undermining its overall reliability.

8. Furthermore, cross-examination serves to reveal any biases or motives

that may influence a witness’s testimony. A witness may have personal

relationships or other incentives that colour their perspectives. Through

pointed questioning, a cross-examiner could illuminate these factors,

allowing a judge to assess the witness’s motivation and the weight to be

given to their testimony. Additionally, cross-examination plays a

significant role in shaping the narrative of the trial. By effectively

challenging a witness, the cross examiner can create doubt regarding

the opposing party’s case.

9. PW01 in her short history to Dr. Wijeratne has stated as follows;

Vide Page 294 of the Case Brief

PW01 has stated that she experienced repeated sexual assault by Sali

during the three months leading up to the medical examination.

Additionally, she has reported an incident of sexual intercourse by Bega

subsequently. Her narrative thus refers to two distinct individuals and

details sexual abuse committed by each.

5

10. In these circumstances, it would be quite fair and crucial for the counsel

representing the appellant to cross examine PW01 thoroughly. This

questioning should focus on the individual’s named in her account and

their precise involvement in the alleged incidents. This process is

essential because it offers a direct opportunity to uncover any

contradictions with other evidence or statements, explore alternative

versions of events that might have been overlooked, or highlight

potential distortions within a narrative, which could stem from bias or

misinterpretation. Successfully doing so would challenge the overall

reliability of PW01’s testimony by introducing reasonable doubt about

its accuracy and completeness.

11. Despite the significant background information presented in the

narrative of PW01, the counsel for the appellant has unfortunately been

prevented from exploring these crucial matters during the cross-

examination.

අධිකරණයෙන්

ඒ ප්‍රශ්නෙට අවසර යනායෙමි. සාක්ිකාරිෙයේ චරිතෙ පිලිබඳව අගතගාමී

ප්‍රශ්නෙක් වන බැවින් එම ප්‍රශ්නෙට අවසර යනායෙමි.

12. This restriction has denied the appellant’s counsel a fundamental

opportunity to pose lawful and highly relevant questions, which were

truly vital to establish the appellant’s defence. Consequently, this flaw

has amounted to a denial of fair trial a situation directly stemming from

a misdirection by the learned High Court Judge. Notably, the learned

Additional Solicitor General, appearing on behalf of the respondent

upholding the highest traditions, has conceded to this fact.

6

13. In such circumstances, I set aside the conviction, disputed judgement,

together with the sentencing order and I order a retrial.

14. The learned High Court Judge in Nuwara Eliya is required expedite the

matter.

15. I make no order regarding costs.

Retrial ordered.

16. The Registrar of this Court is directed to send this Judgement to the

High Court in Nuwara Eliya for compliance.
