26. (1) Subject as hereinafter provided, and the rights granted by a licence issued under this Act, the ownership of minerals is hereby vested in the Republic, notwithstanding any right of ownership or otherwise which any person may have to the soil on, in or, under which minerals are found or situated. (2) Any person who discovers any mineral shall forthwith inform the Director thereof. The necessity for a party to obtain a licence to explore for, mine, transport, process, store, trade in and export minerals arises from section 28 of the said Act, and, section 26(1) thereof is reproduced below;

28. (1) No person shall explore for, mine, transport, process, store, trade in or export any minerals except under the authority of, or otherwise than in accordance with, a licence issued in that behalf under the provisions of this Act and the regulations made thereunder: The GSMB has been identified and established as the licensing authority in section 34 of the Mines and Minerals Act in the following terms;

34. Every application for a licence Act shall be made to the Bureau in such form and shall contain such particulars and shall be accompanied by such documents as may be prescribed and the prescribed fee.

It is proposed to examine the entitlement of the two Petitioners in the instant applications to the reliefs sought in respect of the licenses sought by them within the statutory framework set out by the Mines and Minerals Act. In considering such entitlement of a party, the case presented by such party as well as the objections raised by the other parties to the grant of such reliefs will be considered. Entitlement of Puttalam Ilmenite (Petitioner in CA Writ 169/2024) As reflected in paragraphs (c) and (d) to the prayer of its petition, the principal substantive reliefs sought by the Petitioner are directions to the GSMB to extend the “Retention Licence” granted to it for “one further year from October 2024 onwards” and to grant the Petitioner an Industrial mining licence. I wish to now address the Petitioner’s entitlements to such reliefs. Retention License In the first instance, I wish to point out that there is no category of licenses called a “Retention Licence” contemplated in the Mines and Minerals Act, No. 33 of 1992 (as amended) and the Regulations made thereunder, and, as submitted in the written submissions filed on behalf of the 1st to 3rd Respondents. What is contemplated is the conferment of a “retention period,” as “a regulatory mechanism of limited duration, intended solely to preserve a mineral discovery while enabling the applicant to satisfy clearly defined statutory prerequisites prior to being considered for a mining license.”3 Thus, I will consider the reference to “Retention License” in the petition to denote an extension of the retention period. The Exploration Licence EL/140 (P11) was issued for a period of 24 months from 20.08.2021 to 19.08.2023. Thereafter, upon a request made by the Petitioner on 20.06.2023 (P18), a retention period for the grid units covered by the said Exploration License was granted by the GSMB for a period of 01 year from 10.10.2023 to 09.10.2024 (P19). It is not in dispute that the duration of the said retention period has been further extended during the pendency of this application until 08.10.2025. The 1st to 3rd Respondents have pointed out that the maximum permissible period that an Exploration Licence could be retained is 02 years in terms of Regulation 4(14)(e) of the Mining (Licensing) Regulations No. 1 of 1993, as amended by Gazette Notification No. 2313/37 dated 04.01.2023 (R2). Thus, the said Respondents have taken up the position that the consideration of such relief has become academic. Since the retention period of the Exploration Licence had been extended for a period of 01 year, as specifically sought in paragraph (c) of the prayer to the petition, and, upon consideration of the aforementioned position taken up by the 1st to 3rd Respondents, I am of the view that it would not be necessary for this Court to consider the said relief any longer.

Upon consideration of the aforementioned positions taken up by the parties, I am of the view that the 03 conditions in section 48(3) of the Mines and Minerals Act referred to by the Petitioner have to be construed and interpreted taking into account all applicable provisions of the said Act and the regulations made thereunder, including prescribed forms and licence conditions, which constitute one “integrated statutory scheme” as articulated by the 1st to 3rd Respondents. I will proceed to examine the respective positions of the parties regarding the conditions set out in section 48(3) above and to set out my findings thereon. 1. Determination of Commercial Viability The Petitioner states that it had submitted its Final Exploration Report under Licence EL/410 to the GSMB by letter dated 30.09.2021 (P12), and, that, the GSMB had evaluated the said Report, and, by letter dated 22.10.2021 (P13), communicated its findings to the Central Environmental Authority (CEA) that the Bureau had recommended the project as an “economically viable project for the mining and processing of mineral sands.” The Petitioner has sought to rely on such findings by the GSMB to demonstrate the commercial viability of mining, as contemplated in section 48(3). It is observed that the Final Exploration Report referred to in the letter (P12) itself has not been submitted to this Court, and, thus, I am not in a position to make any observations on the contents thereof. However, the fact that it has been evaluated by the GSMB and that a recommendation has been made thereon by the GSMB that the project was economically viable is evident by the letter (P13). Asha Minerals has taken up the position that the Exploration Licence EL/410, upon which the aforementioned Final Exploration Report was submitted, has been granted illegally and nothing can flow from an illegality. Such a position has been taken on several bases; firstly, the Petitioner had assisted M/s Illuka Resources Lanka (Pvt) and M/s Illuka Lanka PQ (Pvt) Limited in exploration activities during the pendency of Exploration Licence EL/170, without having a valid licence itself in violation of section 28 read with section 63(1)(a) of the Mines and Minerals Act; secondly EL/410 had been granted to the Petitioner in spite of such illegal conduct and based on its relationship to the licence holder of EL/170, which was in violation of section 13(d)(i) of the Act, which contemplates the receipt of an application being received or advertising and calling for expressions of interest; and, thirdly, the Final Exploration Report

16

had been submitted within 42 days of the issuance of the Licence EL/410, which is insufficient for any genuine exploration and determination to be made that a mineral may be mined on a commercial basis.8 However, it is observed that the aforementioned objections to the validity of the Licence EL/410 have only been raised in the objections filed by Asha Minerals in these proceedings, by which time the Final Exploration Report based on such licence too had been submitted (30.09.2021) and evaluated (22.10.2021) and the 24-month validity period of the Exploration Licence had expired (19.08.2023). In such circumstances, I am of the view that, it would neither be appropriate or necessary to consider such objections at this belated stage, whatever their merits may be. In such circumstances, it would be reasonable for this Court to be guided by the findings and the recommendation made by the GSMB on the Final Exploration Report submitted by the Petitioner on the commercial viability of the project. 2. It is incumbent upon this Court to consider the merits or otherwise of the aforementioned argument presented by the Petitioner. In relation to the first proposition, it is my view that the statutory right conferred by section 51 of the Mines and Minerals Act is not absolute or independent and is contingent upon the consent of the owner of the land. The interpretation sought to be placed by the Petitioner does not take into account the purpose and effect of the proviso to the said section. Bindra in Interpretation of Statutes has commented on the proper function of a proviso in the following terms;

The proper function of a proviso is that it qualifies the generality of the main enactment by providing an exception and taking out, as it were, from the main enactment, a portion which, but for the proviso, would fall within the main enactment.41 I am of the view that section 51 should be read and interpreted in a manner that takes into account and gives effect to the contents of the proviso thereto. The proviso to section 51 of the Act essentially serves the purpose of establishing the relationship between the prospective licensee and the owner of the land, which is not only critical for the successful implementation of the licence but is also a matter that should be clarified to the GSMB at the point the issuance of the licence itself is considered. I am also unable to agree with the second proposition advanced by the Petitioner. It is my view that sections 61(2) and (3) have to be read in conjunction with section 51 and not disjunctively. If the interpretation advanced by the Petitioner is accepted, then section 51 would become redundant. The provisions of sections 61(2) and (3) are intended to apply to situations where certain requirements are imposed by the GSMB on the surface owner or occupant and are not complied with after a licence is granted. The use of the word “shall” in section 51 and the word “may” in section 61(2) too militate against the notion that a licensee enjoys an absolute right of access and that the rights of the owner are of no consequence. I am of the view that another factor that cannot be ignored in interpreting the aforementioned clauses is that section 61 relates to the application of standards and procedures of the National Environmental Act, whilst section 51 addresses the issue of access and possession of the site. With regard to the third proposition, I am inclined to the view that the interpretation advanced by the Petitioner has the effect of distorting and rendering the proviso to section 51 redundant. The clear intention of the proviso is to require a prospective licensee who is not the owner of the land to obtain the consent of the owner. The provisions of clause 6(b) of the prescribed form for an industrial mining license application (Form 3.1) reinforce such view. In this context, it is further observed that the words “owner or state organization…. in possession” may have been used as there may be situations where state organizations may be in occupation of lands without owning the same. However, there could be no such doubt in the instant case since the Petitioner has expressly accepted the fact that the SLCC is the owner of the subject land in its possession. Hence, I am of the view that it was incumbent upon the Petitioner to obtain the consent of the SLCC to access the site. It is clear that the Petitioner has failed to obtain such consent, as required by section 51 read with section 34
