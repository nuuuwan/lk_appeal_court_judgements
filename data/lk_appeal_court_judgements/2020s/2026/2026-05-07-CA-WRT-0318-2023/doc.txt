K. Priyantha Fernando, J. (CA)

1. The Petitioner instituted the present application by Petition dated 12th June 2023 seeking,

inter alia, Writs of Certiorari to quash the decisions, directives, and charges contained in

document “P19” and the proceedings of the inquiry conducted by the 1st to

3rd Respondents against the Somawathie Saparamadu Trust (hereinafter referred to as

“SST”) and/or the Petitioner, as well as Writs of Prohibition preventing 1st to

7th Respondents from conducting or continuing such inquiry.

5

2. An interim order staying all proceedings had been granted in the first

instance. Furthermore, on 9th February 2026, the Attorney General informed Court

that 1st to 3rdRespondents had resigned and that no new panel of inquirers had been

appointed. The Petitioner thereafter had sought an extension of the interim relief, which

the 8th Respondent opposed. This Court then directed both parties to file written

submissions, resulting in the present synopsis.

FACTUAL MATRIX:

3. The Court observed that the genesis of the matter lay in a complaint lodged by the 8th

Respondent with the CID on 28th February 2014 alleging misappropriation of SST funds.

The CID investigation, supervised by the Magistrate’s Court of Nugegoda, had concluded

without adverse findings against the Petitioner. Instead, one A.D.S. Wickrama, an

employee tasked with handling SST finances, had been indicted in the High Court of

Colombo for criminal breach of trust involving Rs. 10,446,477. The Petitioner maintained

that he had no involvement in Wickrama’s acts.

4. Simultaneously, the 8th Respondent had lodged an identical complaint with the NGO

Secretariat, leading to inquiries under the Voluntary Social Service Organizations Act.

The Petitioner alleged that these inquiries had been contrived and biased, influenced by

the 8th Respondent, including the appointment of Mr. Ajith Kumara, a close associate of

the 8th Respondent, to the Board of Inquiry. The Petitioner had challenged these inquiries

in CA/WRT/448/15 and CA/WRT/229/18. The Court of Appeal in 2020 had directed

disclosure of complaints and statements prior to recommencement of inquiry, affirming

the Petitioner’s right to respond and participate.

6

5. Despite these directions, on 11th April 2023, the Petitioner received a “Charge Sheet”

(marked P19) alleging personal financial misappropriation. The Petitioner contended that

this document is ultra vires as the Voluntary Social Service Organizations Act, which

empowered a Board of Inquiry only to inquire into and report on organizations, not to

charge individuals. The notice had also violated Regulation 7(1)(b) by providing only

seven days’ notice instead of the statutorily required fourteen days.

6. The Petitioner further submitted that the inquiry had been tainted with mala fides, bias,

reliance on potentially forged documents, and exclusion of the Petitioner from the key

hearings. It was emphasized that the subject matter overlapped with the pending High

Court indictment, thereby rendering the Board’s proceedings an abuse of process and

prejudicial to ongoing judicial proceedings.

POSITION OF THE PETITIONER:

7. The Petitioner contended that that there had been jurisdictional overreach in the instant

matter. The issuance of the Charge Sheet (P19) had been ultra vires and in contravention

to the Voluntary Social Service Organizations Act and its Regulations. Furthermore, the

Board of Inquiry had been a fact finding administrative body with no statutory authority

to prosecute or charge individuals.

8. The Petitioner further contended that the inquiry and the manner in which it was

conducted was a violation of natural justice. The inquiry had been arbitrary, unreasonable,

and procedurally improper. The Petitioner had been denied adequate notice, documentary

evidence, and participation rights, amounting to a violation of natural justice. It was

7

contended that not only was there a violation of the principles of natural justice, but an

abuse of process. The Petitioner submitted that the subject matter had overlapped with the

criminal indictment for which a case was already pending before the High Court. The

Board’s attempt to record evidence from the same witnesses had constituted a parallel

proceeding, risking conflicting findings and witness interference, thereby amounting to an

abuse of process.

9. The Petitioner also specifically pleaded mala fide and bias. It was argued that the inquiry

had been contrived, influenced by the 8th Respondent, and designed to target the

Petitioner personally rather than investigate the SST. The reliance on photocopies of

documents of dubious origin, potentially forged, underscored the sham nature of the

proceedings.

10. Finally, the Petitioner argued that Writs of Certiorari and Prohibition were the

appropriate remedies to quash ultra vires decisions and prevent future unlawful acts.

The Petitioner submitted that as the balance of convenience lies in his favor, a failure to

extend the interim order would subject him to irreparable harm through a biased and

unauthorized prosecution, rendering the final relief nugatory.

11. The Petitioner primarily prayed for the following relief:

− Writs of Certiorari to quash the Charge Sheet (P19) and the impugned inquiry

proceedings;

− Writs of Prohibition to prevent Respondents 1–7 from continuing the inquiry; and

− Extension of the interim order until final determination of the application, to

safeguard his rights and prevent irreparable prejudice.

8

12. In totality, the Petitioner submitted that the inquiry and Charge Sheet were ultra vires,

mala fide, and an abuse of process. The continuation of such proceedings would have

undermined the rule of law and prejudiced pending judicial proceedings. Accordingly, the

Petitioner prayed for the extension of the interim order until final determination of the

application.

POSITION OF THE 8TH RESPONDENT:

13. The Respondent contended that the Petitioner, being a trustee of

the Somawathie Saparamadu Trust (SST), has raised allegations of lack of jurisdiction,

inconsistency with statutory provisions, violation of natural justice, bias, mala fides, and

irregularity of notices and proceedings. The Respondent denied these allegations insofar

as they implicate him and argued that the affairs of the SST have long been subject to

sustained litigation, criminal investigations, and indictments, including proceedings

before the Magistrate’s Courts of Nugegoda and Galle, as well as writ applications

CA/WRT/448/2015 and CA/WRT/229/2018.

14. The Respondent submitted that the interim order dated 13th June 2023, restraining the 1st

to 3rd Respondents from proceeding with the inquiry, was personal to those office holders.

The Respondent argued that since the Chairman had resigned, the Board as constituted

ceased to exist, and therefore the interim order had lost its factual basis. The Respondent

contended that the order cannot bind a future Board yet to be constituted, nor operate in a

vacuum detached from its original foundation. He further submitted that the continuation

9

of such restraint would be unwarranted, especially in light of documentary material

revealing serious irregularities in the financial administration of the SST.

15. The Respondent argued that indictments and statements recorded by the Financial Crimes

Investigation Division demonstrate misappropriation of funds amounting to Rs.

10,446,477.00 /- by an employee, with the Petitioner’s involvement as approving officer

of cheques. He submitted that police investigations before the Magistrate’s Court of

Nugegoda revealed fabricated bills purportedly issued by vendors, while deposits made

with Desano Investments Limited, a dormant family company unauthorized to accept

deposits, further evidenced irregularities. The Respondent contended that these materials

underscore the necessity of a proper inquiry into the affairs of the SST.

16. The Respondent further argued that the Petitioner’s challenge to the charge sheet marked

P19 is untenable, as the Court of Appeal in CA/WRT/229/2018 had already considered a

substantially similar charge sheet (P16) and found no fault with it, observing that it was

merely a synopsis of the complaint. He submitted that the Court had expressly directed

that the inquiry be commenced and concluded expeditiously, but the present ex parte stay

order has frustrated that direction for nearly six years.

17. In law, the Respondent contended that interim orders are intended to preserve the status

quo and cannot operate indefinitely or against non-existent entities. The Respondent

relied on the authority of Duwearatchi v. Vincent Perera, Weerasooriya

v. Sidambaram Chetty [1984] 2 Sri L.R 94, to argue that interim relief depends on

necessity, balance of convenience, and prevention of irreparable injury. The

Respondent submitted that cessation of the interim order will not render nugatory any

10

final relief, nor cause irreparable injury, and that the balance of

convenience favours vacating the order. It was further argued that judicial certainty

requires that interim restraints be directed only against named parties, and cannot extend

by implication to future Boards not before Court.

18. Accordingly, the Respondent submitted that the interim order dated 13th June 2023,

having been issued ex parte against specific individuals, cannot survive the cessation of

that Board, and ought to be vacated or declared to have ceased to operate.

ANALYSIS:

19. It is common ground that 1st to 3rd Respondents have resigned from their positions and

that the Minister under whose preview the Secretariat of Non-Governmental

Organizations comes has not taken steps as of date to appoint a fresh panel of inquirers in

out of whom a board of inquiry would be appointed in terms of the Voluntary Social

Service Organizations (Registration and Supervision) Act No. 31 of 1980 (as amended).

20. The central question before this Court is whether the interim order directed against

specifically named office holders can survive the cessation of their office and bind a

future statutory body not yet constituted. This should be considered in light of the

statutory framework governing the constitution and functioning of a Board of Inquiry

under the Voluntary Social Service Organizations (Registration and Supervision) Act No.

31 of 1980.

21. Section 10 and 11 of the Act provide the legal foundation for the appointment and

constitution of a Board of Inquiry. The relevant provision read as follows:

11

“10. Where, in respect of a voluntary organization registered under this Act, any

allegation of fraud, or misappropriation is made by any person, the Minister may

refer such matter to a Board of Inquiry.

11. (1) The Minister may appoint a Panel consisting of six persons of standing who

are not public officers.

(2) The Minister may, for the purpose of this Act, constitute a Board of Inquiry or

Boards of Inquiry, each consisting of three members chosen from the Panel. The

Minister may nominate one member to be the Chairman of a Board of Inquiry.

(3) The members of a Board of Inquiry shall be paid such remuneration as may be

determined by the Minister in consultation with the Minister in charge of the

subject of Finance.”.

22. Having outlined the statutory nature of the Board of Inquiry, it becomes necessary to

consider the legal principles governing the grant and continuation of interim stay orders.

The governing principle relating to interim stay orders has been enunciated in [1992] 2

Sri L.R. by Gunawardena J., as follows:

“It is important to note here that, when this Court grants interim relief by way of a

stay order, it does not expect the parties to take steps which would substantially

alter the rights of the parties before Court, as such Orders are issued, more to

ensure that status quo is maintained between the parties, till the application is

finally determined”.

23. Furthermore, the nature and scope of the discretion underlying the grant of interim stay

orders has been considered by Seneviratne J in Duwearachchi and Another v. Vincent

12

Perera and Others, wherein His Lordship has referred to the observations of Bertram, C.J.

in Weerasooriya v. Sidambaram Chetty 8 C.W.R 238 as follows:

“the interests of justice therefore required that a stay order be made as an interim

measure. It would not be correct to judge such stay orders in the same strict

manner as final orders. Interim orders by their very nature must depend a great

deal on a judge’s opinion as to the necessity for interim action.”

24. In the instant case, interim order has been issued to preserve the status quo between the

parties until the application was supported and determined, but not intended to suspend

statutory powers in the abstract nor to operate indefinitely. Upon the resignation of the

Chairman, the Board as then constituted has ceased to function. Consequently, the very

status quo which this Court intended to preserve is no longer in existence.

25. It is important to note that the interim orders depend fundamentally on necessity, and

when the circumstances that justified interim intervention have altered or ceased, the

order cannot continue divorced from its factual basis.

26. In the present case, the interim order dated 13.06.2023 reads as follows: “In the

circumstances, the Court is of the view that an order should be issued in terms of clause 2

(1) of the Court of Appeal Rules to maintain the status quo. Further, court directs that the

1st, 2nd, and 3rd Respondents not to proceed with the inquiry fixed on 14th and 16th of June

until the petitioner would support this application.”

27. The plain reading of the above interim order makes it manifest that the restraint operates

only against the 1st, 2nd and 3rd Respondents personally. It does not refer to the Board of

13

Inquiry as a juristic body, nor does it extend to successors, assigns, office holders, or any

future reconstituted Board.

28. It was strenuously contended by the learned President’s Counsel for the Respondents that

an order of Court cannot be extended by implication or inference beyond its express terms

and such certainty is wholly absent in respect of any newly constituted Board. The

interim order has been issued to preserve the status quo between the parties as they then

stood.

29. It is common ground that the Board against whom the restraint operated has ceased to

exist by reason of the resignation of its Chairman. Moreover, the two of the main reliefs

in the prayers (d) and (e) are against the 1st, 2nd and 3rd Respondents who are no longer

constitute Board of Inquiry since there is no Board is in existence. The prayer (d) is to

grant a Writ of Certiorari quashing the proceedings of the inquiry conducted by the 1st to

3rd Respondents against the Petitioner; Prayer (e) is to grant a Writ of Prohibition

preventing any of the 1st to 3rd Respondents from conducting or continuing with an

inquiry. Since there is no longer any Board of Inquiry is in existence, the Petitioner is no

way affected or prejudiced by not extending Interim Order prayed for in prayer (g) of the

Petition. Thus, the balance of convenience favours the Petitioner. In fact, the interim

order granted on 13th June 2023 has lapsed from the time of cessation of the Board of

inquiry.

30. For the above circumstances, it is my considered view that until and unless the Minister

appoints three new members constituting a Board of Inquiry, the question of extending

14

the interim order does not arise as it (the interim order) stands dissolved/ vacated at the

point of cessation of the Board of Inquiry.

31. Accordingly, the application of the Petitioner is refused and no order is made with regard

to costs.
