Adithya Patabendige, J.

The Petitioner, Ecotech Lanka Limited, invokes the writ jurisdiction of this Court seeking,

inter alia, mandates in the nature of writs of certiorari, prohibition and mandamus in respect

of the termination of a Lease Agreement entered into with the 1st Respondent, the Kandy

Municipal Council (KMC), concerning the rehabilitation and operation of the Gohagoda

dumpsite and the implementation of an Integrated Solid Waste Management Project. The

Petitioner challenges the decision to terminate the said lease and the subsequent actions taken

by the Respondents in relation to the management of the dumpsite. The Respondents oppose

the application, contending that the termination was justified due to the Petitioner’s failure to

comply with contractual obligations, environmental requirements, and operational standards.

13

Confinement to Prayer (b)

When this application was taken up for argument on 13th January 2026, learned Counsel for

the Petitioner informed the Court that the Petitioner would confine its case solely to prayer

(b) of the Petition and would not pursue the remaining reliefs. Prayer (b) seeks to impugn the

document marked P41.

Factual Background

The Petitioner is a limited liability company incorporated in Sri Lanka and claims to possess

expertise in integrated solid waste management systems.

The 1st Respondent is the Kandy Municipal Council, the local authority responsible for

municipal administration within the Kandy Municipal limits, including the collection and

disposal of solid waste. The 2nd Respondent is the Municipal Commissioner and Competent

Authority of the KMC, while the remaining Respondents are public authorities and officials

connected with provincial administration and environmental regulation.

For several decades, municipal waste generated in the Kandy Municipal area was disposed of

at the Gohagoda dumpsite near the Mahaweli River. The accumulation of waste over a

prolonged period has resulted in serious environmental and public health concerns, including

air pollution, water contamination, and nuisance to surrounding communities.

To address these issues, KMC took steps to establish an Integrated Solid Waste Management

System for the Kandy Municipal area. Following preliminary studies conducted with the

assistance of the University of Peradeniya, the 1st Respondent called for Expressions of

Interest from private sector entities on 28th April 2008, marked 1R1, for the rehabilitation of

the dumpsite and the implementation of a comprehensive waste management project.

Several proposals were received, including the Petitioner's. Upon evaluation, the Petitioner

was selected to undertake the project. The Petitioner relies on a document marked P3 as

evidencing the award of the project. The 1st and 2nd Respondents, however, contend that the

said document is merely a report submitted to a municipal committee and not a formal Letter

of Award.

14

Subsequently, on 18th January 2010, the Petitioner and the 1st Respondent entered into Lease

Agreement No. 2185 whereby approximately 4.9649 hectares of land comprising the

Gohagoda dumpsite were leased to the Petitioner for a period of thirty years. The lease

agreement was marked as P4, and the English translation as P4a.

Under the terms of the lease agreement, the Petitioner undertook to rehabilitate the dumpsite

and establish an integrated solid waste management system, including facilities for waste

processing, compost production, and energy generation. The 1st Respondent undertook to

permit use of the land for the project, supply municipal waste, and provide assistance,

including relocation of occupants residing within the leased premises.

The implementation of the project required environmental clearance from the Central

Environmental Authority (CEA). The Petitioner submitted the relevant Environmental Impact

Assessment documentation marked P5, and approval was granted by the CEA on 14th

November 2011, marked P6, subject to conditions, valid for a period of three years.

Following the grant of approval, the Petitioner commenced operations at the site in or about

November 2011. The Petitioner states that the dumpsite was at that time in a severely

degraded condition due to decades of uncontrolled dumping.

The Petitioner asserts that delays in implementing certain components of the project,

including waste-to-energy facilities, were attributable to factors beyond its control, including

technical difficulties relating to the composition of waste, financial constraints, and the

continued presence of residents within the project area.

The Respondents dispute these assertions and state that the Petitioner failed to implement the

project in accordance with the feasibility study and the obligations under the Lease

Agreement. They further contend that the Petitioner delayed obtaining regulatory approvals,

failed to achieve the required environmental standards, and did not fulfil several contractual

conditions, including operational targets and payment obligations. The 1st and 2nd

Respondents also state that substantial assistance was provided by the KMC to facilitate the

project, including machinery, infrastructure support, relocation of residents, and financial

assistance (vide 1R2 and 1R3).

15

The 1st and 2nd Respondents further contend that the Environmental Impact Assessment

approval granted to the Petitioner expired on 14th November 2014 and was not renewed

thereafter, and that the Petitioner continued operations without valid environmental clearance.

It is also their position that the sanitary conditions at the dumpsite remained unsatisfactory

and that the project failed to operate in an environmentally acceptable manner.

According to the 1st and 2nd Respondents, the project subsequently deteriorated, resulting in

malfunctioning waste management operations, continued accumulation of garbage, and

environmental pollution. They state that, due to a lack of funds and failure to meet project

objectives, the Petitioner abandoned the project in or about October 2015 without notice,

necessitating the 1st Respondent's (1R6, 1R7, 1R8) takeover of the dumpsite's management.

The 1st and 2nd Respondents also refer to legal proceedings instituted by the CEA and to

actions relating to public nuisance arising from conditions prevailing at the site (1R5).

In terms of the Lease Agreement, disputes arising between the parties were referred to the

relevant provincial authorities. Following consultations, the 3rd Respondent and the Secretary

to the Ministry of Local Government of the Central Province conveyed their decision dated

18th September 2017, marked P40, regarding the cancellation of the project, and thereafter

the Lease Agreement was terminated by the 1st Respondent, dated 19th September 2017,

marked P41.

The 1st and 2nd Respondents maintain that the termination was effected due to the Petitioner’s

failure to obtain necessary environmental approvals for a considerable period of time and

failure to comply with the conditions stipulated in the Lease Agreement. They further state

that the continuation of the project had become untenable in view of the increasing

accumulation of waste and the environmental risks posed to the city of Kandy.

Aggrieved by the termination of the Lease Agreement and the subsequent actions taken by

the Respondents in respect of the Gohagoda dumpsite, the Petitioner instituted the present

application seeking relief in the exercise of the writ jurisdiction of this Court.

16

Clause 3(2)(i) of the Lease Agreement

The material before the Court discloses that P40 embodies the decision of the 3rd Respondent

taken pursuant to Clause 3(2)(i) of the Lease Agreement entered into between the Petitioner

and the 1st Respondent. Under that clause, any problematic situation arising in connection

with the agreement was to be referred to the Commissioner of Local Government of the

Central Province, the 3rd Respondent, and the Secretary of the relevant Ministry, who were

empowered to take the necessary action. It is common ground that the dispute between the

parties was so referred, following which the decision contained in P40 was communicated.

Consequent upon the decision of the 3rd Respondent, Document P41 was issued by the 1st

Respondent.2

Contractual Nature

Moreover, the dispute between the parties arises entirely out of the Lease Agreement marked

P4, entered into between the Petitioner and the 1st Respondent. The rights and obligations of

the parties, including the mechanism for resolving disputes, are governed by the terms of that

agreement. Clause 3(2)(i) expressly provides for the reference of disputes to the competent

provincial authorities. The impugned documents P40 and P41 are referable to actions taken

within that contractual framework.

No statutory provision has been identified that confers upon the Respondents any special

public law power in respect of the termination of the lease. Nor has the Petitioner

demonstrated that the impugned actions involve the performance of a statutory duty owed to

the public at large. The dispute concerns the performance, continuation, and termination of a

commercial arrangement relating to the operation of a solid waste management project. Such

matters fall squarely within the realm of private law.

It is a trite law that the writ jurisdiction under Article 140 of the Constitution concerns the

legality of administrative or quasi-judicial action in the public law domain. It is not intended

to provide a forum for adjudicating disputes arising from contractual obligations or for

enforcing private rights, particularly where an adequate remedy lies in ordinary civil

proceedings.

17

In the case of Lanka Marine Services (Pvt) Ltd. v Sri Lanka Ports Authority and 7 others

2010 (B.L.R.) 187 states as follows.

a) a distinction needs to be drawn between duties which are statutory and duties arising

merely from contract. Contractual duties are enforceable as matters of private law by

ordinary contractual remedies.

b) certiorari lies where statutory authorities with powers vested by Parliament exercise

those powers to the detriment of the public. It does not lie to remedy grievances

arising from an alleged breach of contract.

c) the Statute being superior, reflects the will of Legislators and takes priority over the

private contract.

Conclusion and Order

In view of its entirety, the application amounts to an attempt to challenge the consequences of

a contractual dispute under the guise of a writ application. Accordingly, this Court is of the

view that the reliefs sought by the Petitioner are not amenable to review in the exercise of

jurisdiction under Article 140 of the Constitution.

For the reasons set out above, the Application of the Petitioner is dismissed.

I make no order as to costs.
